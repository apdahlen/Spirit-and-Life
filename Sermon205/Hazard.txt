STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* Persons Anseelse: not merely “respect of persons” in a casual sense, but partiality, favoritism, regard for outward standing. In biblical and doctrinal usage it must retain judicial and theological weight.
* Fortrin: advantages, privileges, prerogatives; in Pauline context, covenantal and religious privilege, not merely personal talents.
* ringe: lowly, humble, socially small; not merely “insignificant.”
* tveægget: two-edged; biblical resonance with the Word as double-edged, bringing either life or judgment.
* Misundheds Træk hos Gud / hans Misundhed: dangerous if flattened into modern “envy.” In older devotional usage this points to God’s jealous, exclusive, discriminating zeal, His refusal to share His glory with human pretension, and His active concern for His own. The term is rhetorically sharp and must be preserved with care.
* retfærdig: in context often “self-righteous” if joined to “rich” or contrasted with the poor sinner; must be judged by context, not automatically rendered “righteous” in a positive sense.
* Forargelse: offense, scandal, stumbling; more than irritation. It often names the spiritual offense taken at God’s way of grace.
* livsalige Ord: life-blessed, blessed words of life, words full of life and salvation. A literal “life-blessed” is awkward in English; the rendering must preserve both vitality and salvific sweetness.
* Bysbarn / Landsmænd: fellow townsman / countrymen, but with communal familiarity that feeds contempt.
* sønderknust Hjerte: broken heart in a spiritual sense, crushed in conscience.
* enfoldigt: simple, plain, guileless; never “naive” in a dismissive modern sense.
* gribende: moving, affecting, piercing.
* opsigtsvækkende: sensational, attention-grabbing; in context something impressive and striking.
* særdig Flid: special effort, exceptional pains taken.
* Letsind: light-mindedness, spiritual frivolity.
* kjødelige Nysgjerrighed: fleshly curiosity, carnal curiosity; a deeply theological expression, not mere inquisitiveness.
* Guds Kraft til Salighed: direct biblical phrase; should remain recognizable as “the power of God for salvation.”
* revsede: rebuked, censured.
* anser Personer: shows partiality, regards persons, takes account of outward persons. Must remain tied to biblical diction.
* forbarmer sig: has mercy, shows mercy.
* føniciske Enke: Phoenician widow; historical precision matters.
* Melkrus / Oljekrukke: jar of meal / jar of oil, or flour jar / oil jug. The domestic concreteness should stay vivid.
* barnlige Tillid: childlike trust, not childishness.
* Trællestand: state of bondage, servitude.
* Høihed og Værdighed: rank and dignity, greatness and status.
* to sig: wash himself.
* redebonde: ready, willing, compliant.
* enfoldige Frelsesvei: simple way of salvation; doctrinally crucial.
* bøiedes: was bent, yielded, was humbled.
* stødte os tilbage: cast us back, drove us away, rejected us.
* Hjertelag: disposition of heart, inward temper.
* Besøgelsestid: time of visitation; strong biblical phrase.
* Livsens Lugt til Liv / Dødens Lugt til Død: must preserve Pauline phrasing.
* Horkar: whoremonger, adulterer, fornicator. The register is morally grave and older; “sexual sinner” would be too weak.
* rettroende Menighed: orthodox congregation, confessionally sound congregation.
* Sjælehyrde: shepherd of souls, pastor; the former preserves devotional force.
* Alters: the Lord’s Table / Holy Communion / the altar. Here “goes regularly to the altar” preserves the older churchly idiom.
* nyt Forsæt: new resolve, fresh purpose.
* kom ihu: remember.
* Blusel: shame, blush of shame, deep disgrace-consciousness.
* forkommen af Hunger: perishing from hunger, ruined by hunger, brought low by hunger.
* arme Synder: poor sinner; must remain spiritually charged.
* lade Salighed vederfares dit Hus: let salvation come upon your house / grant salvation to your house; biblical resonance with Zacchaeus must remain.
* Livsens Brød: Bread of Life.

Syntactic Hazards

* The prose moves in long cumulative sentences with doctrinal buildup. Breaking them into short modern units would flatten the argument and weaken sermonic pressure.
* Semicolons and colons often mark logical pivots, not mere pauses. They should often be preserved.
* Rhetorical questions function as accusatory or probing pastoral turns; they must remain pointed.
* Biblical citations are woven into the prose as active authorities, not decorative quotations.
* Repetition is intentional: “der er ingen Persons Anseelse hos Gud” recurs as refrain, verdict, comfort, and scandal.
* Parallel contrasts drive the sermon: life or judgment, faith or offense, blessing or condemnation, poor sinner or proud righteous person. These need structural clarity.
* Sudden direct address to “Ven” shifts from exposition to personal application; this must remain immediate.
* Historical examples are narrated as analogical proof, not merely illustrative anecdotes.

Theological Hazards

* The core doctrine is justification and salvation apart from privilege, status, ethnicity, or outward religiosity.
* “No partiality with God” must retain its force against covenant pride, churchly self-reliance, and moral self-commendation.
* Faith is presented not as assent alone but as trusting reception of the divine Word.
* Offense at grace is a spiritual crisis: the proud stumble over the simplicity of salvation.
* The widow, Naaman, the publican, the Canaanite woman, and Zacchaeus are not random examples but witnesses to God’s way with the lowly and needy.
* Sin is concrete guilt, spiritual leprosy, hunger, bondage, blindness, and brokenheartedness.
* Christ’s Word is effectual: either salvation or judgment.
* The sermon assumes the Lutheran distinction between outward privilege and inward faith.
* The warning to the orthodox congregation is severe: doctrinal correctness, church attendance, emotional response, and moral restraint do not save apart from repentance and faith.
* “Misundhed” connected with God risks misunderstanding; yet the idea of jealous divine zeal and exclusive mercy should remain alive.

Voice Hazards

* The tone is solemn, admonitory, pastoral, and searching.
* Moral urgency comes from the logic of exposure and consequence, not from emotional exaggeration.
* The preacher addresses the conscience directly and without cushioning.
* The prose alternates between consolation for the broken and severe warning for the complacent; this contrast must not be equalized.
* Familiar biblical stories are retold with homiletic immediacy. They are meant to judge and save the hearer now.
* The diction is elevated but not ornamental. Overly literary English would distort the plain severity.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* “Der er ingen Persons Anseelse hos Gud” is the governing biblical formula. It means God shows no favoritism based on outward standing, privilege, lineage, reputation, or religious status. The natural English equivalent is “There is no partiality with God.” This preserves Pauline and judicial force.
* Mary’s song from Luke 1 is a biblical allusion to the Magnificat. Its reversals of proud and lowly, hungry and rich, form the theological pattern for the whole sermon.
* “Det er tveægget” echoes the scriptural idea of the Word as two-edged. Here the point is not only sharpness but divisive efficacy: it cuts toward either life or judgment.
* “Liv eller Dom, Tro eller Forargelse” condenses a biblical-pietist understanding of the preached Word. “Forargelse” is best rendered “offense” in the spiritual sense, or “stumbling offense” if explanatory force is needed.
* “en arm Synder” is a classic devotional phrase. “Poor sinner” is the natural equivalent and should be retained.
* “en rig retfærdig” likely means one rich in self-righteousness, or at least one who is outwardly advantaged and inwardly secure. The phrase should not be made psychologically vague.
* “Hans egne Bysbørn” expresses the familiarity that breeds contempt. “His own townsmen” or “his fellow townspeople” works better than a flat “people from his city.”
* “efter Sædvane” is biblical phrasing: “as was his custom.”
* “tage de livsalige Ord til Hjertet” means to receive the life-giving Word inwardly, penitently, and in faith. A literal “take to heart” is good, but the salvific tone must be preserved by context.
* “ingen bleven omvendt ved at finde en Prædiken udmærket” attacks aesthetic admiration without repentance. “No one was ever converted by finding a sermon excellent” preserves the rebuke.
* “Professorprædiken” is culturally bound. It means a sermon displaying academic brilliance and learned distinction. “A professor’s sermon” is literal, but the implied criticism should remain apparent through context.
* “kjødelige Nysgjerrighed” is not neutral curiosity; it is fleshly appetite for spectacle. “Carnal curiosity” preserves theological register.
* “det livsalige Ord, der er en Guds Kraft til Salighed” links experiential piety to Romans 1:16. “The blessed Word, which is the power of God unto salvation” or “for salvation” keeps biblical resonance.
* “de ansaa sig fortrinsvis berettigede” means they considered themselves specially entitled. The note of arrogant claim must remain.
* “Han forbarmer sig over hvem han vil og forhærder hvem han vil” is a direct Pauline citation from Romans 9:18 and should closely follow standard English Bible phrasing.
* “kaste alle Bekymringer paa ham” echoes 1 Peter 5:7. “Cast all cares upon Him” is the natural biblical equivalent.
* “vor misundelige Fader” is theologically difficult. The sense is not petty envy but a jealous Father who zealously cares for His own and will not yield His children to false trusts. “Our jealous Father” is likely the best rendering.
* “lagt frem for os sin enfoldige Frelsesvei” means God has set before us the plain way of salvation. The phrase critiques pride that prefers difficult works to simple faith.
* “kun at tro paa Jesu Blod!” distills evangelical simplicity and scandal. It must remain abrupt and sharp.
* “kom krybende til Korset” is vivid penitential imagery. “Came crawling to the Cross” keeps the abasement without explanation.
* “kjendte ikke sin Besøgelsestid” is a strong biblical phrase from Luke 19:44. “Did not know the time of its visitation” is the natural equivalent.
* “en Livsens Lugt til Liv eller en Dødens Lugt til Død” is from 2 Corinthians 2:16. The familiar English form is “an aroma of life unto life” and “an aroma of death unto death,” though “fragrance” is also possible.
* “en from Sjælehyrde” is more than a minister; it is a spiritually faithful shepherd. “A pious shepherd of souls” preserves tone.
* “Toldere og Skjøger gaar ind i Guds Rige før eder” is a gospel humiliation of outwardly respectable hearers; it should remain direct and unsoftened.
* “lade Salighed vederfares dit Hus” echoes Luke 19:9 concerning Zacchaeus. “Bring salvation to your house” or “let salvation come to your house” are natural renderings.
* “hverken skal hungre eller tørste i al Evighed” echoes John 6 and should retain biblical cadence.
* The King James tradition can sometimes supply familiar phrasing for cited texts, but the overall prose should not slip into archaic pronouns or inherited KJV stylization. Use biblical cadence selectively, not wholesale.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

Thesis

* The sermon opens with the governing proposition: “There is no partiality with God.”
* This is presented as a “great truth” upheld by Paul against Jewish claims to salvation by privilege.

Doctrinal Grounding

* The preacher immediately broadens the claim by bringing in Mary’s song, which displays the same divine pattern: the proud scattered, the mighty cast down, the lowly exalted, the hungry filled, the rich sent away empty.
* He then states the governing homiletic principle: this divine attribute, like the Word itself, is two-edged. It yields either life or judgment.

Initial Polarization

* For the poor sinner this truth is supreme comfort.
* For the rich righteous person it is bitter offense.
* Christ’s earthly ministry is then named as proof: the same trait in Jesus saved some and enraged others.

Narrative Entry into the Text

* The Nazareth episode becomes the central case study.
* The preacher recounts what Jesus did: read Isaiah 61, applied it to Himself, and spoke so blessedly that all praised and marveled.

False Positive Exposed

* The hearers seemed favorable, but admiration is not repentance.
* The preacher interrupts with a contemporary anecdote about a learned man whose sermon was judged “quite good” but not impressive enough. This functions as a mirror for superficial hearers.
* Nazareth likewise wanted more than Christ’s saving Word; they wanted signs and special treatment.

Unmasking of the Heart

* Jesus reveals their hidden thoughts.
* Their problems are named: frivolity, carnal curiosity, pride, vanity, and entitlement.
* Their offense is not lack of evidence but resentment that grace is not arranged around their importance.

Scriptural Proof from Israel’s History

* The widow of Zarephath and Naaman are introduced as historical demonstrations that God does not regard persons.
* Each example follows the same logic:
* * humanly despised outsider
* * divine intervention bypassing many in Israel
* * faith receiving the Word
* * abundance of mercy given without regard to status

Interpretive Expansion of Each Example

* The widow example emphasizes trust amid need.
* The Naaman example emphasizes the offense of simple grace and the humbling of pride.
* Both become analogies for present hearers.

Turning Point to Present Application

* What was salvation for the widow, Naaman, and repentant sinners became judgment for Nazareth.
* The preacher then widens the judgment to unbelieving Israel as a whole.

Direct Address and Self-Examination

* The sermon pivots with “And now, friend.”
* The hearer must decide whether this truth has become judgment or salvation.
* The preacher probes both kinds of hearers:
* * the self-assured churchly person with moral and ecclesial privileges
* * the ashamed sinner who has nothing to boast of but sin and shame

Final Contrast

* To the privileged but unbroken hearer: fear, for God shows no partiality.
* To the broken sinner: comfort, for God shows no partiality.

Conclusion

* The sermon ends by calling the hearer back to Nazareth, back to Isaiah 61 as read by Jesus, and back to praise for the truth that with God there is no partiality.
* The refrain returns transformed into gospel consolation for faith.

STEP 4: BIBLICAL ECHO IDENTIFICATION

* Romans 2:11: “There is no partiality with God.” Explicit citation; governs the sermon.
* Luke 1:51–53: Mary’s song (the Magnificat). Explicit citation.
* Luke 4:23–30: the day’s Gospel text. Explicit citation.
* Isaiah 61:1–2: Jesus reads and applies this messianic prophecy. Explicitly paraphrased.
* Romans 1:16: “the power of God unto salvation for everyone who believes.” Strong echo in “en Guds Kraft til Salighed for hver den, som tror.”
* Deuteronomy 10:17: explicit allusion that God is no respecter of persons.
* Romans 9:18: “He has mercy on whom He will, and whom He will He hardens.” Explicit citation.
* 1 Kings 17:8–24: Elijah and the widow of Zarephath. Explicit narrative allusion.
* 2 Kings 5:1–14: Naaman the Syrian healed by Elisha. Explicit narrative allusion.
* 1 Peter 5:7: “casting all your care upon Him.” Echo in “kaste alle Bekymringer paa ham.”
* Luke 18:13: the publican’s prayer, “God be merciful to me, a sinner.” Explicit quotation in adapted form.
* Luke 19:9: Zacchaeus and salvation coming to his house. Explicit allusion.
* John 6:35, possibly also 6:58 and 4:14: Bread of Life, no hunger or thirst forever. Strong echo.
* 2 Corinthians 2:16: “a savor/aroma of life unto life, and of death unto death.” Explicit echo.
* Matthew 21:31: “Tax collectors and prostitutes go into the kingdom of God before you.” Explicit citation/allusion.
* Luke 19:44: “you did not know the time of your visitation.” Echo in “Besøgelsestid.”
* General gospel echoes: the Canaanite woman, the Samaritan leper, the sighing publican, Christ crucified by offended Jews.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* The warning sections must remain sharp. The preacher is not neutrally analyzing Nazareth; he is exposing the same heart in the hearer.
* The consolations must be strong but not sentimental. Comfort comes through the same severe truth that condemns pride.
* The translation must preserve the preacher’s habit of pressing biblical examples directly onto the conscience.
* “Friend” must not sound casual. It is pastoral but searching.
* Exclamations such as “But no” and “O, how blessed” should be retained where they carry homiletic force.
* The repeated refrain must accumulate force each time, not sound repetitive in a weak way.
* Warnings against churchly presumption must remain concrete and socially uncomfortable.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* “skulde tro” should be rendered with its inferential pressure: “One would think” rather than a softer paraphrase.
* “vilde tage” should remain volitional or consequential: “would also take,” not merely “might take.”
* “kunde fri dem” should remain “could deliver them,” preserving real saving power.
* “maatte vente forgjæves” carries necessity joined to disappointment: “had to wait in vain.”
* “vilde handle efter indtil Dagenes Ende” should remain “would act accordingly until the end of days.”
* “skulde to sig syv Gange” is an instruction, not suggestion: “was to wash himself seven times.”
* “havde vi været mere redebonde” should preserve the contrary-to-fact condition: “we would have been more willing.”
* “holdt han ud” should preserve persistence under the Word: “he persevered” or “he kept on.”
* “tør neppe nærme dig” should remain “dare scarcely approach,” not simply “hesitate to come near.”
* “han vil komme ind” and “han vil borttage” and “han vil give” must retain strong promise: “He will come in,” “He will take away,” “He will give.”
* “Saa kom atter” is exhortational imperative: “Then come again.”

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

* Persons Anseelse: partiality
* Forargelse: offense
* Salighed: salvation
* Tro: faith
* omvende sig: repent / be converted according to context
* naadig: merciful / gracious according to prayer context; in Luke 18:13 “be merciful” is right
* retfærdighed / retfærdig in negative contrastive contexts: righteous / self-righteous depending on context, but care is needed
* Synder: sinner
* Guds Ord / Ordet: the Word of God / the Word
* Frelsesvei: way of salvation
* livsalige Ord: blessed words of life / blessed words
* Besøgelsestid: time of visitation
* Misundhed when attributed to God: jealousy / jealous concern / jealous zeal, with context carrying the sense
* Spedalskhed: leprosy
* Sønderknust Hjerte: broken heart
* Livsens Brød: Bread of Life
* Menighed: congregation
* Sjælehyrde: shepherd of souls
* Alters: the altar

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* God’s impartiality is treated as a sword-edge and must remain active, not abstract.
* Spiritual states are imaged as hunger, leprosy, bondage, blindness, brokenheartedness.
* Pride is unveiled, laid open, and exposed; these bodily metaphors of inward disclosure should stay vivid.
* “Came crawling to the Cross” should remain concrete.
* “Bread of Life” and “neither hunger nor thirst” must remain figurative and scriptural, not paraphrased into “spiritual satisfaction.”
* “aroma of life” / “aroma of death” must remain metaphorical.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* Severe doctrinal assertion at the opening.
* Exultant gospel reversal in Mary’s song.
* Stark warning that the same truth brings either life or judgment.
* Narrative admiration in Nazareth, immediately undercut by exposure.
* Sharp satire in the anecdote about the “professor’s sermon.”
* Tender consolation in the widow and Naaman narratives.
* Renewed severity in the application to Nazareth and Israel.
* Most pointed warning in the address to the respectable churchgoer.
* Strongest consolation in the address to the ashamed sinner.
* Final uplift in the summons to hear Christ again and give thanks.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Many sentences should remain long enough to carry preached momentum.
* Refrains and contrasts should be placed where a speaker’s emphasis naturally falls.
* Questions should remain brief and striking: “And why?” “But do you believe this?”
* Biblical quotations should sound declaimable.
* The final exhortation should move with invitational rhythm, not become clipped explanatory prose.
* Repeated vocatives such as “O” and “Friend” should be preserved where they animate the voice.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

With God there is no partiality.

Fourteenth Sunday after Trinity.

—

Luke 4:23–30. And He said to them, You will surely say this proverb to Me: Physician, heal yourself! The great things which we have heard were done in Capernaum, do here also in Your own country! But He said, Truly I say to you, no prophet is well received in his own country. But in truth I say to you: There were many widows in Israel in the days of Elijah, when heaven was shut up for three years and six months, at the time when there was a great famine in the whole land, and to none of them was Elijah sent, except to Zarephath in Sidon, to a widow. And there were many lepers in Israel in the time of the prophet Elisha, and none of them was cleansed, except Naaman the Syrian. And all who were in the synagogue were filled with wrath when they heard this. And they rose up and thrust Him out of the town and led Him to the brow of the hill on which their town was built, in order to throw Him down. But He passed through the midst of them and went away.

“There is no partiality with God” (Rom. 2:11): this is the great truth which Paul declares and maintains when, in opposition to the Jews’ claim upon salvation by virtue of their many advantages, he proclaims that a man is saved by faith alone, without regard to whether he is Jew or Greek.

It is the same thing Mary sings when Elizabeth greeted her as the Mother of God:

“He has shown strength with His arm; He has scattered those who are proud in the thought of their heart; He has cast down the mighty from their thrones and exalted the lowly; the hungry He has filled with good things, and the rich He has sent away empty” (Luke 1:51–53).

It is with this divine attribute as with the Word of God itself: it is two-edged; it always works either life or judgment, faith or offense. This blessed trait of God’s jealousy, that He does not regard persons, is the most blessed comfort of a poor sinner, and the bitterest offense of a rich righteous man.

This same divine attribute in Jesus was what at one and the same time saved the Canaanite woman, the leprous Samaritan, and the sighing publican, and stirred up the Jews’ bitterness, so that at last they hanged Him on the Cross and brought judgment down upon themselves.

In today’s text His own townsmen led Him out to throw Him down from a mountain.

And why?

He had come into their synagogue, had, as was His custom, read from the Word of God and spoken; He had chosen for His text the sixty-first chapter of Isaiah, where the prophet foretells the Messiah who shall come and proclaim “the gospel to the poor, heal those who have a broken heart, proclaim release to the captives, that the blind shall receive their sight, and that the oppressed shall receive their freedom.”

He had expounded these words concerning Himself and had spoken so blessedly that they all gave Him praise and marveled.

One would think that they would then also take the blessed words to heart, repent, and believe that this their townsman was in truth the Messiah, who could deliver them from the bondage of sin and give balm to broken hearts.

But no. No one was ever converted by finding a sermon excellent, still less by marveling that an acquaintance of theirs, whom they may formerly have held in high esteem, could preach so well.

A learned man once preached in a congregation, and spoke simply and movingly of sin and grace. Yes, they said, it was quite a good sermon, but it was no professor’s sermon. They had expected something different, something highly learned and striking, something that showed he had taken special pains for them; one felt disappointed, and there was half a spirit of dissatisfaction in the congregation.

The people of Nazareth also thought, no doubt, that these were blessed words Jesus had spoken to them; but they expected something more, something extraordinary, something specially intended for them, His own countrymen, some sign or wonder or great thing; and when they had to wait in vain, they were disappointed, bitterly disappointed. They forgot His blessed words and began to wonder by what right He could claim to be the one in whom Isaiah’s prophecy had been fulfilled—He?

“Is not this Joseph’s son?” they said.

Then it was Jesus, who knew the innermost thoughts of their hearts, who sharply cut it open and laid it before them. He revealed to them their frivolity and carnal curiosity, in that, over their desire to see wonders, they forgot the blessed Word, which is the power of God unto salvation for everyone who believes. He rebuked them for their proud vanity, according to which they considered themselves especially entitled to demand wonders from Him, their countryman, who had done such great things in Capernaum. And by examples drawn from the history of Israel He showed that the words which the Lord had spoken through Moses (Deut. 10:17), that there is no partiality with Him, He had acted upon and would act upon until the end of days. “He has mercy on whom He will, and whom He will He hardens” (Rom. 9:18).

Did the Lord look to the widow in Zarephath because she was a poor heathen woman, whom chosen Israel thought it had a right to despise? No, the Lord does not regard persons. He passed by the many widows in Israel and sent Elijah to this Phoenician widow, when she and her son were just about to die of hunger. While there was drought and famine everywhere else, even in the king’s house, her jar of meal and her cruse of oil never became empty so long as Elijah dwelt there; and Elijah raised her son again when he died. And why was such an abundance of blessing poured out over this despised woman? Because she believed Elijah’s word and trustfully baked a cake for him from the last meal she had. The Lord did not disappoint her childlike trust; for there is no partiality with Him. O, how blessed it is to cast all cares upon Him in the firm and unshaken faith that He is our jealous Father, who has care for us. But what was blessing for the widow in Zarephath, and salvation for every poor sinner who trustfully believes the Word of God, that was an offense to the inhabitants of Nazareth.

And what do you say of Naaman? He was a Syrian, an enemy of Israel, who even had a captive Jewish girl in his bondage. Nor was there any lack of lepers in Israel at that time; but Naaman the Syrian alone was healed by Elisha. And why? Was he less sinful than other men? O no!

When Elisha would have nothing to do with his gifts, paid no regard to his rank and dignity, but sent word that he was to wash himself seven times in the river Jordan, he became angry and cried out: “Are not Abana and Pharpar, the rivers of Syria, better than all the waters of Israel?” He was exactly as you and I have been when God has knocked upon our hearts and set before us His simple way of salvation. We have often taken offense at its simplicity. Had it been something difficult that God required of us, we would have been more willing. But only to believe in Jesus’ blood!

So it also went with Naaman. But little by little, as he looked upon his misery, his leprous body, and the prophet’s simple word was held before him, even by his servants, his proud heart yielded; he went down into the despised river Jordan, dipped himself under once, twice, three times—in vain; but in faith in the prophet’s word he persevered, and when he came up out of the water the seventh time, he was fresh and clean as a child. The Lord had saved him. He did not look to whether he was Syrian or Israelite, whether he was circumcised or uncircumcised; for there is no partiality with God.

But what was Naaman’s salvation, what was your salvation and mine when we came crawling to the Cross, that God did not, because of our leprosy, because of our many sins, cast us back, that same thing became the offense and judgment of the Nazarenes. When Jesus did not regard their persons and paid no heed to their lofty claims because He had been brought up among them, but on the contrary showed from God’s own Word that it is precisely such proud and unbelieving Pharisees whom God passes by with His jealousy, they became so enraged that they even laid hands on Him to kill Him. His hour had not yet come, and He went away through the midst of them. But they had laid bare the disposition of their hearts, the same disposition which in the blinded people of Israel brought forth the cry, Crucify, and the judgment that scattered them over the whole earth unto this day. They did not know the time of their visitation and took offense because God did not regard their persons and their hypocritical righteousness and the temple and circumcision.

And now, friend, what is this for you: There is no partiality with God? Has it become judgment for you or salvation? Has it become an aroma of life unto life or an aroma of death unto death?

Do you still have something to boast of? If not anything great, then at least something small? The Nazarenes could at least boast only that Jesus had been brought up in their midst. What have you? You have, after all, committed no gross sins; you are no murderer or whoremonger. Perhaps you belong to a congregation, an orthodox congregation—as it is so finely expressed—where there are even many God-fearing people and a pious shepherd of souls. Perhaps you read the Word of God diligently and go regularly to church and to the altar; you believe everything that stands in the Bible, and often under the sermon you have been moved to tears and to a new resolve. All this is good and well; but, friend, I tell you, remember Nazareth and fear! For there is no partiality with God, and tax collectors and harlots go into the kingdom of God before you.

Or perhaps you have nothing to boast of except your sin and your shame; you are leprous as Naaman and perishing from hunger as the widow in Zarephath; you are the chief of sinners and scarcely dare approach the Cross of Jesus, but stand far off and sigh: “God be merciful to me, a poor sinner!”

O, what a blessed message for you, that God does not regard persons; He will come into your wretched heart and let salvation come to your house, as He did with Zacchaeus. He will take away your leprosy and give you the Bread of Life, so that you shall neither hunger nor thirst for all eternity. But do you believe this?

Then come again with Him to Nazareth, listen again to Jesus’ reading and exposition of the sixty-first chapter in Isaiah, keep the blessed words in your heart, and lift up your soul in thanks and praise because “there is no partiality with God.”

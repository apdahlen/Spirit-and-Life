STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

* Lexical Hazards

* "Stad" does not mean merely a modern "place" in a casual sense; here it carries the sense of abiding place, dwelling-place, or treasure-location of the heart. In the sermon title "En Stad i Himelen," it resonates with the passage’s repeated "Liggendes" and with the idea of one’s true store or resting place being in Heaven.

* "Liggendes" is archaic and contextually means laid-up goods, treasure, stored wealth, that which is laid by. It should not be flattened into something vague like "things" or modernized into financial jargon.

* "rent Øie" must be handled carefully. In the biblical context it is not merely physically "clean" but spiritually sound, single, clear, unclouded in aim.

* "frittent Menneske" appears to be an older or irregular form in context meaning a free, liberated, or disentangled person, likely one inwardly freed for a single heavenly aim. It cannot be translated mechanically without regard to argument.

* "forvexles" means confused, diverted, or misled, not simply visually "mixed up."

* "enfoldigt Sind" and "enfoldigt Syn" are doctrinally and rhetorically important. "Enfoldig" in older usage means single, simple in the sense of undivided, sincere, single-eyed, not foolish or simplistic.

* "uimodstaaelige Drift" means irresistible drive or impulse.

* "forkrænkelige" means perishable, corruptible, subject to decay.

* "Stad" in "det naturlige Menneskehertes Stad" must retain the sense of abiding seat, dwelling, or chosen resting-place of desire.

* "Omvendelse" must remain "conversion" or "turning/conversion," not merely improvement or change of outlook.

* "fortvarig" here means fleeting, short-lived, transitory.

* "tilende" means at an end.

* "saligt" and "Salighed" must retain their theological force: blessedness, salvation, beatitude, eternal blessedness, depending on context. "Happy" would be too weak.

* "bedaarer" means stupefies, bewitches, infatuates, deludes.

* "Mammons Træl" must remain "Mammon’s slave" or "slave of Mammon," preserving biblical resonance.

* "Usalighed" means blessedness lost, misery, damnation-like unblessedness; it must not be reduced to ordinary unhappiness.

* "Husvalelse" is consolation, comfort, refreshment, relief; here its negation intensifies eternal deprivation.

* "attræe" means desire, long for.

* "det Under" means the miracle, not merely an event.

* "Hengiven" in context is likely a typographic or orthographic form for "hengivenhed" or a phrase implying humble surrender/committal to Jesus’ cross and blood. The sense is humble self-surrender.

* "forvildede Hjerter" means hearts gone astray, wandering, led astray.

* "friet" means delivered, rescued.

* "Trældommens skammelige Lænker" carries strong moral and spiritual shame-language and must not be softened.

* "faaet smage Guds Godhed og den tilkommende Verdens Kræfter" clearly echoes Hebrews and must retain biblical force.

* "fortrolig med Himmelen" means more familiar, intimate, at home with Heaven.

* "opladte Øie" and "opladte Hjerte" mean opened eye and opened heart.

* "did" means thither, there, to that place; in natural English often "there," but the directional force matters.

* "inderlig Godhed" means inward, heartfelt, tender goodness; not merely politeness.

* "Forthynd" is likely an archaic/print form corresponding to "byd"/"forman"/"charge" in the cited scriptural passage; context clearly indicates an imperative to admonish or charge the rich.

* "nærværende Verden" means this present world.

* "Liggendefæ" again indicates treasure laid up, a stored possession, a laid-up treasury.

* "forundt" means granted, allowed.

* "enfoldig Vei" means simple/direct way, not naïve way.

* "uføgelig Velgjerning" appears to mean singular, uncommon, notable, or exceptionally apt kindness/benefaction; context suggests an unusually fitting kindness in loving the rich without envy.

* Syntactic Hazards

* The prose relies on long cumulative sentences with semicolon pivots and clause-stacking. Breaking them into short modern sentences would weaken the sermonic build and logical pressure.

* Questions are often rhetorical and sequential, meant to examine conscience. Their order and escalation must remain intact.

* Biblical quotation is embedded into the sermon’s own syntax. The translation must distinguish quotation while preserving flow.

* Repetition such as "Derfor" and parallel constructions carry argumentative force; these should be preserved rather than varied away for style.

* Contrastive structures (Earth/Heaven, blindness/sight, temporal/eternal, rich/poor) organize the discourse. Syntax must keep these contrasts crisp.

* The sermon uses explanatory apposition and accumulative clauses to deepen rather than merely repeat a point. Translation must not compress these into a summary.

* Theological Hazards

* The opposition between the natural heart and conversion is doctrinal, not psychological only. "Natural" here means fallen, unregenerate.

* Love of the world is treated as spiritually determinative and damnable; this must not be softened into mere distraction.

* The relation between opened eyes, knowledge of sin and grace, and heavenly desire is conversion theology with Lutheran-pietist coloring.

* "Jesu Kors og Blod" must remain explicit. It names atonement, not merely moral example.

* The movement from seeing one’s sin, to fleeing to Golgotha, to seeing the Father’s heart opened in the Son is doctrinally precise.

* Dying to the world and living unto God must retain Pauline force.

* Heavenly treasure is interpreted through love to immortal neighbors; this must remain ethically concrete without losing eschatological orientation.

* The sermon does not preach salvation by merit; rather, heavenly treasure is spoken of within grace, conversion, and Spirit-wrought love. Translation must not imply moralism.

* Voice Hazards

* The voice is solemn, exhortational, searching, and pastorally severe. Any smoothing into neutral academic English would erase the author’s force.

* The address shifts from exposition to direct admonition: "dersom du vil blive salig" and "Er dine Øine aabnede... ?" These direct confrontations must remain personal.

* The sermonic urgency depends on inevitability and consequence, not emotional excess. The English must preserve that pressure.

* The alternation between warning and consolation is essential. The severe diagnosis of worldliness is matched by rich gospel consolation; both must stand in full strength.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* "Samler eder ikke Liggendes paa Jorden ... men samler eder Liggendes i Himelen"

* Meaning: Do not lay up for yourselves treasures on earth, but lay up treasures in Heaven.

* Scriptural context: Matthew 6:19-20.

* Theological implication: The command concerns the heart’s orientation, not merely external possession.

* Natural English equivalent: "lay not up for yourselves treasures upon earth ... but lay up for yourselves treasures in Heaven." Since the user requested modern pronouns for Scripture quotations, "Do not lay up for yourselves ..." is appropriate, while preserving cadence.

* "Hvor eders Liggendes er, der vil og eders Hjerte være"

* Meaning: The location of treasure reveals and governs the heart.

* Scriptural context: Matthew 6:21.

* Theological implication: Desire follows perceived good; worship and love are disclosed by one’s treasure.

* "Øiet er Legemets Lys"

* Meaning: The eye is the lamp/light of the body.

* Scriptural context: Matthew 6:22-23.

* Theological implication: Spiritual perception determines the condition of the whole person.

* A natural English equivalent is "The eye is the body’s lamp," but because the sermon builds on "light," "The eye is the body’s light" may preserve closer continuity with the older wording.

* "enfoldigt Sind og enfoldigt Syn"

* Meaning: singleness of heart and singleness of sight; undivided inward aim.

* Scriptural/theological implication: A disciple must not have a divided object of desire.

* Literal translation as "simple mind" would mislead modern readers.

* "det naturlige Hjertes ... Drift at elske Verden"

* Meaning: the fallen heart’s irresistible bent is to love the world.

* Scriptural context: resonates with 1 John 2:15-17.

* Theological implication: World-love belongs to the unconverted state.

* "Verden med dens forkrænkelige Ting ... det naturlige Menneskehertes Stad"

* Meaning: the world with its perishable things is the natural human heart’s abiding place/resting-place.

* Theological implication: The unconverted heart settles its home in what must perish.

* Literal "city" for "stad" would be wrong here.

* "lægge nyt Lys i vort Øie og sætte nyt Maal for vort Liv"

* Meaning: to give new spiritual illumination and redirect life’s end.

* Theological implication: conversion changes both perception and goal.

* "fortabes med Verdens Fortabelse"

* Meaning: perish with the world’s destruction.

* Scriptural context: world passing away, 1 John 2:17; broader eschatological judgment language.

* "lade Øiet blændes af Jordens Forfængelighed"

* Meaning: let the eye be dazzled/blinded by earthly vanity.

* Theological implication: vanity is spiritually deceptive, not merely empty.

* "Pengegjerrighed er en Rod til alt ondt"

* Meaning: love of money is a root of all kinds of evil.

* Scriptural context: 1 Timothy 6:10. The older Danish-Norwegian form says "a root to all evil." In English, one may preserve the traditional rhythm while avoiding doctrinal overstatement by using "a root of all evil" if closely mirroring the cited version, or "a root of all kinds of evil" if clarifying. Since the sermon cites the older form, preserving its wording is preferable.

* "gjennemstunget sig selv med mange Smerter"

* Meaning: pierced themselves through with many sorrows.

* Scriptural context: 1 Timothy 6:10.

* King James offers a strong natural equivalent here.

* "Mammons Træl"

* Meaning: slave of Mammon.

* Scriptural context: Matthew 6:24 nearby in context though not quoted.

* Theological implication: service to wealth is spiritual bondage and idolatry.

* "evigt Savn, en uendelig Tomhed, en umættelig Længsel"

* Meaning: eternal deprivation, endless emptiness, unquenchable longing.

* Theological implication: hell is described not only as punishment but as irreversible deprivation of the object wrongly loved.

* "tage Tilflugt til Golgata"

* Meaning: flee for refuge to Golgotha, that is, to Christ crucified.

* Scriptural context: crucifixion site; pietistic shorthand for faith in the atoning death of Christ.

* Literal translation should remain concrete; explanatory paraphrase would weaken it.

* "fra Korset seet op til Faderens Hjerte"

* Meaning: from the cross, seeing the Father’s heart opened in mercy.

* Theological implication: the Father’s love is known in and through the Son’s sacrifice.

* "faaet smage Guds Godhed og den tilkommende Verdens Kræfter"

* Meaning: tasted the goodness of God and the powers of the world to come.

* Scriptural context: Hebrews 6:5.

* A close biblical English equivalent is readily available and should be preserved.

* "ved Kristi Kors er død for Verden og levende for Gud"

* Meaning: through Christ’s cross has died to the world and lives unto God.

* Scriptural context: Pauline theology, especially Galatians 6:14 and Romans 6.

* "samler sig Liggendes"

* Meaning: gathers treasure/store for oneself.

* The sermonic repetition matters and should be rendered consistently as "lays up treasure" or "gathers treasure."

* "Faderhjemmet"

* Meaning: the Father’s home/house.

* Scriptural context: John 14:2 and broader Christian imagery of the heavenly home.

* Theological implication: Heaven is filial belonging, not merely location.

* "binder Menneskehjerters Kjærlighed ved inderlig Godhed"

* Meaning: wins/binds the love of human hearts through heartfelt goodness.

* Theological implication: heavenly treasure is bound up with grace-shaped love that leaves an eternal bond.

* Literal "binds" can be retained because the image is strong and intelligible.

* "samler sig selv et Liggendefæ, en god Grundvold for det tilkommende"

* Meaning: lays up for themselves a treasure/store, a good foundation for what is to come.

* Scriptural context: 1 Timothy 6:19.

* King James-like phrasing is natural here, though modern pronouns should be used.

* "Hjerte trænger Hjerte"

* Meaning: heart needs heart.

* Theological implication: human beings require mutual love across social divisions.

* This compact aphoristic wording should be preserved.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Thesis introduction

* The text speaks of two things: heavenly treasure and a pure eye.

* These two belong together: the liberated person must have Heaven as the goal of all the heart’s longings and efforts, and must look straight toward that goal without earthly distraction.

* This is summarized as singleness of heart and singleness of sight.

* Diagnosis of the natural condition

* The natural heart irresistibly loves the world and its perishable things.

* Different people attach themselves to different earthly goods, but all natural hearts locate their desire in the earthly.

* Because the natural eye sees no farther than earthly treasure, the whole person remains in darkness.

* Christ’s exhortation interpreted

* The command to gather heavenly treasure and direct the heart toward Heaven is a call to total conversion.

* Christ would place new light in the eye and a new goal before life, so that people do not walk in darkness and perish with the world.

* Ground for the warning

* Christ sees the transitoriness of the world and the brevity of human life.

* A heart bound to temporal things cannot be blessed when abruptly thrust into eternity without them.

* Hence Scripture’s continual exhortations to renounce the earthly and seek the heavenly.

* Special warning against riches

* Scripture especially warns against storing earthly treasure and making it life’s goal.

* 1 Timothy 6:9-10 is cited as proof.

* Love of money blinds the eye, deceives the heart, and makes the whole person a slave of Mammon.

* The result is eternal deprivation when the world passes away.

* Direct appeal to the hearer

* If you would be saved/blessed, your heart must be loosed from the world.

* You must learn to love, seek, and desire heavenly and eternal things.

* Means: conversion only

* This change happens only by thorough conversion.

* Conversion is described as miraculous opening of eyes and heart to sin and grace, vanity and heavenly blessedness.

* It involves heartfelt repentance for loving the creature more than the Creator and humble surrender to Jesus’ cross and blood.

* Forgiveness and mercy reopen the eyes to Heaven.

* Conscience-probing examination

* A series of rhetorical questions asks whether this miracle has happened.

* Has the hearer seen not only the world’s pleasures but its perdition?

* Has the hearer fled to Golgotha and there seen the Father’s heart opened in the Son?

* Consolation for the converted

* Blessed are such people: they are delivered from bondage, have found the Father’s heart and the Savior’s love, and have tasted God’s goodness and the powers of the coming world.

* Description of the converted life

* The one dead to the world and alive to God is detached from earth and joined to Heaven.

* Such a person’s daily question is how to pass safely through the dangerous world and become more familiar with Heaven.

* That person now seeks treasure in Heaven.

* Practical question: can heavenly treasure truly be gathered?

* The answer is yes, by God’s wondrous grace.

* Those whose eye and heart are opened to heavenly things have both a way and means of gathering treasure in Heaven.

* Practical exposition of heavenly treasure

* One lives among immortal people.

* Since human hearts endure beyond the earth, winning their love by heartfelt goodness stores treasure in Heaven.

* 1 Timothy 6:17-19 is cited to show that the rich gather such treasure by generosity and good works.

* Inclusion of the poor

* The poor also have a simple way to gather treasure in Heaven.

* If treasure is gathered through love toward people, then the poor have equal room as the rich.

* The poor can do good through patience, kindness, and love.

* Even the rich, surrounded by envy and bitterness, need this love.

* Aphoristic conclusion: heart needs heart.

* Final exhortation

* Turn heart and eye toward Heaven.

* Let the light of God’s love illumine you.

* Then you will learn what it is to have heavenly treasure and to increase it day by day.

STEP 4: BIBLICAL ECHO IDENTIFICATION

* Matthew 6:19-23 is the governing text and is quoted at the outset.
* "The world and its lust" echoes 1 John 2:15-17.
* "Perish with the world’s perdition" also resonates with 1 John 2:17 and broader final-judgment language.
* The warning about becoming rich, falling into temptation and a snare, and piercing oneself with many sorrows is from 1 Timothy 6:9-10.
* "Mammon’s slave" strongly echoes Matthew 6:24.
* Repenting for cleaving to the creature instead of the Creator echoes Romans 1:25.
* Fleeing to Golgotha and seeing the Father’s heart opened in the Son reflects Johannine theology, especially John 3:16 and the Father revealed in the Son.
* "Taste the goodness of God and the powers of the world to come" echoes Hebrews 6:5.
* "Dead to the world and alive to God" echoes Romans 6:11, Galatians 2:19, and Galatians 6:14.
* "Father’s house/home" echoes John 14:2.
* The charge to the rich in this present world is from 1 Timothy 6:17-19.
* The idea that immortal human souls outlast the earth resonates with the broader biblical contrast between temporal creation and eternal destiny.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* Warnings against worldliness must remain severe, not toned down into advice about priorities.
* "cannot become blessed/saved" and "eternal misery/unblessedness" must retain full gravity.
* The conscience-questions must sound searching and urgent, not merely reflective.
* Consolation must remain rich and declarative: "Blessed are you then" should retain pastoral certainty.
* The final exhortation must close with forward-driving encouragement, not vague uplift.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* "skal have" must remain strong: "must have," not "should have."
* "skal se" likewise requires "shall/must look," depending context; here "must look straight at the goal" best preserves demand.
* "kan undgaa" in negative form must retain impossibility or inevitability: "cannot avoid."
* "skulle vandre i Mørke" should retain intended divine purpose/result: "that we should not walk in darkness."
* "maa savne" means "must lack/must be without," not merely "may miss."
* "maa dit Hjerte først løsrives" must remain necessity: "your heart must first be loosed."
* "kan blive seende" is true possibility contingent upon conversion: "can become seeing / can be made to see." Natural English: "can receive sight."
* "kun" repeated throughout must remain emphatic: "only."
* "skulde kunne" in the passage on the poor expresses deprived possibility and should be preserved.
* "du skal lære" in the final sentence is promissory and forceful: "you shall learn" or "you will learn." Here "you shall learn" preserves sermonic tone without archaism overload.

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

* "Himmel" = "Heaven"
* "jordisk / timelig" = "earthly / temporal"
* "naturlige Hjerte / naturlige Menneskehjerte" = "natural heart / natural human heart"
* "Omvendelse" = "conversion"
* "Naade" = "grace"
* "Synd" = "sin"
* "Naade" and "Barmhjertighed" must be distinguished as "grace" and "mercy"
* "Salig / Salighed" = "blessed / blessedness" where possible; in direct salvation contexts, "saved" may be used if needed, but consistency favors "blessed" and "blessedness"
* "Fortabelse" = "perdition" or "destruction" depending context; for strongest doctrinal resonance, "perdition" often fits best
* "Forfængelighed" = "vanity"
* "Kjærlighed" = "love"
* "Godhed" = "goodness"
* "Faderens Hus / Faderhjemmet" = "the Father’s house / the Father’s home"
* "Jesu Kors og Blod / Kristi Kors" = "Jesus’ cross and blood / Christ’s cross"
* "Længsel / Længsler" = "longing / longings"
* "Træl" = "slave"
* "Mørke" = "darkness"
* "Lys" = "light"
* "rent Øie" = "pure eye" or "clear eye"; because the sermon itself repeats "rent," "pure eye" should be maintained.

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* Treasure imagery must remain treasure imagery, not abstract values language.
* Eye/light/darkness must remain concrete and linked to the whole body/person.
* The abyss opening its jaws must remain vivid and not be paraphrased away.
* Chains of bondage must remain chains.
* Earth/Heaven contrast must remain spatially and spiritually concrete.
* The Father’s heart opening itself must remain personal and imagistic.
* Heart-to-heart language in the final pages must remain concrete, since the sermon’s practical theology depends on it.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* Severe diagnosis of worldliness is followed by gracious invitation to conversion.
* Conscience-probing questions intensify into a beatitude-like consolation: "Blessed are you then."
* After doctrinal explanation comes practical encouragement about gathering treasure in Heaven.
* The rich are warned, yet also given a path of obedience.
* The poor are explicitly comforted and included, preventing despair.
* The final tone is exhortational and luminous rather than condemnatory.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Preserve long, breathing sentence-units where argument accumulates.
* Retain anaphoric "Therefore" where it drives sermonic movement.
* Keep rhetorical questions in sequence without interruption.
* Preserve paired phrases and triads such as "an eternal deprivation, an infinite emptiness, an unsatisfied longing."
* Let the ending land as a spoken exhortation with forward momentum.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

A Dwelling-Place in Heaven.

Fifteenth Sunday after Trinity.

Matthew 6:19-23. Do not lay up for yourselves treasures on earth, where moth and rust corrupt, and where thieves break through and steal; but lay up for yourselves treasures in Heaven, where neither moth nor rust corrupts, and where thieves do not break through nor steal. For where your treasure is, there your heart will be also. The eye is the body’s light; therefore, if your eye is pure, your whole body will be full of light; but if your eye is evil, your whole body will be dark. If therefore the light that is in you is darkness, how great is that darkness!

Our text speaks of two things: heavenly treasure and a pure eye; and yet these two are most closely connected. For together both sayings mean this, that a liberated man must have the goal of all his heart’s longings and strivings in Heaven, and that he must always look straight toward the goal without letting his sight be confused by any earthly thing. In other words: it is singleness of mind and singleness of sight that the Savior requires of His disciples.

It is the irresistible bent of the natural heart to love the world, or the things that are in the world. In one way or another, in one form or another, the world with its perishable things is the abiding place of the natural human heart. For some, it is money and possessions that draw the heart to themselves; for others, power; for others, honor; for others, pleasure; for others, earthly comfort and well-being; but no natural heart can avoid having the goal of its longings in earthly and perishable things. For the natural eye sees no farther than earthly treasures, and when the eye is dark and blind, then the whole man also remains in darkness and blindness.

Therefore, when the Lord exhorts us to gather heavenly treasures and direct the heart’s longings toward Heaven, it is an exhortation to a whole and complete conversion from the natural mind and its longings and strivings. He would place new light in our eye and set a new goal for our life, that we should not walk in darkness and perish with the world’s perdition.

The Savior sees how fleeting the world and its lust are; He sees how short men’s stay upon earth is, and how soon their life is at an end; He knows that the heart which clings to temporal and earthly things cannot become blessed when it loses them and is suddenly torn out into eternity and there must lack everything that was the heart’s delight and joy. Therefore He exhorts so earnestly to a thorough conversion from a worldly mind, and warns so heartily against letting the eye be dazzled by the vanity of earth. Therefore the whole Holy Scripture is full of exhortations to let go of the earthly and direct eye, mind, and heart toward the heavenly.

In a most special way Scripture warns against gathering treasures on earth and making these the goal of life. Therefore we read: "Those who will be rich fall into temptation and a snare, and into many foolish and hurtful desires, which sink men into destruction and perdition; for the love of money is a root of all evil; because some longed after it, they wandered from the faith and have pierced themselves through with many sorrows" (1 Tim. 6:9, 10).

It blinds the eye and stupefies and deceives the heart when a man sees no farther than the world and its lust. The eye grows dark and the mind earthly; and the whole man becomes a slave of Mammon, who with all his toil prepares for himself only an eternal misery; for his happiness was in the earthly and temporal, and when the world passes away with its lust, then there remains only an eternal deprivation, an infinite emptiness, an unsatisfied longing; and the heart suffers for ever without relief, without consolation.

Therefore, if you would become blessed, your heart must first be loosed from the world and your eye must look higher up and farther on than the vanity of the world. You must learn to love, seek, and desire the heavenly and eternal things, if your heart is to find blessedness in God’s Heaven.

How can this come to pass? It happens only by a thorough conversion. That is the only way on which a blind man of the world can receive sight; it happens only by the miracle that opens eye and heart to sin and grace, to the vanity of the world and the blessedness of Heaven. It happens only by a heartfelt repentance that my heart has fastened itself to the creature instead of the Creator, and by a humble surrender to Jesus’ cross and blood. Only the forgiveness of our sins and God’s mercy toward our poor, wandering hearts can again open our eyes to Heaven’s glory and place in our souls a longing for our true home in the Father’s house.

Have your eyes been opened in this way? Has this incomprehensible miracle taken place in you? You, who before saw only the world and its lust, have you also seen the world’s perdition? Have you cast an eye down into the abyss that opens its jaws to swallow those who so carelessly walk on along the world’s way and run so eagerly after its seductive joys? Have you, terrified and broken over your sin, taken refuge at Golgotha and from the cross looked up to the Father’s heart, which opens itself to all who come to His Son? Blessed are you then; for you have been delivered from the shameful chains of bondage with which vanity had bound your immortal soul. Blessed are you then; for you have found the Father’s heart and the Savior’s love, and you have been given to taste the goodness of God and the powers of the world to come.

But he who through Christ’s cross has died to the world and is alive to God is loosed from earth and joined to Heaven; his eye has become pure and his heart heavenly. He sees rightly, and the longings of his heart are heavenly. For him it is a daily question how he may come safely through the perilous world, how he may become more and more familiar with Heaven and its joys. It is no longer on earth, but in Heaven, that he seeks his treasure and lays up store for himself.

Is it then truly possible to gather treasures in Heaven? Is there any way by which we here in time may live for eternity, labor for eternity, lay up a treasure in eternity? Yes, by God’s wondrous grace. Those who have the opened eye and the opened heart for the incorruptible and heavenly things, those who long thither, where the Father’s home is opened for them, they also have a way and a means by which they can gather treasures in Heaven.

You live in the midst of immortal men. You dwell upon an earth that shall perish; but the human hearts that beat so near your own, they shall continue to live when the earth is no more. And if it is a glory on earth to possess the love of a heart, then it is not less, but greater, glory to possess the love of a heart in Heaven’s incorruptibility. Therefore the art of gathering treasures in Heaven consists in this, that you bind the love of human hearts by heartfelt goodness. Hear what Scripture says concerning the rich: "Charge those who are rich in this present world that they be not high-minded, nor trust in uncertain riches, but in the living God, who gives us richly all things to enjoy; that they do good, become rich in good works, gladly give, distribute, thus gathering for themselves a treasure, a good foundation for what is to come, that they may lay hold on eternal life."

But now the poor man in this world? Is it not granted to him to gather treasures in Heaven? It would indeed be hard if he for whom earthly life is so heavy and straitened should not also be able to have this joy, to gather treasures in Heaven. God be praised, there is a way just as simple for the poor as for the rich. If we gather treasures in Heaven by our love toward men, then there is room for the poor no less than for the rich. The rich need the poor man’s heart no less than the poor need the rich man’s heart. The rich can do good with their possessions; the poor can do good with their goodness, their patience, their kindness, their love. The rich are so surrounded by envy and hatred and ill will and bitterness and faithlessness, that it is a singular kindness toward them when a poor man loves them and thanks them, rejoices in their prosperity, and sees in it also a wise leading of God and a gracious gift. Heart needs heart, and it is just as easy for a poor man to gladden a human heart as it is for a rich man.

Turn, then, heart and eye toward Heaven; let the light of God’s love illumine you, and you shall learn what it is to have a heavenly treasure and to increase it from day to day.

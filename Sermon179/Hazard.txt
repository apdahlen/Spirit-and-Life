STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* Gjæstebud – literally "feast" or "banquet," but carries biblical and parabolic resonance tied to kingdom imagery.
* Frænder – archaic for relatives or kinsmen.
* Vederlag – recompense, reward, repayment; carries moral and eschatological nuance.
* Løndom – secrecy, hiddenness; biblical phrase "your Father who sees in secret."
* urette Mammon – biblical phrase referring to worldly wealth (Luke 16).
* Livsanskuelse – worldview; but in devotional context implies moral-religious orientation toward life.
* Levedis – manner of living or life-practice; archaic nuance.
* Naadestunder – "hours of grace" meaning opportunities of salvation.
* forkrænkelige – perishable/corruptible.
* Husholdningsvisdom – stewardship wisdom; economy of divine household.
* Bankforretning – metaphorical financial transaction used spiritually.
* enfoldigt – simple, single-hearted, guileless.
* trostholdigt – trustfully, confidently.
* Løsepenge – ransom.
* Opofrelse – self-sacrifice.
* nødlidende – suffering, needy.
* fortrykt – oppressed or burdened soul.

Syntactic Hazards

* Long sermon sentences with accumulative reasoning and semicolon logic.
* Parallel moral contrasts: world vs eternity, temporal vs eternal reward.
* Rhetorical questions intended to expose false reasoning.
* Sequential exhortation clauses building moral pressure.

Theological Hazards

* Distinction between works and salvation by Christ’s blood.
* Almsgiving framed as fruit of faith rather than purchase of salvation.
* Eternal reward language connected to resurrection of the righteous.
* "Unrighteous Mammon" teaching from Luke 16.
* Treasure in heaven imagery (Matthew 6).
* Faith producing love toward neighbor.

Voice Hazards

* Strong prophetic rebuke of worldly living.
* Pastoral exhortation mixed with eschatological warning.
* Sermonic rhetorical rhythm that must not be flattened.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* "gjøre sig Venner ved den urette Mammon" – biblical idiom from Luke 16:9 meaning using worldly wealth to serve others in ways that have eternal significance.
* "Møl og Rust" – Matthew 6:19 imagery referring to corruptible earthly treasure.
* "Tyvehaand" – thieves stealing; idiom from treasure-in-heaven passages.
* "et Haarsbred fra den evige Pine" – hair’s breadth from eternal torment.
* "Herren har seet i Løndom" – Matthew 6 teaching about the Father who sees in secret.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

Thesis: Jesus’ teaching about inviting the poor reveals the true spiritual meaning of earthly goods.

Movement of argument:

* Narrative setting: Jesus rebuking both guests and host.
* Diagnosis of worldly religion seeking immediate reward.
* Warning against social feasting that ignores eternity.
* Presentation of the alternative: feasts for the poor.
* Clarification: charity does not purchase salvation.
* Theological center: salvation through Christ’s blood alone.
* Fruit of faith: sacrificial love toward neighbor.
* Practical application: true heavenly stewardship.
* Concluding exhortation: learn heavenly wisdom with earthly goods.

STEP 4: BIBLICAL ECHO IDENTIFICATION

* Luke 14:12–15 (main text)
* Luke 16:9 – unrighteous Mammon
* Matthew 6:19–20 – treasure in heaven
* 1 Corinthians 13 – giving goods without love
* Matthew 25 – feeding the hungry, clothing the naked

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* Maintain sharp warning tone toward worldly pleasure.
* Preserve direct moral pressure on reader.
* Maintain solemn references to judgment and eternity.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* Imperatives such as "Gjør Gjæstebud for de fattige!"
* Moral necessity conveyed through sermonic instruction.

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

* Salighed → salvation
* Tro → faith
* Kjærlighed → love
* Evighed → eternity
* Herlighed → glory

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* Banking metaphor for charity and heavenly reward.
* Feast imagery for kingdom participation.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* Contrast between worldly pleasure and eternal seriousness.
* Warning followed by consolation.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Preserve sermon cadence with extended clauses.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION

THE TRUE BANQUET

Seventh Sunday after Trinity

Luke 14:12–15. And he said also to the one who had invited him: When you make a dinner or a supper, do not invite your friends, nor your brothers, nor your kinsmen, nor rich neighbors, lest they also invite you again and you receive repayment. But when you make a feast, invite the poor, the crippled, the lame, the blind. Then you shall be blessed; for they have nothing with which to repay you, but it shall be repaid to you at the resurrection of the righteous. But when one of those who sat at table with him heard this, he said to him: Blessed is the one who eats bread in the kingdom of God.

According to this and the preceding narrative Jesus is a remarkable guest. He was with a Pharisee and sat at table in his house. And he seized the occasion to speak weighty and serious words both to the guests and to the host. To the guests he spoke about sitting in the lowest place and humbling themselves. To the host he speaks about whom he ought to invite to a feast.

There lies sharp sting and piercing seriousness in both of these table discourses. For the guests were likely among those who sought honor from men, but not the honor which is from the only God. And the host, who was a Pharisee, probably sought more the recompense for his works which he could receive immediately, than the recompense which is in secret from the heavenly Father and which is first revealed on the day of Jesus Christ in the resurrection of the righteous.

The host invited friends and brothers and kinsmen and rich neighbors who could give a feast in return; but he had no thought of making for himself such friends by means of the unrighteous Mammon as could receive him into the eternal dwellings.

Therefore it is not merely the meaning of Jesus Christ to give rules for what kinds of gatherings are fitting or not fitting in the congregation of God; but it is his meaning to chastise all that religion and morality, the whole view of life and the whole manner of living, which strives after earthly and temporal and perishable advantages and distinctions, but forgets the eternal responsibility and the eternal and incorruptible glory.

Large or small gatherings which are held, so to speak, in turn from one to another, in which a brief temporal enjoyment is all that is sought and given, are not according to the mind of Jesus. He always and continually has eternity before his eyes. And when he sees how people through brief pleasures and empty diversions squander precious hours of grace and thoughtlessly draw near to death and eternity, then he has compassion on such misery, because he sees how they move along the edge of the abyss and are only a hair’s breadth from eternal torment.

He sees in this a disposition that will not do; therefore he warns against that kind of gatherings which have no value for eternity, and he recommends an entirely different kind of gatherings, which are wholly ordered according to the demands of eternal responsibility.

To feed the rich and let the hungry and poor go away empty-handed—that is the mind of the world and the wisdom of the world. It has its reward in the world and its pleasantness in the present time. But it is a dreadful folly for the one whose eye is directed toward the eternal; for in the judgment it counts for nothing that a man has enjoyed life together with his friends, when he has neglected to clothe the naked, feed the hungry, and visit the sick and the sorrowful.

Make feasts for the poor! Help the helpless! This is Jesus’ counsel to those who wish to hold the true banquet. To take temporal means and transform them into eternal values, into a heavenly treasure which moth and rust cannot destroy and which the hand of thieves cannot seize—this is the heavenly wisdom of stewardship and the true spiritual banking.

And this happens quite simply and confidently by having the hand of the poor as one’s bank and boldly venturing one’s means there; for the one who does so for God’s sake has stored both capital and interest with the Lord, and it shall be repaid to him in the resurrection of the righteous.

How does this happen, or how does it hang together? If it could be explained to human reason, and if it were taken in a fleshly way, then it would only become a new Phariseeism which thinks to purchase God’s salvation by alms. That is not what Jesus means.

It is not a transaction or a clever speculation. It is something infinitely different: it is the boldness of faith and the sacrifice of love which Jesus means. For even if someone gives all his goods to the poor but has not love, he cannot with all his goods—even if they were millions upon millions—purchase for himself a single moment of salvation with God.

There are many worldly people who, when dying, have given away all their goods to the poor, not out of love for their fellow men but out of fear of the torments of hell; does God sell salvation for such a price? Oh no, no!

There is only one ransom which delivers from death and hell and which obtains salvation: it is the blood of Jesus, poured out for poor sinners. But only the one in whom Christ’s love has become a new life, so that he loves his neighbor and therefore helps him in his need—only he has the disposition which finds salvation with God in God’s love.

Faith in God and love toward the brothers are required in order to hold the true banquet for the poor, the crippled, the lame, and the blind. There is required the faith which continually sees the vanity of earthly things and believes in the eternal and incorruptible and heavenly things.

There is required the faith which turns its back on the world and fixes its gaze upon the heavenly kingdom and upon the misery that is here below. When the heart is thus loosened from earthly things because it grows together with the heavenly, then there is room for the right kind of banquet, then the divine art of household stewardship is learned.

For it consists in sharing the perishable things of the world with suffering brothers, because it is human hearts that are gladdened thereby. To prepare for a suffering fellow human being a joyful hour, a true gladness of heart, to relieve distress, to remove bitterness and grief from an oppressed soul, to wipe the tears of one who weeps in the love of Jesus Christ—this is what is pleasing to God.

By this a person gathers for himself an incorruptible treasure in heaven. Joyful and radiant shall those who have thus been helped receive their helper and friend into the eternal dwellings, if they themselves have attained salvation with God.

And if someone whom you have helped in sacrificial love has not attained salvation, do not fear for the reward; for the Lord has seen in secret and shall repay you openly.

Thus the wondrous table discourse of Jesus in the Pharisee’s house is a blessed guidance for those who truly with sincere hearts wish to deal rightly with the things of the earth.

Blessed is the one who boldly ventures to keep house according to Jesus’ instruction. He shall lack nothing on earth, nor in heaven.

But woe to the one who is so careful with earthly things that he does not dare to put them at interest with God.

So long we think about our old age and about our family until we often forget to think about the eternity that lies before us. So carefully we think about securing for ourselves a carefree livelihood on earth that we often forget the eternal torment and the hunger that is never satisfied and the thirst that is never quenched.

Oh that the Lord might teach us the true heavenly wisdom in the use of our earthly goods, so that we might one day sit at table in the kingdom of God.

For blessed is the one who for all eternity shall enjoy God’s glory and be satisfied with the vision of his face.

STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* Salig / Ufalig: "Salig" here is not merely "happy," but blessed in the deepest eschatological and theological sense. "Ufalig" is not common modern Norwegian; in context it means unsaved, unblessed, spiritually ruined, under woe. The title therefore requires a rendering that preserves both beatitude and doom.
* Alle Helgens Dag: All Saints’ Day, with liturgical and devotional weight.
* Selvprøvelse: self-examination in a spiritual and penitential sense, not mere introspection.
* Sønderknuselse: brokenness, contrition, being crushed inwardly before God; stronger than ordinary sorrow.
* Husvalelse: consolation, refreshment, inward solace; old devotional diction.
* Salighedsvilkaar: conditions or terms of blessedness; doctrinally weighty.
* forkrænkelig / Forkrænkelighed: perishable, corruptible, subject to decay. Strong biblical resonance with corruption/incorruption.
* Retter: dishes, fare, provisions; in context the nourishing fare of the Father’s house.
* Vederkvægelse: refreshment, reviving consolation, spiritual restoration.
* forstøder / forstødt: cast out, reject, thrust away.
* Bespotter: revile, reproach, speak abusively against.
* tilsidesat: set aside, passed over, rejected.
* gjennemskuet: seen through, exposed for what it is.
* delagtig: made partaker of, given participation in.
* besværede: burdened, heavy-laden.
* tomhændet: empty-handed, with strong evangelical force.
* Skibbrud: shipwreck; figurative but vivid and essential.
* Halmstraa: straw, straw-like last support; image of false hope.
* Naadesolen: the sun of grace; metaphor must remain vivid.
* Rum / give Rum: room, space, welcome, inward capacity to receive.
* overbevise sig om Synd: be convicted of sin.
* uforfyndt: freely, without payment, without merit.
* erfare: experience, come to know by experience.

Syntactic Hazards

* The prose depends on long, cumulative sentences with semicolon logic and sermon-like progression. Breaking these into short modern units would flatten the argument and weaken the exhortation.
* Repetition is deliberate: "salig, salig," "borte, borte," "intet, intet," "Forsent!" The force lies in recurrence, not economy.
* The author moves from doctrinal assertion to direct address without warning; this shift must remain immediate and unsoftened.
* Chains of parallel clauses build spiritual pressure. These must be preserved, especially in warnings and appeals.
* The beatitude text from Luke governs the whole meditation; the exposition repeatedly echoes its cadence.

Theological Hazards

* Poverty, hunger, weeping, and rejection are spiritual categories as expounded here, though not stripped of lived suffering. The interpretation is inward, penitential, and Christological.
* The contrast between World and Kingdom is absolute and moral, not merely psychological.
* Grace is wholly gift: Christ asks nothing to be brought, but gives all to the empty-handed. Any wording that suggests merit would distort the passage.
* Conviction of sin, loss of worldly comfort, and inward sorrow are presented as preparatory and necessary in the way of salvation.
* Salvation and perdition are treated with full eternal consequence: Heaven, Hell, grace, judgment, death, eternal joy, eternal loss.
* Jesus is not merely teacher or example, but the only way by whom one becomes partaker of heavenly goods.

Voice Hazards

* The tone is solemn, earnest, exhortational, and pastorally severe; modernization would make it sound therapeutic rather than theological.
* The preacher addresses conscience directly and presses inevitable consequence rather than emotional display.
* Severity and consolation are sharply contrasted; neither may be diluted.
* The rhetoric has devotional heat without sentimentality. The English must preserve gravity without inflated archaism.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* "Salige er I fattige" and the following beatitudes echo Luke 6 directly. "Blessed are you poor" is the natural English biblical equivalent.
* "Springer af Fryd": literally "leap for joy." This is a direct biblical expression and should remain so.
* "Møl og Rust ikke kan fortære": clear echo of Matthew 6:19-20. "Moth and rust do not consume" or "corrupt" preserves biblical resonance.
* "Stene istedetfor Brød": echo of Matthew 7:9. The world’s food proves to be stones instead of bread.
* "Svinemad" and "Faderhusets hjemlige Retter": strong allusion to the prodigal son in Luke 15. The soul is starving on swine’s food until fed with the fare of the Father’s house.
* "Verdens Bedrøvelse, som virker Døden": close echo of 2 Corinthians 7:10. A natural English equivalent is "the sorrow of the world, which works death."
* "Guds Ords Lys": biblical-pietistic phrase for revelation by Scripture.
* "trætte og besværede": clear echo of Matthew 11:28, naturally rendered "weary and heavy-laden" or "weary and burdened." Since the translation aims to preserve gravity without archaic pronouns, "weary and burdened" or "weary and heavy-laden" are both viable; "weary and heavy-laden" carries stronger scriptural resonance.
* "slippe det Halmstraa" in shipwreck: image of relinquishing false worldly hope in order to grasp Christ’s saving hand.
* "Naadesolen": not a fixed English idiom, but "the sun of grace" preserves the metaphor and devotional intensity.
* "Livsens Vand uforfyndt": clear echo of Revelation 22:17 and Isaiah 55:1. Best rendered "the water of life freely."
* "intet Øie har seet og intet Øre har hørt": 1 Corinthians 2:9. English should echo familiar biblical cadence: "what no eye has seen and no ear has heard."
* "den Herre Jesus Kristus": solemn confessional naming; should be retained as "the Lord Jesus Christ."

Expressions where literal translation would obscure force

* "ufalig" should not be translated by a flat or psychological term such as "unhappy." The force is spiritually ruined or under woe. In the title, "Blessed and Unblessed" preserves structural contrast, though "Blessed and Under Woe" is possible. "Blessed and Unblessed" best fits the sermonic pairing.
* "Huus’s Goder" literally "the house’s goods" is better as "the good things of the Lord’s house" to preserve devotional sense.
* "gjøre hans Navn til Haan og Spot" should be rendered idiomatically as making his name a byword of mockery or an object of scorn and derision.
* "daarer os med sine Tillokkelser" is not merely "charms us" but deceives, beguiles, seduces us with its allurements.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Opening thesis: Jesus’ sermon is severe and consoling at once; it brings both self-examination and comfort.
* First doctrinal contrast: Christ names as blessed the opposite of what the world calls happiness, and pronounces woe on those whom the world counts fortunate.
* First major claim: To become truly happy, one must lose all. Poverty is the condition in which the Kingdom is received as pure gift.
* Supporting explanation: All earthly possessions are perishable; blessedness must therefore consist in the imperishable heavenly goods.
* Second major claim: This loss is accompanied by suffering.
* Development 1: Hunger signifies the soul’s torment when worldly satisfactions prove false and only God can satisfy.
* Development 2: Weeping signifies inward sorrow, not worldly grief, but grief over estrangement from God and the soul’s deception by the world.
* Development 3: Rejection signifies conflict with the world, which hates the soul that has seen through its emptiness.
* Christological pivot: Yet blessed is the one who turns from the world and finds Jesus, for He Himself is the only way to heavenly and eternal goods.
* Evangelical invitation: Christ invites the weary and heavy-laden, asks nothing to be brought, and gives all to the empty-handed.
* Practical call: Let go of the last worldly support in the shipwreck and grasp Christ’s outstretched saving hand.
* Counter-thesis: Unblessed is the one who is rich, full, merry, and praised in the world.
* Warning: The more the world fills the heart, the more dreadful the final loss when death strips all away.
* Eschatological climax: The terror of awakening too late to one’s self-deception, when only the hated God and feared Hell remain.
* Urgent appeal: Hasten before the last beam of the sun of grace disappears.
* Closing universal application: The Kingdom is for the poor, hungry, weeping, and rejected; all may become such through conviction of sin and renunciation of the world.
* Final promise: Grace is for all who need grace; blessedness for all who will know their unblessedness.
* Concluding lyrical prayer: a devotional stanza sealing the sermon emotionally and spiritually.

STEP 4: BIBLICAL ECHO IDENTIFICATION

* Luke 6:20-26: explicit sermon text and governing framework.
* Matthew 6:19-20: treasure that moth and rust do not consume.
* Matthew 7:9 and Luke 11:11: stone instead of bread.
* Luke 15: the prodigal son, swine’s food, the Father’s house.
* 2 Corinthians 7:10: the sorrow of the world works death.
* Matthew 11:28: the weary and heavy-laden invited to find rest in Christ.
* Revelation 22:17 and Isaiah 55:1: the water of life freely.
* 1 Corinthians 2:9: what no eye has seen and no ear has heard.
* Johannine and general New Testament soteriology: Christ as the sole way to life, the giver of heavenly bread, joy, rest, and salvation.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* Warnings must remain sharp: the world deceives, seduces, and leads toward perdition.
* Consolations must remain rich but not sentimental: Christ receives the empty-handed and gives all.
* Repeated exclamations and direct imperatives must be preserved where present: "O let go," "Hasten," "Too late!"
* The preacher’s movement from exposition to address must remain immediate and searching.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* "Det maa til" must be rendered with full necessity: "It must be so" or "This is necessary." Here "It must be so" best preserves sermonic force.
* "skulle mættes," "skulle le," "skulle hungre": future certainty, not mere possibility.
* "kan ikke" and similar forms must remain impossibility, not difficulty.
* "skal slippe" in evangelical invitation is not a suggestion but a real demanded relinquishment.

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

* Salig = blessed
* Ufalig = unblessed (with the understood force of spiritually miserable / under woe)
* Guds Rige = the Kingdom of God
* Verden = the world
* Salighed = blessedness
* Naade = grace
* Synd = sin
* Trøst / Husvalelse / Vederkvægelse = comfort / consolation / refreshment, according to context
* Forkrænkelighed = perishability / corruption
* Frelse / frelst = salvation / saved
* Fortabelse = perdition
* Himmelglæde = heavenly joy
* Livsens Vand = the water of life

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* The world’s food as stones, swine’s food, hollow laughter, false riches: these images must remain concrete.
* Shipwreck, straw, and the Savior’s outstretched hand form a unified salvation image and must not be paraphrased away.
* The sun of grace vanishing into death’s and Hell’s night must remain pictorial and severe.
* God’s fountain full of water and the water of life freely must remain vivid, not explained away.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* Severe warning against the world contrasts with tender invitation from Christ.
* Contrition, grief, rejection, and struggle contrast with refreshment, rest, heavenly joy, and blessedness.
* Eschatological terror contrasts with evangelical promise.
* The final hymn stanza shifts into intimate prayer; this tonal narrowing must be retained.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Sentences should often remain extended where the original accumulates force.
* Repetition should be audible, not trimmed.
* Direct address to "you" should sound proclaimed, not conversational.
* Clause rhythm should carry the sermon forward with measured weight.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

Blessed and Unblessed.

All Saints’ Day.

Luke 6:20-26. And He lifted up His eyes upon His disciples and said: Blessed are you poor, for yours is the Kingdom of God. Blessed are you who now hunger, for you shall be filled. Blessed are you who now weep, for you shall laugh. Blessed are you when men hate you, and when they cast you out and revile you and cast out your name as evil for the Son of Man’s sake. Rejoice in that day, and leap for joy! For behold, your reward is great in Heaven. In the same way their fathers did to the prophets. But woe to you rich! For you have already received your consolation. Woe to you who are full! For you shall hunger. Woe to you who now laugh! For you shall mourn and weep. Woe to you when all men speak well of you! For in the same way their fathers did to the false prophets.

Jesus’ sermon is altogether serious and severe, and yet blessed and full of comfort. These are words that can bring us to earnest self-examination, and that can lay us low in deep contrition; they are also words that can bring us sweet consolation on our pilgrim-wandering here below.

He sets as the condition of blessedness the opposite of what the world counts happiness; and He cries woe over those who possess happiness according to the judgment of the world.

The first thing needed in order to become happy is to lose everything. For poor is the one who has nothing. But to the one who has nothing, the Kingdom of God is given for nothing. And blessed is the one who receives the Kingdom of God. Everything that a human being has in the world is perishable; blessedness therefore is not to be found in it, for blessedness is eternal happiness, happiness that is never lost, a treasure which moth and rust cannot consume, which perishability cannot touch. Blessed therefore is the one who lets go of all earthly and perishable things and receives instead the eternal, heavenly, and imperishable things.

But this does not take place without suffering. As hunger is tormenting and painful, so the heart’s demand for blessedness is a dreadful torment so long as it has not been satisfied by God. If the world’s food has shown itself to be stones instead of bread, swine’s food instead of human nourishment, then the soul suffers unspeakably until it is filled with the familiar fare of the Father’s house. It will not do to be full of the things of the world, if you would enjoy the good things of the Lord’s house; they are for the hungry, who are no longer satisfied by the world.

It does not go without tears; for the mind that is content and glad in the bondage of perishability is shut up against heavenly joy. There is certainly also a worldly weeping, which is just as bad as the world’s laughter, a sorrow of the world, which works death. It is not that which Jesus praises. He means the soul’s weeping, which springs from its longing after God and His fellowship, when nothing in the world can give refreshment and comfort, the sorrow of the heart when the whole world has shown itself to be a disappointment and a deceit to the soul, the sorrow of the heart over this, that the happiness of life has been lost through sin—it is that sorrow which prepares the way for heavenly comfort.

Nor does it go without conflict. For when the world perceives that a soul no longer allows itself to be deceived by its false riches, by its swine’s food, by its merry and hollow laughter, then all its bitterness awakes. It feels itself rejected and set aside; it feels itself like a deceiver who has been caught in his deceit, like a hypocrite from whose face the mask has been torn away, and it rages in wrath because someone has dared to proclaim what it and its goods are, because someone has seen through its hollowness and emptiness and turned to other and better things and begun to ask after the Kingdom of God and the blessedness of Heaven. The one who rejects the world is rejected by the world. In contempt the world will turn its back upon him and make his name an object of mockery and scorn. In malice it will present him as a hypocrite, because its own falsehood has been laid bare.

But blessed, blessed is the one who, hungering and thirsting after truth, weeping and sighing after joy, has turned from the world and has found Jesus. For it must be so. There is no other way of becoming partaker of the heavenly, invisible, and eternal goods than the way which is Jesus Himself. He is the One who has come down from Heaven to bring heavenly life and joy and blessedness and refreshment to the poor, the hungry, the weeping, and the rejected. He has come and graciously invites all the weary and heavy-laden to find rest with Him.

And He shuts no one out; for He does not require that you bring Him anything. He demands nothing from you; He will give you everything, only come empty-handed, so that you may receive His gifts. But if hand and heart and mouth are full of the world and its joy and its food, how then can He give you heavenly joy and heavenly bread? He sets no condition for your salvation except this one alone, that in your shipwreck in the world you must let go of the straw which you still clutch in your hand, and take hold of His outstretched Savior’s hand. O let go your hold upon the world’s perishability, and then let yourself sink down into the faithful arms of mercy, and you are saved. Blessed, blessed! Leap for joy, for you lost that which drew your heart down into perdition, and you gained the Friend who alone is mighty to bear you into Heaven’s eternal joy. Then you gained the happiness which is hidden from the world, but revealed to those who love God.

But unblessed is the one who is rich and full and glad in the world and praised by men. For the more the world fills and satisfies the heart, the more worldly happiness smiles upon us and beguiles us with its allurements, the more we delight in the world and the world delights in us, the more dreadful will be the day on which it all vanishes. What then remains for the worldly mind and heart when death comes, when the world fades, when everything, yes, all of it, is gone, gone forever? When there is only the God whom you hate, and the Hell which you fear, and nothing, nothing else to all eternity? How dreadful to have one’s eyes opened to the long self-deception when it is too late! Too late! O hasten, before the last beam of the sun of grace vanishes into the night of death and Hell; hasten to let go of the enchanting, ravishing, seductive world, which deceives you out of your happiness and blessedness, and lay hold on the eternal life which is in the Lord Jesus Christ and in no other.

For the poor, the hungry, the weeping, and the rejected belong the Kingdom of God and the blessedness of God. It has room for all human hearts without exception, if only they will make room for the Kingdom of God. There is no one who cannot become poor, if he will let the Spirit convict him of sin. There is no one who cannot become hungry, if he will hold himself back from the world’s swine’s food. There is no heart in which there cannot arise tears and sorrow and lamentation, if it will see its perdition in the light of God’s Word. Grace for all who need grace; blessedness for all who will know their unblessedness. God’s fountain is full of water, if only you would come and drink and take the water of life freely. Then you would experience how blessed that is which no eye has seen and no ear has heard, that which God has prepared for those who love Him.

Deliver me from the world!
Hide me, ah, hide me in You!
The joy in the world
Is indeed no joy for me.

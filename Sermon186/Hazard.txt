STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* Trofast / Trofasthed: more than mere reliability; includes fidelity, stewardship-faithfulness, covenantal loyalty in service to a master.
* Husholdere: stewards, not merely household managers; carries biblical resonance from parables and apostolic stewardship language.
* den urette Mammon: the unrighteous mammon / unjust mammon; biblical phrase that should not be flattened into merely “money.”
* det fremmede: that which belongs to another, what is another’s, not simply “the foreign.”
* eie: own, possess as one’s own.
* pengebegjærlige / Pengegjerrighed: love of money / avarice / greed; doctrinally weightier than simple thrift.
* Vederstyggelighed: abomination; strong biblical-moral term, not just something offensive.
* trænger derind med Magt: presses into it / forces his way into it; difficult because of exegetical ambiguity, but the forceful movement must remain.
* timelige / aandelige: temporal / spiritual; “earthly” may fit contextually in places, but the contrast is doctrinal, not merely material versus immaterial.
* Mammontjener / Mammonsdyrkelse / Mammons Trælle: servant of Mammon / worship of Mammon / slaves of Mammon; must preserve personified lordship.
* Slave-sind: slavish mind, slave spirit, servile disposition; must not lose moral contempt in the term.
* sprænge Baandene: burst the bonds; vivid emancipation imagery.
* gudelige: outwardly pious / devout; context warns against false godliness.
* aandeligt Snaf: literally spiritual “sniffings” / scraps / trifles; contemptuous term for shallow spiritual fussiness without power. Needs idiomatic English preserving disdain.
* Sjælstilstande: states of soul, soul-conditions; spiritual condition, not psychological mood.
* løsrives / lørevne: be loosed from / torn loose from; liberation from the world’s hold.
* udvortes: outward, external; common pietistic contrast with the heart.
* ærbart: respectable, decorous, outwardly honorable.
* forsagede: renounced.
* Trælbinderens Børn: literally the slave-driver’s children / children of the taskmaster. Likely figurative for those bred under legal bondage. Must preserve harshness.
* Naaden: grace, specifically freeing grace.
* fornægter de sig: deny themselves; ascetic renunciation.
* Skin / Gudsfrygtighedens Skin: appearance / semblance / show of godliness.
* Last: vice; stronger and more moral-theological than “fault.”
* Hengivelse: devotion, surrender, attachment.
* fryder sig ved Synet: delights in the sight.
* forhaaner: mocks, insults, holds in contempt; stronger than “dishonors.”
* Ve: woe; prophetic denunciation.
* Trællen bliver ikke i Huset evindelig: “The slave does not remain in the house forever”; explicit biblical echo must remain recognizable.
* utro Husholder: unjust / unfaithful steward, echoing Luke 16. Context requires “unfaithful” or “false to Mammon,” not simply immoral.
* Omhyggelighed: carefulness, diligence, solicitude.
* Kristensjæl: Christian soul; direct devotional address.
* nøisomt: frugally, sparingly, simply.
* meddele: give, share, distribute to those in need; biblical diaconal term.
* det mindste / det store: the least / the great, echoing the Gospel lection; should remain formulaically stable.
* Miskundhed: mercy.
* befatte os med: concern ourselves with, involve ourselves in.
* Gavmildhed: liberality, generosity.
* indbildte Trofast: imagined faithfulness / supposed faithfulness.
* salig: blessed.

Syntactic Hazards

* Long accumulative sentences carry sermonic momentum and moral pressure; they should not be broken into short contemporary units unless absolutely necessary for intelligibility.
* Repeated antitheses structure the thought: small/great, temporal/spiritual, God/Mammon, faithful/unfaithful, outward/inward, slave/son.
* Semicolon logic is central. Clauses often intensify or expose the previous clause rather than merely add information.
* Repetition of formulae (“tro mod Herren,” “utro mod Mammon,” “tro i det mindste,” “tro i det store”) must remain conspicuous.
* Rhetorical questions and apostrophe (“Hvorledes skal jeg da være tro, siger du...”; “o Kristensjæl”) are essential features of oral exhortation.
* Embedded biblical quotations interrupt and authorize the sermon’s flow; they must remain distinct yet integrated.
* Exclamatory denunciations and laments (“Ve...”; “Gud forbarme sig over os!”; “O...”) are structurally significant and must not be toned down.

Theological Hazards

* The distinction between outward renunciation and inward freedom by grace is a central Lutheran-pietistic concern; it must remain clear.
* Serving God and Mammon is treated not as a mild inconsistency but as spiritual slavery and hypocrisy.
* The relation of Law, wrath, grace, and freedom is precise: some are driven by Law and wrath into outward renunciation, yet remain inwardly bound because grace has not set them free.
* “Faithfulness” is not legalistic scrupulosity but love-governed stewardship under the Lord.
* Mercy over sacrifice alludes to the heart of divine economy; this must not be reduced to mere philanthropy.
* Use of money is a spiritual test revealing the heart’s true lord.
* The final contrast between slave and son concerns abiding in the house and eternal destiny, not merely social position.
* Faith, hope, and love are presented as the “great” sphere corresponding to faithfulness in “the least”; the connection must remain organic.

Voice Hazards

* The tone is solemn, admonitory, urgent, pastoral, and polemical. Any smoothing into neutral academic English would erase the author’s force.
* The preacher addresses both community and individual conscience directly; this must remain pointed.
* Irony against false piety and avarice is sharp and should not be dulled.
* The prose uses moral inevitability rather than emotional ornament; the English should preserve this pressure.
* The language alternates between warning, diagnosis, and evangelical direction toward Christ’s cross; these tonal shifts must remain distinct.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* “Den urette Mammon” is the biblical idiom from Luke 16. It means wealth belonging to the fallen order, wealth marked by injustice and moral danger. “Unrighteous Mammon” preserves the scriptural register best.
* “Tjene to Herrer” is a direct biblical expression meaning divided ultimate allegiance, not merely overcommitment. It requires master-slave force.
* “Med ægte Slavesind og ubrødelig Trofast træller under Mammons Aag” combines servile mentality with yoke imagery. This is not simple hard work; it is inward bondage under a false lord.
* “Aandeligt Snaf uden Kraft og Sandhed” is a contemptuous pietistic phrase for a mass of spiritual talk, nibblings, devotional fuss, or scraps without reality. A rendering like “a mass of spiritual trifling without power and truth” preserves contempt and emptiness.
* “Løsrives fra Verden med hele sit Hjerte” means inward detachment from the world, not physical withdrawal. Theologically it concerns the heart’s allegiance.
* “Tage igjen i Pengegjerrighed, hvad de forsagede i andre Retninger” means compensating in greed for other renunciations; asceticism in one area is offset by covetousness in another.
* “Trælbinderens Børn” evokes the offspring or kind of the taskmaster, probably alluding to souls formed under legal bondage. The expression carries experiential-theological force more than a fixed idiomatic English equivalent.
* “Frihed i Naaden” means liberation effected by grace, likely with Pauline resonance. It should not become merely emotional relief.
* “Gudsfrygtighedens Skin” recalls biblical language akin to “a form of godliness.” The idea is an appearance devoid of truth.
* “Hjertets Hengivelse til Verden” means the heart’s surrender to the world; inward devotion is the issue.
* “Han tjener med Munden og ærer med Læberne” clearly echoes biblical language about lip-service. Natural English should preserve that scriptural recognition.
* “Trællen bliver ikke i Huset evindelig, men Sønnen bliver i Huset til evig Tid” echoes John 8:35. A natural English equivalent close to common Bible phrasing helps preserve the resonance.
* “Mammon siger...” personifies Mammon as a catechist preaching prudential anxiety. This is a dramatic sermonic personification and should remain vivid.
* “Kom med til Jesu Kors” is invitational but not sentimental. It directs the hearer to Christ crucified as the school of true love and freedom.
* “Det narrer Mammon grundigt” means thoroughly outwits or deceives Mammon. The irony is that industriousness and thrift can be anti-Mammon when governed by charity.
* “For at faa nok til at meddele til dem, som har Behov” uses biblical language of sharing with those in need; compare apostolic admonitions on labor for generosity.
* “Herren har Behag i Miskundhed og ikke i Offer” echoes Hosea 6:6 and Gospel usage. “Mercy” and “sacrifice” should remain exact.
* “Smuler tilovers for Lazarus” evokes the rich man and Lazarus narrative and the image of crumbs. It should remain concrete.
* “Langt fremme i Bønnemøder og Forsamlinger” means prominent, advanced, conspicuous in prayer meetings and gatherings, not spiritually mature in truth.

King James or familiar biblical equivalents that are natural in English

* “Unrighteous Mammon”
* “No servant can serve two masters” / “You cannot serve God and Mammon”
* “justify yourselves before men”
* “an abomination before God”
* “the Law and the Prophets”
* “The slave does not remain in the house forever; the son remains forever”
* “Well done, good and faithful servant... enter into the joy of your lord”
* “mercy and not sacrifice”

Expressions where literalism would obscure force

* “aandeligt Snaf” should not be rendered literally as “spiritual sniffing”; it needs an idiomatic equivalent preserving contempt.
* “Trælbinderens Børn” may need contextual expansion such as “the children of the taskmaster” rather than a flat literalism that becomes opaque.
* “tjene Penge” literally “serve/earn money” here means make money, but with an ethical overtone of worldly gain.
* “skyr en daarlig Anledning til at bortødsle Pengene” is not merely avoiding bad spending opportunities; it means shunning sinful occasions for waste.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Thesis: True Christianity includes true faithfulness because believers are God’s servants and stewards, accountable to Him in everything.
* First development: Faithfulness must be toward the Lord, not toward Mammon. Mere fidelity in worldly accumulation is false faithfulness.
* Second development: A common religious error is to try to serve Mammon in temporal things and the Lord in spiritual things. This collapses into hypocrisy and idolatry.
* Example: The Pharisees embody this error. Their outward decency concealed an inward bondage to money.
* Contemporary application: The same pattern appears among those frightened by Law and wrath into external renunciation, but not inwardly freed by grace.
* Intensification: Avarice is the most dangerous vice because it looks respectable, prudent, and even ascetic while actually binding the heart to the world.
* Doctrinal inference: Whoever combines godliness with greed necessarily despises God while professing to serve Him. One cannot serve two masters.
* Warning: This double slavery leads to miserable life and eternal ruin; the slave does not abide in the house forever.
* Pivot: Therefore one must become unfaithful to Mammon in earthly matters in order to be faithful to the Lord.
* Objection raised and answered: Does this condemn thrift and diligence? No. Waste and laziness are also fleshly. The issue is whether one’s economy is governed by love learned at Christ’s cross.
* Positive account: Grace frees from God’s wrath and from the world’s bondage, enabling true faithfulness in all things, temporal and heavenly alike.
* Practical criterion: Labor, thrift, and prudence become true unfaithfulness to Mammon when they are exercised so one may have enough to share with those in need.
* Conclusion by test: Faithfulness in the least means using earthly goods in love and for God’s kingdom; whoever fails here does not understand the greater divine stewardship either.
* Final warning against spiritual pride: People imagine themselves faithful in “great” spiritual matters while neglecting charity, mission, and household faithfulness.
* Final consolation: Blessed is the one faithful in the least; the Lord will acknowledge such a servant in the end.

STEP 4: BIBLICAL ECHO IDENTIFICATION

* Luke 16:10-17: opening lection and controlling framework throughout.
* Luke 16:13: “No servant can serve two masters... You cannot serve God and Mammon.”
* Luke 16:14-15: Pharisees as lovers of money; “justify yourselves before men... God knows your hearts... what is exalted among men is an abomination before God.”
* Luke 16:16-17: “The Law and the Prophets were until John... everyone presses into it... not one tittle of the Law falls.” Present in lection, though not central to the sermon’s development.
* Luke 18:11: “not like other men, extortioners, unjust, adulterers...” adapted in the description of Pharisaic self-righteousness.
* John 8:35: “The slave does not remain in the house forever; the son remains forever.” Quoted with explicit exhortational use.
* Matthew 15:8 / Mark 7:6 / Isaiah 29:13: serving with the mouth and honoring with the lips while the heart is far away.
* Ephesians 4:28: laboring so as to have something to share with anyone in need; strongly echoed in the positive account of work and thrift.
* Matthew 25:40: “As you did it to one of the least of these... you did it to me.” Quoted nearly directly.
* Hosea 6:6 / Matthew 9:13 / 12:7: “I desire mercy, and not sacrifice.”
* Luke 16:19-31: Lazarus and crumbs; invoked in “crumbs left over for Lazarus.”
* Matthew 25:21 / 23: “Well done, good and faithful servant... you have been faithful over a little... enter into the joy of your lord.”
* Possible resonance: 1 Corinthians 4:1-2 on servants of Christ and stewards required to be found faithful.
* Possible resonance: Romans/Galatians themes of bondage under Law versus freedom in grace.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* Warnings against greed must remain severe, not moderated into generic caution about materialism.
* Rebukes of hypocritical godliness must remain direct and uncomfortable.
* The address to the hearer’s conscience (“you must...”) must be preserved.
* The lamenting exclamations (“Woe...”; “God have mercy on us!”; “O...”) should remain strong and unembarrassed.
* The turn toward Christ’s cross must retain evangelical warmth without becoming soft or sentimental.
* The final blessing must sound earned through the preceding discipline of exhortation and promise.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* “maa” should usually be “must,” not “should,” where necessity is doctrinal or absolute.
* “kan ei / gaar ikke an” should remain “cannot / it is not possible,” not merely “it does not work very well.”
* “vil” in prophetic or logical consequence should often remain “will.”
* “skulde” in normative statements should often be “ought to be” or “was to be,” depending on context.
* “kan” where ability is enabled by grace should remain “can,” preserving real possibility grounded in divine action.

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

* Trofast / Trofasthed = faithful / faithfulness
* tro mod Herren = faithful to the Lord
* utro mod Mammon = unfaithful to Mammon / false to Mammon
* Mammon = Mammon

* Gudsfrygt / Gudsfrygtighed = fear of God / godliness depending on context

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* Yoke and bonds of Mammon must remain concrete.
* Slavery/master imagery must remain explicit throughout.
* Mammon’s “school” and Mammon’s speech are personifying metaphors that should remain vivid.
* The heart “hanging upon” the world should remain imagistic, not paraphrased into abstract preference.
* “Crumbs for Lazarus” should remain concrete and narrative.
* “House” in the slave/son saying should remain house, not household membership in abstract terms.
* Faithfulness in the least/great should remain as stewardship scale imagery rather than generalized maturity.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* Early doctrinal exposition moves into sharp denunciation of false piety.
* This severity intensifies in the treatment of avarice and double slavery.
* A pastoral turn occurs with the question about thrift and diligence; the preacher answers without relaxing moral rigor.
* At Christ’s cross the tone becomes invitational and evangelical.
* The closing then returns to admonition against spiritual pride before ending in eschatological consolation.
* These transitions should remain clear and not be blended into one uniform tone.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Maintain periodic sentences where thought accumulates by balanced clauses.
* Keep repeated formulae audible.
* Preserve semicolon turns where the second clause exposes or deepens the first.
* Let direct address and exclamation stand as spoken exhortation.
* Avoid clipped modern prose; the translation should feel speakable from a pulpit without theatrical archaism.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

Faithful in small things and in great.

Ninth Sunday after Trinity.

Luke 16:10-17. He who is faithful in what is least is faithful also in what is great, and he who is unrighteous in what is least is unrighteous also in what is great. If then you have not been faithful in the unrighteous Mammon, who will entrust to you the true? And if you have not been faithful in what belongs to another, who will give you what is your own? No servant can serve two masters; for either he will hate the one and love the other, or else he will hold to the one and despise the other; you cannot serve God and Mammon. But the Pharisees also heard all this, and they were lovers of money, and they mocked him. And he said to them: You are those who justify yourselves before men, but God knows your hearts; for what is exalted among men is an abomination before God. The Law and the Prophets were until John; from that time the kingdom of God is preached by the Gospel, and everyone presses into it with force. But it is easier for heaven and earth to pass away than for one tittle of the Law to fall.

To true Christianity belongs a true faithfulness, since we are God’s servants and God’s stewards. We owe the Lord an account for all things; therefore there is required faithfulness in small things and faithfulness in great, faithfulness in temporal things and faithfulness in spiritual things.

But all our faithfulness must be faithfulness toward the Lord. It is of no use to be a faithful servant of Mammon, who with a true slave’s spirit and unbroken faithfulness toils under Mammon’s yoke all the days of his life, without ever lifting a finger to burst the bonds. If anyone would be a Christian, his faithfulness must be a faithfulness toward the Lord, both in temporal things and in spiritual things. It will not do to act as so many truly pious people do, to serve Mammon in temporal things and the Lord in spiritual things; in the end it becomes nothing but worship of Mammon, all of it, with a hypocritical show of the fear of God and a mass of spiritual trifling without power and truth.

These are states of soul more dangerous even than that in which a person tries to serve the Lord in the spiritual and yet remains in the service of Mammon in the earthly. That was the Pharisees’ misfortune. They would serve the Lord without in truth being loosed from the world with their whole heart; then their life became outwardly decorous and righteous, and so they were “not like other men, robbers, unjust, adulterers;” but then in greed for money they took back again what they had renounced in other directions.

So it goes in our days as well with the children of the taskmaster, with those who, frightened by the Law and by wrath, are driven to an outward renunciation of the world and its pleasure, without their hearts having won freedom in grace. Hard and strict, bitter and without love, they deny themselves all outward joys in the world; but greed for money keeps their hearts bound in the slavery of Mammon; and while they would serve God in the spiritual, and yet are not torn loose from the world in the earthly, in reality they become only Mammon’s slaves, despite all the show of godliness.

There is therefore no more dangerous vice than greed for money. It is so decent and respectable; it is so sparing and sensible; it looks like the most perfect renunciation of the world, it looks like an unbroken faithfulness in the management of earthly goods, but in reality it is a complete surrender of the heart to the world and to all its lusts; for the miser’s heart secretly enjoys everything that his money can buy for him, without his laying out any of his precious treasure. He delights in the sight of the money, because he knows what it could buy; but he keeps the money, because he knows that once he has spent it, he can enjoy it no longer.

In that way, the one who would be faithful to two masters is compelled to love the one and despise the other. For he who joins the fear of God with greed for money, he mocks and despises the God whom he serves with his mouth and honors with his lips, while his heart hangs upon the world and Mammon. Woe to them under this double slavery; God’s slave and the world’s slave at one and the same time; frightened by the Law into slaving under God, bound by the world into slaving under Mammon in the dreadful vice of greed for money. And yet there are frightfully many people whose case is exactly this. O that they would remember that “the slave does not remain in the house forever, but the Son remains in the house forever!” A tormented and miserable life in bitter anguish is led by such a slave, who would serve both God and the world, and in the end it finishes in eternal torment; for “the slave does not remain in the house forever.”

No, it is not possible to serve two masters. It is not possible to be faithful to Mammon in earthly things and to be faithful to the Lord in spiritual things. You must be unfaithful to Mammon and break his yoke and give notice to his service in earthly things, if you would be a servant of the Lord in true faithfulness. You must become an unfaithful steward toward Mammon, if you are to become a faithful steward for the Lord.

How difficult this is! And yet it must be learned in Mammon’s school. Mammon says: Be sparing, be careful, be diligent, seize every opportunity to make money, flee and shun every opportunity to spend it; remember that you may soon become ill, think that in some years you will become old and frail, therefore save and scrape together as much as ever you can. It sounds so reasonable; it rings so sensibly; it looks like faithfulness; but alas, it is faithfulness toward Mammon and unfaithfulness toward God; whereas your position, O Christian soul, ought to be faithfulness toward God and unfaithfulness toward Mammon.

How then shall I be faithful, you say, if thrift and diligence are not faithfulness? Is it the meaning that we should be wasteful, lazy, slack, indifferent, negligent? Oh no, you know far better than that. That too is only a new way of slaving under your fleshly lusts. It becomes no faithfulness toward the Lord. But come with me to Jesus’ cross and learn there first of all what it is to love, to love God, to love human beings; then you shall become able to understand what it is to be unfaithful to Mammon and faithful to God. If grace makes you free, free from God’s wrath, free from the slavery of the world, then you can become faithful in everything toward the Lord, faithful in the earthly, faithful in the heavenly, faithful in small things and faithful in great. For faithfulness consists in loving and using everything in the service of love.

It thoroughly deceives Mammon, and is the true unfaithfulness toward him, when a child of God is both industrious and thrifty, both frugal and prudent, both uses a righteous opportunity to make money and shuns a bad opportunity to squander money, in order to have enough to share with those who are in need. In this lies that which is unfaithful toward Mammon, but faithful toward the Lord; for he says: “As much as you have done to these little ones, you have done to me.”

If anyone would be faithful in what is least, faithful toward the Lord and unfaithful toward Mammon, he uses his earthly means in the service of love, in the service of the kingdom of God. If he is truly faithful in this, then he is also faithful in what is great, in faith, in hope, in love. But he who does not understand how to do good and to share, neither does he understand the great divine stewardship, whose chief point is this, that the Lord has delight in mercy and not in sacrifice. It is of no use to say: Yes, I know God’s love well enough, but I have nothing left over except crumbs for Lazarus and for my suffering fellow human beings and for the spread of the kingdom of God among Jews and Gentiles. That is not faithfulness toward the Lord; it is faithfulness toward Mammon.

O that we could become a little more faithful in what is least! We so quickly imagine that we are so faithful in what is great that we have no need to be faithful in what is least. We think we see so deeply into God’s revelation and into the doctrine of God’s Word that we need not concern ourselves with charity and mission and all such things as require sacrifice and liberality. We serve the Lord in what is great, and then it can be all the same with what is least. We are far advanced in prayer meetings and assemblies, we are so faithful in this that it may be all the same at home in the house. God have mercy on us! If we have become too great to be faithful in what is least, then the Lord does not care for our imagined faithfulness in what is great. The Lord will say to us as to the Pharisees: “You are those who justify yourselves before men, but God knows your hearts; for what is exalted among men is an abomination before God.”

But blessed is he who is faithful in what is least; for in his own time the Lord shall say to him: “Well, good and faithful servant, you have been faithful over little, I will set you over much; enter into the joy of your Lord!”

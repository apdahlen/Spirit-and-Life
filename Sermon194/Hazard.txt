STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* Selvophøielse / Selvfornedrelse: Not merely psychological “self-esteem” versus “self-abasement,” but morally and spiritually charged terms denoting sinful self-exaltation and voluntary humbling of self before God.
* Farisæersindet: More than “Pharisaic mindset” in a modern casual sense; it denotes the inward spiritual disposition of the Pharisee, especially self-righteousness.
* den nøgne Sandhed: “the naked truth,” with forceful moral directness; should not be weakened into “plain truth” where the sharper exposure is intended.
* Vederstyggelighed: Strong biblical-theological term, best rendered “abomination,” not merely “offense” or “something detestable.”
* hæderligt / Hæderlighed: “honorable” / “honorableness,” but in context bound up with social respectability and outward decency.
* retskaffen / Retskaffenhed: “upright” / “uprightness,” with ethical seriousness; should not be flattened into “decent” or “good.”
* billigt: In older usage, “fair,” “reasonable,” “just,” not “cheap.”
* bentfrem: “straightforwardly,” “directly,” “plainly”; preserves the sense of worldly frankness.
* falske Skin: “false appearance” / “false show”; linked to biblical condemnations of hypocrisy.
* hoffærdige / Hoffærdighed: Archaic religious term for pride, haughtiness, arrogance. “Pride” is usually the best English equivalent, but the elevated moral register must remain.
* Gudfrygtighed: “godliness,” “fear of God,” or “piety” depending on context. Here it is not mere religiosity but professed godliness.
* Forsmædeligheds Sag: Literally something like a matter of shame/disgrace/contumely; here likely meaning that their godliness has become a matter of pride and mutual contempt or reproach. This requires careful handling so that the rhetoric is preserved.
* bukker under: “succumb,” “bend beneath,” not merely “struggle with.”
* tilkommer: “belongs to,” “is due to.”
* Forstandere: “leaders,” “overseers,” “rulers,” depending on ecclesial context. “Leaders” is safest unless a stronger church-office nuance is needed.
* agte paa Ordet: “heed the Word,” not merely “listen to the word.”
* Personen være en Sag for sig selv: keep the person distinct from the matter itself; do not make personal attachment or aversion decisive.
* Kald: “calling” or “office,” depending on emphasis; here both vocational and sacred-office senses are present.
* Herrens Hjord: “the Lord’s flock,” a biblical pastoral image that must remain.
* Herskesyge: lust to rule, domineering spirit.
* Storagtighed: self-importance, pretension to greatness.
* Ugreie: conflict, trouble, estrangement.
* Ubodfærdighed: impenitence, unrepentance. “Impenitence” best preserves theological precision.
* Gjenstridighed: stubborn resistance, contumacy, obstinacy.
* saare trang Vei: very narrow way; likely echoes Matthew 7:14 and must retain biblical force.
* glimrende Gaver: brilliant gifts, shining talents; must not sound merely complimentary.
* Slughed: cunning, craftiness, shrewdness in a negative sense.
* Pavedømmets Mørke: “the darkness of Popery” is historically exact but polemically loaded; “the darkness of the papacy” preserves confessional sharpness without slang.
* Udfarelses Aand: difficult phrase. Likely refers to the Spirit of sonship proceeding forth in filial relation, or the filial Spirit by which one goes forth/has access to the Father. This must be rendered carefully without doctrinal distortion.
* Erkjendelse: recognition, knowledge, realization, acknowledgment; often more spiritually weighty than mere cognition.
* fri uforskyldt Kjærlighed: free unmerited love; doctrinally important, must preserve gratuity.
* de kristnes Frihed: Christian liberty/freedom in a theological, not political, sense.
* Kjød og Blod: biblical “flesh and blood,” not merely human nature in an abstract way.
* Persons Anseelse: respect of persons / partiality; biblical judicial and moral language.
* forbitredes ikke: “was not embittered,” not merely “was not upset.”
* et Rov: from Philippians 2:6. Must be handled in line with biblical source; likely “a thing to be grasped” in modern translations, but the sermon’s wording should be respected while preserving sense.
* Guds Lam: “the Lamb of God,” fixed biblical title.
* slaa sig for sit Bryst: “strike his breast,” an unmistakable echo of the publican in Luke 18.
* hovmode sig: exalt oneself proudly, be puffed up.

Syntactic Hazards

* The sermon uses long cumulative sentences with repeated clauses and semicolon-like logical turns. These must not be broken into overly short modern English sentences.
* Many paragraphs advance by rhetorical questions followed by an authoritative correction. This pattern must remain intact.
* The discourse moves between exposition of Scripture, diagnosis of ecclesial corruption, admonition, and Christological example without explicit modern transitions; these pivots must be preserved.
* Repetition is structurally meaningful: “Der er kun en...” / “ingen...” / “der er ingen...”. These repetitions build sermonic force and should remain repetitive in English.
* The text often uses paired formulations: “agte Guds Ord høit og sig selv ringe, at sætte Herren høit og sig selv lavt.” These parallelisms must be maintained.
* The quoted Gospel text has older biblical syntax. The translation of the sermon should distinguish between scriptural citation and authorial prose while preserving continuity.
* Embedded scriptural quotations are woven into the author’s own prose without explicit citation markers beyond quotation marks. These must be recognized and handled carefully.
* Conditional constructions accumulate theological consequence: “Skal vi derfor ikke..., saa er det nødvendigt...” This logic must remain explicit.

Theological Hazards

* The central contrast between self-exaltation and self-humbling is not merely moral but eschatological: God opposes the proud and exalts the humble.
* Pharisaic self-righteousness must remain a theological category, not a generic critique of hypocrisy alone.
* The distinction between outward honor before men and abomination before God is crucial.
* The critique of clergy and congregations concerns misuse of the Word, the ministry, and ecclesial authority; this must retain confessional and pastoral seriousness.
* The work of the Holy Spirit is decisive throughout. Humility, true confession of Christ, right relation to the Father, discipleship, brotherhood, and love all depend upon the Spirit.
* “Of grace, of mercy, of free unmerited love” is a doctrinal sequence emphasizing salvation by grace alone.
* Christian equality and brotherhood are grounded not in social theory but in common reception of grace.
* Christian freedom is defined as willing service, especially of the greater to the lesser; this is a theological paradox that must remain paradoxical.
* “Flesh and blood” signifies fallen human incapacity to understand divine wisdom.
* Christ is presented both as doctrinal model and redemptive Savior: the one who did not seek his own honor, bore the world’s sin, and humbled himself unto death.
* The sermon concludes with universal indictment of sin and the need for mercy, echoing the tax collector’s prayer.

Voice Hazards

* The tone is solemn, searching, admonitory, and pastorally severe. It must not be softened into reflective religious prose.
* The author frequently implicates both preacher and hearer; this mutual exposure must remain direct.
* Anti-clerical and anti-congregational critiques are balanced; neither side should be muted.
* The Lutheran-pietistic earnestness depends on moral pressure through consequence, not emotional ornament.
* Exclamations such as “O, hvor vanskeligt...” and “O, hvilket Exempel...” must retain gravity without becoming sentimental.
* Rhetorical questions are not exploratory; they are instruments of conviction.
* The prose is proclamation-oriented. Cadence, buildup, and repeated contrasts are part of the voice and should remain audible.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* “Jeg takker dig, Gud, at jeg ikke er som andre Mennesker” is the Pharisee’s prayer from Luke 18:11. It introduces the inward principle of self-righteous comparison.
* “Hvad som er høit blandt Mennesker, er en Vederstyggelighed for Gud” echoes Luke 16:15. “Abomination before God” is the natural biblical equivalent and should remain strong.
* “Paa Moses’ Stol sidder...” refers to Matthew 23:2, the seat of Moses, denoting teaching authority within Israel.
* “Tankeremmer” refers to phylacteries, from Matthew 23:5. A literal rendering such as “frontlets” may obscure meaning for modern readers; “phylacteries” is accurate but specialized. In a voice-preserving translation, “phylacteries” is acceptable.
* “Kvastene store paa sine Klæder” refers to the enlarged fringes/tassels of garments, also Matthew 23:5.
* “Rabbi, Rabbi” marks appetite for spiritual distinction and public honor.
* “Mose Stol” becomes in the sermon a figure for ecclesial teaching office; this extension must remain visible.
* “agte paa Ordet” is a pietistic-church expression meaning to heed, attend to, submit to, or take seriously the Word of God.
* “lade Personen være en Sag for sig selv” means not allowing personal relations to determine one’s obedience to God’s Word.
* “Herrens Hjord” is a biblical pastoral metaphor. “The Lord’s flock” is the natural English equivalent.
* “den Helligaands Vei” is not a technique or program but the Spirit’s way, that is, the Spirit’s operation and leading.
* “ingen kan kalde Jesus Herre uden ved den Helligaand” echoes 1 Corinthians 12:3.
* “den sønlige Udfarelses Aand” appears to refer to the filial Spirit by whom believers cry to God as Father, echoing Romans 8:15 and Galatians 4:6. The precise wording is unusual; the sense is that only by the Spirit of adoption/sonship can God truly be called Father.
* “gjennem Kors til Herlighed” condenses the Christian pattern of following Christ through the cross to glory; compare Luke 24:26, Romans 8:17, and broader New Testament theology.
* “Af Naade, af Barmhjertighed, af fri uforskyldt Kjærlighed” is a doctrinal triad stressing wholly undeserved salvation.
* “den største er villig til at være alles Tjener” echoes Matthew 23:11 and the broader teaching of Mark 10:43–44.
* “Kjød og Blod fatter ikke...” echoes biblical language about natural human incapacity; compare Matthew 16:17 and 1 Corinthians 2.
* “efterlod os et Exempel, at vi skulde efterfølge hans Fodspor” echoes 1 Peter 2:21.
* “uden Persons Anseelse” is biblical language for impartiality, “without respect of persons.” Literal rendering may sound archaic in English prose, but here that biblical register is fitting.
* “Han agtede det ikke for et Rov at være Gud lig” echoes Philippians 2:6 in older translation form. Literal “robbery” would likely mislead; a rendering that preserves the biblical weight without doctrinal confusion is needed.
* “nagled ham til Korsets Træ” echoes crucifixion language with scriptural resonance, especially Acts and 1 Peter.
* “Guds Lam, som bar al Verdens Synd” echoes John 1:29.
* “lydig indtil Døden, ja Korsets Død” echoes Philippians 2:8.
* “slaa sig for sit Bryst” and “Gud være mig Synder naadig!” unmistakably echo Luke 18:13. “God be merciful to me, a sinner!” is the natural biblical equivalent.
* “Tornene paa den trange Vei” intensifies the biblical “narrow way” with suffering imagery; literal translation preserves force.

Places where literal translation would obscure force

* “billigt” must not be translated “cheap.”
* “Forsmædeligheds Sag” needs interpretive care; a wooden translation could sound incoherent. The intended sense is that their godliness has become bound up with pride and reproachful contempt.
* “Udfarelses Aand” should not be translated mechanically. The likely theological meaning should be rendered in a way that communicates filial relation through the Spirit.
* “et Rov” should not become a misleading idiom in English. It should be rendered in a way informed by Philippians 2:6.
* “hovmode sig selv” should not be flattened to “feel proud,” since the sense is active self-exaltation.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Thesis introduction: The Pharisee’s prayer reveals in one sum the whole Pharisaic mind, namely self-exalting righteousness before men.
* First movement: The preacher exposes how natural and even virtuous self-exaltation appears in human eyes.
* Scriptural contradiction: Christ declares that what is exalted among men is an abomination before God, and that God knows the heart behind self-justification.
* Ecclesial application: The same Pharisaic spirit returns within the Church when professed godliness becomes another form of pride.
* Specific pastoral application to ministers: Those who sit on Moses’ seat, teachers and leaders in God’s congregation, are tempted to steal for themselves the honor that belongs to God and His Word.
* Specific pastoral application to congregations: Congregation members often attach themselves to the priest rather than bow to the Word; personal conflict then overrides obedience.
* Consequence: This produces misery, division, domination by clergy, reaction by congregants, and tearing apart of the congregation.
* Transitional lament: It is exceedingly difficult to be a true priest and a true congregation member, because both must honor God’s Word and humble themselves.
* Theological solution: There is only one way— the way of the Holy Spirit. Any other spirit is the spirit of the world, which always leads to self-exaltation and pride.
* Further consequence: If the congregation is built without God’s Spirit, it may still gather around gifts, cleverness, or practicality, but human exaltation and pride will inevitably result.
* Exhortational pivot: Therefore, if the Church is not to fall back into Pharisaic being and papal darkness, both priests and congregations must heed Christ’s words.
* Doctrinal expansion: There is one Lord, one Father, one Guide, and therefore all are brothers; Christian equality is grounded in grace alone.
* Ethical implication: Christian freedom consists not in ruling but in serving one another, with the strongest bearing the greatest burdens.
* Anthropological contradiction: Flesh and blood cannot understand this paradoxical greatness and freedom.
* Christological resolution: Therefore one must look to Jesus Christ, who both spoke truth without partiality and suffered without bitterness.
* Christ as supreme example: He did not seek His own honor, humbled Himself, bore the world’s sin, and sought the Father’s glory and the Church’s good.
* Final appeal to conscience: Each person must therefore strike the breast and cry for mercy.
* Final universal indictment: No one has fully followed the example; all retain proud flesh.
* Concluding judgment promise: The Lord’s word stands forever—whoever exalts himself shall be humbled, and whoever humbles himself shall be exalted.

* Matthew 23:1–12: the appointed Gospel text quoted at the head of the sermon.

* Romans 8:14, 17 and broader discipleship theology: being led by the Spirit, through suffering to glory.
* Ephesians 2 and Titus 3 themes: salvation by grace, mercy, unmerited love.
* Mark 10:43–44 / Matthew 20:26–27: greatness as service.
* Matthew 16:17 / 1 Corinthians 2:14: flesh and blood does not comprehend divine truth.
* 1 Peter 2:21: Christ left us an example, that we should follow His steps.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* Warnings against pride must remain absolute and judicial, not advisory.
* The critique of both clergy and laity must remain equally piercing.
* Sentences of lament should preserve ache and gravity, not become melodramatic.
* Christ’s example must be rendered with reverent weight and doctrinal exactness.
* The final confession of sin must include collective guilt, not merely general human weakness.
* The concluding dominical sentence must sound fixed, irrevocable, and everlasting.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* “skal” in scriptural and sermonic statements must often remain “shall,” especially where judgment, command, or certainty is intended.
* “maa” should usually remain “must,” not “may have to” or “ought to,” unless context clearly requires otherwise.
* “bør” would normally require “ought,” but the present text relies more on “skal” and “maa.”
* “kan ei” / “ingen kan” must remain strong impossibility: “cannot” / “no one can.”
* “ville gjerne” in the Gospel citation is not mere future but desire: “they love to” or “they like to.”
* “skulle holde” in quoted Scripture should preserve obligation: “are to keep” or “shall keep,” depending on cadence.

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

* Selvophøielse = self-exaltation
* Selvfornedrelse = self-humbling

* Gudfrygtighed = godliness
* Guds Ord / Ordet = God’s Word / the Word

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* Moses’ seat must remain a concrete image of teaching authority.
* Heavy burdens laid on shoulders must remain vivid.
* The Lord’s flock must remain pastoral imagery, not abstract membership language.
* The narrow way and its thorns must remain embodied and difficult.
* Christ’s footsteps must remain concrete, not abstract “example.”
* Nailing to the tree of the cross must retain its stark physicality.
* Striking the breast must remain a bodily sign of contrition.
* Bearing the heaviest blow and heaviest burdens must remain concrete images of Christian service.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* Preserve the contrast between worldly plausibility and divine verdict.
* Preserve the contrast between false godliness and true humility.
* Preserve the contrast between ecclesial misery and the one true way of the Spirit.
* Preserve the contrast between worldly greatness and Christian greatness.
* Preserve the contrast between Christ’s fearlessness in speaking truth and His patience in suffering.
* Preserve the contrast between lofty doctrine and lowly penitence in the closing movement.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Keep long accumulative clauses where the sermonic reasoning mounts.
* Preserve repeated openings such as “There is only one...” and “there is no one...” to sustain oral force.
* Retain rhetorical questions as spoken appeals to conscience.
* Use punctuation that allows breath and weight: commas, semicolons, and periodic buildup where natural.
* Preserve the cadence of the closing citation so that it lands as a final spoken sentence of judgment and promise.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

Self-Exaltation and Self-Humbling.

Eleventh Sunday after Trinity.

Matthew 23:1–12. Then Jesus spoke to the people and to His disciples, saying: The scribes and the Pharisees sit on Moses’ seat. Therefore all that they tell you to keep, keep and do; but do not according to their works. For they indeed say it, but do it not. For they bind heavy burdens, such as are hard to bear, and lay them upon men’s shoulders; but they themselves will not move them with their finger. But all their works they do in order to be seen by men; for they make their phylacteries broad and the tassels on their garments large. And they love to sit uppermost at feasts and in the chief seats in the synagogues. And they love to be greeted in the marketplaces and to be called by men, Rabbi, Rabbi. But you shall not allow yourselves to be called Rabbi; for one is your Guide, Christ, but you are all brothers. And you shall call no man on earth your Father; for one is your Father, He who is in Heaven. And neither shall you allow yourselves to be called guides; for one is your Guide, Christ. But the greatest among you shall be your servant. But whoever exalts himself shall be humbled, and whoever humbles himself shall be exalted.

“I thank You, God, that I am not as other men are,” says the Pharisee who went up to the temple to pray. This word reveals in a single sum the whole Pharisaic mind. It appears so right and proper in the eyes of men that one tries to rise above another, that we are almost inclined to regard it as a virtue, contrary to all law and order, if anyone dares, together with our Lord and Savior, to speak the naked truth: “What is high among men is an abomination before God.”

Can it really be true? Has not an honorable man the right to praise himself for his honorableness? Has not an upright man reason to praise himself for his uprightness? Has not a rich man reason to be proud of the wealth he has acquired by diligence? Is it not altogether fair to despise thieves, drunkards, robbers, deceivers, adulterers, and ruffians? Thus one may ask, almost without end; and the answer comes so naturally and so straightforwardly from the mouth of the world that it seems almost impossible to object to it.

But the Savior nevertheless dares to do so. He says: “You are those who justify yourselves before men, but God knows your hearts.” He has no tolerance for the false show that conceals the proud thoughts of the world. Among men such pride is well regarded, if only it keeps within reasonable bounds; but before God it is nonetheless an abomination.

But within God’s Church, much of the same spirit unfortunately returns. There are many who have fallen into the Pharisees’ and the scribes’ dreadful sin. Their godliness has become for them a matter of pride and reproach. They will not place themselves on a level with the world, and as they forget love, they become, by the new pride with which they despise the world, once again equal with the world in their hearts.

And those who sit on Moses’ seat, who are teachers and leaders in God’s congregation, how often do they not succumb to the terrible temptation which drove the Pharisees and the scribes into pride and self-exaltation. The honor that belongs to God and His Word they steal for themselves and seize to themselves. And the congregations are unfortunately often so willing to have it thus. There are many members of the congregation who would rather hold to the priest than bow before the Word; and there are many members of the congregation who would rather go out of the congregation when they have become enemies with the priest than follow Jesus’ command and injunction, to heed the Word and let the person be a matter by himself.

From this there has come an abundance of misery also among us. A priest becomes great in himself because his calling is high and holy, and he wants to rule over the Lord’s flock instead of serving it with the Lord’s Word and the Lord’s love. By his lust to rule and his self-importance he calls forth resistance and strife. Then the priest’s opponents are to be God’s opponents, and thus the congregation is torn apart. And conversely: members of the congregation come into conflict with the priest, and then they use their personal ill will toward the priest as an excuse for their own impenitence and stubborn resistance to the Word, and then either the priest must go, or else they must go out of the congregation.

Or is there anyone who can deny that thus it has often happened?

O, how difficult it is to be a true priest and a true member of the congregation! To esteem God’s Word highly and oneself lowly, to set the Lord high and oneself low, when shall we learn this aright?

There is only one way by which it can be learned. It is a very narrow way; for it is the Holy Spirit’s work. If any other spirit reigns in priest and congregation than the Holy Spirit, then it is the spirit of the world. But the spirit of the world always leads to self-exaltation and pride. If God’s congregation is built without God’s Spirit, it is gathered either by the priest’s brilliant gifts or by the priest’s cleverness and cunning or by the congregation members’ practical ability, and the Spirit from God is lacking—then there must arise human exaltation and pride, however much a show of godliness may be cast over it all.

If, therefore, when we build God’s Church on earth, we are not to fall back into the Pharisees’ way and the darkness of the papacy, then it is necessary to heed Jesus’ word concerning the scribes and the Pharisees. It concerns the priests, and it concerns the congregations. There is only one way for both to true and genuine humility before God and to true and genuine and living love toward one another. It is, as said above, the Holy Spirit’s way. There is only one Lord, that is Jesus Christ, but “no one can call Jesus Lord except by the Holy Spirit.” There is only one Father, namely He who is in Heaven, but no one can call Him Father except by the filial Spirit of adoption. There is only one Guide, that is Christ, and no one can be Christ’s follower through the cross to glory except the one who is driven by Christ’s Spirit. Therefore you are all brothers, and no one has a higher rank or is of nobler birth than another. In this is the Christians’ equality; in this is their brotherhood. This recognition is that which must penetrate all God’s children: By grace, by mercy, by free unmerited love God has saved me and us all; therefore I have nothing of which to boast.

But herein consists the Christians’ freedom, that the greatest is willing to be the servant of all. Not that the priest shall command the congregation, nor that the congregation shall command the priest; but that all should be willing to serve one another, and the strongest and greatest shall be the most eager to take up the heaviest burdens, the most willing to bear the heaviest blow, the most enduring in labor, the most patient in suffering.

But flesh and blood does not comprehend that kind of greatness and that kind of freedom. It appears a contradiction to our reason that greatness and freedom should consist in humility and self-humbling. Then it is needful to look to Jesus Christ, who left us an example, that we should follow His footsteps.

He understood it: both to speak the truth wholly without respect of persons and to suffer without murmuring whatever might follow upon the truth’s fearless testimony. He did not bend the Lord’s truth in order himself to gain exaltation and honor; but He was not embittered against those who for that reason nailed Him to the tree of the cross. He did not regard it as a thing to be seized to be equal with God, but He became willingly the Lamb of God, who bore all the world’s sin, and humbled Himself, so that He became obedient unto death, indeed the death of the cross. He sought not His own honor, but the Father’s; He sought not His own welfare, but the congregation’s.

O, what an example to follow! Therefore let each one strike his breast and say: “God be merciful to me, a sinner!” For truly, there is no one who has wholly followed the example; there is no one who has not bent back before the thorns on the narrow way, there is no one who does not carry within him a proud flesh that gladly would exalt itself and despise others; there is no one who is not reluctant to be the servant of all.

But the Lord’s word stands fast forever: “Whoever exalts himself shall be humbled, and whoever humbles himself shall be exalted.”

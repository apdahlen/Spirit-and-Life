
STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* Skrøbeligheds Aand: Literally “spirit of infirmity.” This is not merely weakness in a general sense, but the biblical expression from Luke 13, carrying both bodily affliction and a spiritual-demonic dimension. It must not be flattened into mere “illness” without preserving the scriptural weight.

* sammenkrumpen: More vivid than simply “bent over.” It conveys being drawn together, shrunken, doubled in upon oneself. The bodily image should remain severe and visible.

* løst / løses / Baand / bundet: These words form a sustained liberation motif. They belong both to the miracle narrative and to the sermon’s doctrine of deliverance from Satan, sin, and bondage. English equivalents must remain consistent enough to preserve the chain: bound, loosed, bond, bondage.

* Skrøbelighed: In context, not only “frailty” in the modern mild sense, but disabling infirmity. “Infirmity” best preserves gravity and biblical resonance.

* Mismod: More than passing discouragement; it suggests despondency, dejection, spiritual heaviness. “Despondency” or “dejection” is preferable to weaker modern terms.

* tilvisse: Archaic emphatic affirmation, approximately “indeed,” “certainly,” “to be sure.” It should retain sermonic firmness.

* kanske: Archaic/older Scandinavian form of “perhaps.” No difficulty in sense, but its placement can matter rhetorically.

* Elendighed / Jammer: These carry strong existential and spiritual misery, not mere inconvenience. “Misery” and “wretchedness” may be needed depending on context.

* sønderslide et Hjerte: Literally “tear a heart asunder.” The expression is emotionally and spiritually intense and should not be weakened into “sadden deeply.”

* ligedoverfor: “Toward,” “in the face of,” “with regard to,” here indicating culpable indifference toward sin. The moral relation must stay explicit.

* fortvivlede Vanvid: Literally “desperate madness.” Strong moral-spiritual language; it should not be softened.

* Livsrod: “Root of life,” a vivid image of inward vitality being gnawed away. The metaphor should remain rather than be paraphrased clinically.

* Ulivssaar: Literally a wound of death/non-life, an inward mortal wound. This is highly figurative and should not be reduced to “problem” or “hurt.”

* svar Byrde: “Heavy” or “grievous” burden, not “difficult” in a mild sense.

* bæver tilbage: To recoil in trembling fear. This preserves both emotional and bodily response.

* ynkes over: To have pity, compassion, deep merciful regard. “Pity” can sound thin in English; “have compassion on” often better preserves dignity.

* Vælde: Dominion, power, sway. In Hebrews 2:14 language, it must preserve the sense of rule or dominion, not mere strength.

* formedelst: “Because of,” “through,” “by reason of,” with an older formal doctrinal cadence.

* Trældom: Bondage, servitude, slavery. In this theological context, “bondage” is usually best.

* skjænker: “Bestows,” “grants,” not merely “pours” or “gives,” especially in sacramental context.

* Salighed: Not merely happiness, but blessedness/salvation. The context determines whether “salvation” or “blessedness” is best.

* udsluppen: Escaped, delivered out, gotten free. The sense of actual release from captivity must remain vivid.

* Livskilden / Kildevæld: “Fountain of life” / “spring-well,” rich biblical imagery that should remain concrete.

* magtpaaliggende: A solemn expression of earnest concern or urgent obligation. It should not be diluted into casual concern.

* nidkjære: Zealous, fervent. The word carries religious earnestness and pastoral intensity.

* Løndom: Secrecy, hiddenness. “In secret” preserves biblical and sermonic cadence.

* ubeskåret Vidnesbyrd: Literally “uncut testimony,” meaning whole, untrimmed, uncompromised witness. This must not be modernized into something flat like “clear message.”

* vederkvægende: Refreshing, reviving, restoring. It has biblical devotional force.

* døddende og levendegjørende: “Slaying and making alive,” probably echoing biblical formulations. The sharp paradox must remain.

* aandløs: Literally “spiritless,” but in context also spiritually lifeless. It should preserve moral and spiritual indictment.

Syntactic Hazards

* The sermon moves in long cumulative sentences with repeated clauses and parallel structures. These should not be broken into short modern sentences unless absolutely necessary for intelligibility.

* Rhetorical questions are central to the sermonic argument. They are not requests for information but instruments of conviction and exhortation. Their force must remain direct.

* Semicolon logic and clause chaining build doctrinal consequence step by step. Restructuring them too freely would flatten the reasoning.

* The sermon shifts between narrative exposition, doctrinal diagnosis, direct address, exhortation, and appeal. These transitions are often embedded within the same paragraph and must remain visible.

* Repetition is purposeful: lider, lider; kommer, kom; der, hvor ... der, hvor ... It should largely be preserved because it creates oral cadence and spiritual pressure.

* Apostrophic address (“O,” “Du,” “Vil du ikke komme?”) is integral to the preaching voice and should not be toned down.

* Scriptural quotation and sermonic prose are interwoven. The translation must distinguish neither too sharply nor too little, but preserve continuity.

Theological Hazards

* The sermon identifies sin as the true source of human misery. This doctrinal assertion must remain explicit and uncompromised.

* Affliction is treated both bodily and spiritually; the miracle becomes a figure of deliverance from sin and Satan. Translation must preserve both levels.

* Satan is named personally and actively as the binder of souls. This should not be generalized into impersonal evil.

* Christ is presented as the only Physician, the compassionate deliverer, and the one who through death has destroyed the devil’s dominion. The exclusivity and doctrinal precision must remain intact.

* The means of grace are explicitly named: the preached Word, Baptism, and the Supper. Their sacramental efficacy is asserted, not merely symbolized.

* Syndernes Forladelse, Liv og Salighed must retain doctrinal clarity. These are not emotional states but concrete gifts of salvation.

* The relation between Law and Gospel near the end is essential. The witness must be both wounding and healing, killing and making alive. This Lutheran polarity must remain sharp.

* The rebuke of clergy is theological and pastoral, not merely moral criticism. “Heartless” and “spiritless” priests are condemned for failing to mediate liberating Word.

Voice Hazards

* The tone is solemn, earnest, exhortational, and pastorally severe. Any smoothing into gentle devotional prose would distort the author.

* The address to the sinner is immediate and implicating: “Du er syg, du dør.” This must remain stark and direct.

* The prose rises repeatedly into urgent appeal without becoming theatrical. English diction must preserve heat through consequence and inevitability, not melodrama.

* The sermon alternates compassion for sufferers and severity toward indifference. These tonal contrasts are part of the voice and must not be equalized.

* The preacher’s lament over neglected worship and negligent ministers belongs to an older Lutheran homiletic register. It should remain grave and public, not personalized or psychologized.

* Biblical resonance should be felt throughout, but without importing artificial Jacobean archaism. The dignity must come from syntax, rhythm, and doctrinal firmness.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* Abrahams Datter: “Daughter of Abraham” is directly biblical from Luke 13:16. It signifies covenant inclusion and dignity. It should remain literal.

* bundet af Satan / Djævelens Baand: This is not merely metaphorical language for hardship; it reflects a biblical-theological understanding of spiritual bondage. “Bound by Satan” and “the devil’s bonds/bondage” preserve that framework.

* Ormen gnaver paa deres Livsrod: This image suggests corruption eating at the root of life from within. It resembles biblical and devotional imagery of inward decay, conscience, and mortality. A literal rendering preserves force better than explanation.

* skjule sit Ulivssaar: A figurative inward death-wound, hidden from self and others. Literal retention is preferable because the image is theologically charged.

* søge Lægen: Christ as Physician is a deep biblical and devotional motif. It naturally recalls the Gospel image of those who are sick needing a physician. “Seek the Physician” is better than “get help.”

* himmelske Læge: “Heavenly Physician” preserves the devotional-Christological title.

* Herrens Hus / Herrens Dag / Herrens Ord: These are not casual references to church, Sunday, and Scripture; they carry covenantal and liturgical weight. Literal rendering is appropriate.

* Daabens Vand og Nadverens Vin skjænker Syndernes Forladelse, Liv og Salighed: This expression is sacramental and confessional. It should be rendered in a way that preserves the claim that these gifts are bestowed there. “Bestow forgiveness of sins, life, and salvation” is likely strongest.

* Livskilden aabnet / det rige Kildevæld: These are fountain images, with likely biblical echoes from prophetic and Johannine imagery about living water and the fountain of life. They should remain as fountain/spring language.

* et helt og ubeskåret Vidnesbyrd om Synd og Naade: A vivid pastoral idiom meaning a full, untrimmed, uncompromising proclamation of sin and grace. Literal imagery helps preserve the sermonic edge.

* sønderskjærende og vederkvægende, saarende og lægende, døddende og levendegjørende: This chain expresses the double work of God’s Word, especially in Lutheran preaching: the Word wounds and heals, kills and makes alive. The parallelism must be retained almost exactly.

* Fanger skal frigives, døde skal blive levende: Biblical deliverance language, echoing prophetic and Gospel patterns. Literal rendering preserves the scriptural cadence.

* KJV equivalents where useful in biblical citations:

* spirit of infirmity

* daughter of Abraham

* whom Satan hath bound (though pronouns must be modernized in the translation)

* had the power of death / dominion of death should be handled carefully; the KJV-like wording can guide rhythm, but archaic pronouns should be avoided.

* Expressions where literal translation alone could obscure force:

* Skrøbelighed by itself might sound too mild as “frailty”; in context “infirmity” is needed.

* ynkes over as merely “feel sorry for” would trivialize Christ’s compassion.

* aandløs as merely “without spirit” would be awkward; “spiritless” with context retains force.

* ubeskåret if translated without explanation in analysis could seem strange, but in the final translation “unmutilated” would be too physical; “whole and uncut” or “whole and untrimmed” witness better conveys the meaning.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Opening narrative frame:

* The sermon begins with the Gospel account of the woman afflicted for eighteen years and healed on the Sabbath.

* Her desire for deliverance is presented as self-evident and humane.

* Christ’s power and compassion are immediately affirmed.

* First doctrinal expansion:

* The woman is not unique.

* Countless others suffer bodily and inwardly for years and decades.

* The particular miracle becomes a window into universal human misery.

* Central theological diagnosis:

* Many sufferers do not seek help because they do not understand their true misery.

* The true suffering and root of all suffering is sin.

* Humanity’s indifference toward sin is portrayed as tragic and irrational.

* Intensification through direct appeal:

* The preacher laments those who are spiritually dying yet refuse the Physician.

* He imagines crying out directly to secure sinners: “You are sick, you are dying.”

* Pastoral distinction:

* Not all are indifferent.

* Some do feel their misery and know its cause in sin.

* Yet even these often delay, lacking courage and strength to seek Christ.

* Christological consolation:

* Jesus has compassion on all such bound souls.

* His compassion is grounded in his saving work: through death he has rendered powerless the one who had the dominion of death.

* Therefore help is both willing and effectual.

* Practical summons:

* The hearer is directed where to find Christ: where the Word is preached, where the congregation gathers, where Baptism and the Supper bestow forgiveness, life, and salvation.

* The miracle is transposed into the present life of the Church.

* Urgent invitation:

* Come to the Lord’s House bowed down by sins and sorrows.

* There long suffering may end abruptly in absolution and freedom.

* Broader lament and ecclesial exhortation:

* Many neglect the Lord’s House, Day, and Word, thereby neglecting their own salvation.

* The congregation is exhorted to seek that the Lord’s House be filled with suffering souls.

* Address to ministers:

* Ministers must awaken and zealously speak the Word of life.

* Many hidden sufferers exist whom they do not perceive.

* They must proclaim a full witness of sin and grace.

* Climactic conclusion:

* Through the Gospel’s power, Sunday after Sunday, the bound will be loosed, captives freed, sick healed, suffering refreshed, dead made alive.

* Negative warning: let none be like the heartless ruler of the synagogue.

* Final condemnation: every heartless and spiritless priest shares his mind, even if he does not speak his words.

* Final petition: may the Lord grant words from Law and Gospel that break bonds and set the bound free.

STEP 4: BIBLICAL ECHO IDENTIFICATION

* Luke 13:10–17 is quoted at the beginning, forming the sermon’s explicit scriptural basis.

* spirit of infirmity / bowed together / daughter of Abraham / whom Satan had bound / loosed from this bond on the Sabbath day: all from Luke 13:10–17.

* the only Physician: likely echoes the Gospel motif in Matthew 9:12, Mark 2:17, and Luke 5:31: those who are sick need a physician.

* by death he has made powerless the one who had the dominion of death ... to free those who through fear of death were subject to bondage all their lifetime: clear echo of Hebrews 2:14–15.

* where two or three gather / Christ present where Word and sacrament are given: not a direct quotation, but resonates with New Testament ecclesial presence language, especially Matthew 18:20 and Lutheran sacramental theology.

* Baptism and the Supper bestowing forgiveness, life, and salvation: confessional-sacramental language rooted in New Testament promises and Lutheran catechetical formulations.

* fountain/source imagery:

* Livskilden may echo Psalm 36:9: “with you is the fountain of life.”

* Kildevæld and healing imagery may also evoke prophetic and Johannine living-water language (Isaiah 55, Jeremiah 2:13, John 4, John 7).

* killing and making alive: likely echoes Deuteronomy 32:39 and 1 Samuel 2:6, also a common Lutheran formulation for the twofold work of God’s Word.

* captives freed, the bound released: resonates with Isaiah 61 and Luke 4 themes of liberation.

* dead made alive: broad New Testament resurrection and regeneration language, especially Johannine and Pauline echoes.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* The warnings to sinners must remain severe: the preacher says they are sick unto death and dying. This should remain uncompromising.

* Christ’s compassion must be full and warm, but never sentimental. The force comes from his real power to save.

* The lament over human indifference to sin must retain horror and grief.

* Appeals such as “come, come!” must remain urgent and oral.

* The rebuke to ministers must stay sharp. The sermon does not merely encourage better preaching; it indicts dead, heartless ministry as akin to the synagogue ruler’s spirit.

* The closing vision of liberation through preaching should rise with strong cumulative cadence.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* bør / burde: should, ought, must according to context. In Luke 13:16, “ought not this woman ... be loosed” preserves argumentative force.

* maa / må: must, have to, or may depending on context, but necessity should not be weakened.

* kan ei / kunde aldeles ikke: “could not at all” or “could absolutely not,” preserving impossibility.

* vil: often expresses will, intention, or refusal, not mere future. In many places it must remain “will” rather than softened English constructions.

* skal: often future with strong certainty or promise; sometimes near-imperatival inevitability. It should retain firmness.

* lader det være dig magtpaaliggende: should not become weak advice; it is an earnest exhortation approaching imperative force.

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

* Synd = sin
* Naade = grace
* Syndernes Forladelse = forgiveness of sins
* Liv = life
* Salighed = salvation, unless context requires blessedness; here “salvation” is generally preferable
* Djævel = devil
* Satan = Satan
* bundet / Baand / Trældom = bound / bond or bonds / bondage
* Læge = Physician
* Herrens Hus = the Lord’s House
* Herrens Dag = the Lord’s Day
* Herrens Ord = the Lord’s Word
* Guds Ord = the Word of God
* Evangelium = Gospel
* Menighed = congregation, or Church where context is broader; in this sermon “congregation” often best for the assembled people, “Church” for broader ecclesial sense
* Lov og Pris = praise and thanksgiving? Here “praise” is sufficient since “lov” in older Scandinavian devotional usage often means praise. But in “Herrens Lov og Herrens Evangelium,” Lov must be “Law.” The contexts must be distinguished carefully.

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* The bowed body rising with healing mirrors the soul rising from sorrow and despondency. This figurative parallel should remain visible.

* Sin as the source or fountainhead of misery must remain causal and organic.

* The worm gnawing at the root of life must remain concrete.

* The hidden death-wound and the heart’s blood running drop by drop must remain vivid and corporeal.

* The fountain of life and rich spring-well of preaching must remain living-water imagery.

* The Word as cutting, refreshing, wounding, healing, slaying, making alive must remain a sequence of active metaphors, not abstractions.

* Bonds being broken and captives set free must stay as liberation imagery throughout.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* Compassion for the suffering woman contrasts with indignation toward the synagogue ruler.

* Lament over sinners’ indifference contrasts with tender invitation to those who feel their misery.

* The sermon alternates diagnosis and consolation, warning and promise, rebuke and encouragement.

* The address to the congregation is hopeful and mobilizing; the address to ministers is both exhortative and accusatory.

* The concluding petition tempers severity with dependence on the Lord’s gift.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Repetitions such as “lider og lider,” “kom, kom,” and “der, hvor ...” should be preserved wherever possible.

* The translation should keep major periodic sentences intact to preserve pulpit rhythm.

* Vocative openings (“O,” “But,” “Will you not come?”) should remain audible.

* Cadence should rise toward the end of paragraphs, especially in exhortatory and climactic passages.

* Lineation in the canvas is plain text, but the prose itself should still read as proclaimable speech.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

Eighteen Years of Sufferings Ended on a Sabbath.

Nineteenth Sunday after Trinity.

Luke 13:10–17. And he was teaching in one of the synagogues on the Sabbath. And behold, there was a woman who had had a spirit of infirmity eighteen years, and she was bowed together and could not at all straighten herself up. But when Jesus saw her, he called to her and said to her: Woman, you are loosed from your infirmity. And he laid his hands on her, and immediately she straightened herself up and praised God. Then the ruler of the synagogue answered, being angry that Jesus had healed on the Sabbath, and said to the people: There are six days on which one ought to work; come therefore on those days and let yourselves be healed, and not on the Sabbath day! Then the Lord answered him and said: You hypocrite! Does not every one of you on the Sabbath loose his ox or his ass from the stall and lead it away to water it? But this woman, who is a daughter of Abraham, whom Satan had bound, think of it, for eighteen years, ought she not to be loosed from this bond on the Sabbath day? And when he said this, all who were against him were put to shame, and all the people rejoiced over all the glorious works that were done by him.

When a poor woman has suffered for eighteen years, there is no one who wonders that she would gladly be delivered from her sufferings. It is so heavy a fate to bear that, if anyone could understand even a little of it, compassion would surely fill his soul, and this wish would rise within him: Oh, that help might be found for such misery!

Jesus had power to help, and he helped. The sick woman in our text received unexpected help on that Sabbath when Jesus was teaching in the synagogue. And as her body was healed, so too her soul rose up out of the depth of sorrow and despondency, and God’s praise rose up from her heart and mouth. Happy woman, who on that Sabbath found her way to the synagogue and to Jesus.

But is she the only one who suffers, and the only one who is helped? Is there not a multitude, which no one can number, who suffer and suffer from day to day, from year to year? Are there not millions who bear pain and sorrow through a long life, a life that seems longer still because it is so full of suffering? Yes indeed, there are many, many, innumerable ones who suffer, suffer in the body, suffer in the soul, suffer in heart and mind; sometimes for eighteen years, sometimes for twice eighteen.

And yet there are many among these suffering ones who seek no help in their distress; perhaps there are many among them who do not understand their own misery. For the true suffering of the human heart, and the source of all sufferings, is its sin; and sin—who knows it, who seeks deliverance from it? It is a sight fitted to tear a heart asunder with the deepest sorrow, to behold the incomprehensible indifference of men toward sin and its dreadful consequences. They are sick, sick unto death, sick in soul and heart with a mortal sickness, and yet this desperate madness, that they will have no help, will seek no help! Even if now and then they perceive how the worm gnaws at the root of their life, yet they seek no remedy; they only try to hide their death-wound from themselves and others and to continue their accustomed life, while their heart’s blood runs drop by drop, until there is no hope any more.

Oh, that it were possible to cry it into the ear and heart of the secure sinner: You are sick, you are dying; hasten to seek the Physician, the only Physician who can help you, who can help all.

But then there are also those who feel their misery, and for whom sin has become a grievous and pressing burden. They know not only that they are wretched, but they also know whence their misery comes. But even these often go on for many long periods and do not seek the help, do not seek the Physician. They lack courage to go to him, they lack strength to seek him out; they recoil trembling before the Physician, and they remain lying in the sin that torments them with unspeakable agonies.

Is there anyone who has compassion on all these manifold sufferers? Yes, the same one who had compassion on the woman who for eighteen years had been bound by Satan, Jesus has compassion on all those human souls whom Satan has bound. Whether it be for a long time or a short; whether it be eighteen years or twice as long; whether you understand that you are bound, or imagine that you are free. Jesus has compassion on all these suffering human hearts; he would so gladly make them free. He has the power to do so; for through death he has made powerless the one who had the dominion of death, namely the devil, in order to free those, as many as through fear of death were under bondage all their lifetime. A man bound and ill-used by the devil is an object of the heartfelt compassion of God and of our Savior.

Will you then not be helped, when Jesus both can and will help? Have you no compassion on yourself, so that you will seek out the heavenly Physician? Or do you not know where he is to be found? Try there where that daughter of Abraham found him, in the synagogue, where he was teaching, on the Sabbath, when he spoke the Word of God. There, where the Word of God is spoken, there, where the Gospel is preached, there, where the congregation gathers in prayer, there, where the water of Baptism and the wine of the Supper bestow forgiveness of sins, life, and salvation, there he himself is, the living Son of God, personally present; there he will lay his own helping hands on you, and you shall be healed. The sufferings of a long life shall abruptly end, and it shall become a blessed Sunday for you, if you come bowed down by your sins and sorrows to the Lord’s House, and go forth free, saved, escaped from the devil’s bonds by the forgiveness of sins.

Will you not come? You have suffered long enough, you have been sick so many, many years. All can yet become well again on the Lord’s Day, in the Lord’s House. There the fountain of life is opened, there the remedy is to be found, come, come!

Oh, there are so many who neglect the Lord’s House and the Lord’s Day and the Lord’s Word, though it is their own deliverance and salvation that by this they neglect. Yet there is, God be praised, an incomprehensible riches of the proclamation of God’s Word among us, and yet so many who do not come to the rich spring-well. Congregation of God, awake, and let it be a matter of urgent concern to you that the Lord’s House may be filled, filled with sick, suffering human beings who can find remedy for their souls through the Lord’s Word.

And you, servants of the Lord in his House, awake, and become zealous to speak the Word of life to human hearts. There are many who suffer, and you do not know it; there are many who hunger, without your being able to see it; there are many who weep in secret, whose tears are hidden from you; there is many a heart that bleeds from a hidden wound.

Oh, let a whole and untrimmed witness concerning sin and grace sound forth, cutting asunder and refreshing, wounding and healing, slaying and making alive, every Sunday from your pulpits, and then the incomprehensibly blessed thing shall come to pass from Sunday to Sunday by the power of the Gospel of God: the bound shall be loosed, captives shall be set free, the sick shall be healed, the suffering shall be refreshed, the dead shall become alive. Let none be found like that heartless ruler of the synagogue, who was angry that Jesus healed on the Sabbath. Alas, alas, he is not the only one of his kind in the history of the congregation of God. Every heartless and spiritless priest has the same mind, even if he does not have the same words in his mouth.

Think on the poor souls in the devil’s bonds, and may the Lord himself grant to find releasing words from the Lord’s Law and the Lord’s Gospel, to break the bonds and set the bound at liberty!

STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* "Ven" must be rendered as "friend," but with full weight: not casual companionship, rather a faithful, trustworthy, spiritually sufficient friend.
* "Nød og Død" carries both temporal distress and ultimate extremity; "in need and death" is formally accurate, but "in distress and death" better preserves solemnity and breadth.
* "trygt kan sætte min hele Hjertens Tillid" is stronger than simple "trust"; it is a full-hearted, secure entrusting of oneself.
* "Trang og Tørv" is an older pair for inward need, want, necessity, poverty of condition; it should not be flattened into a single modern word.
* "redebon" means ready, willing, prepared; it has moral eagerness, not mere availability.
* "tilsiger" means what love and faithful friendship require, prescribe, bid him do; not merely "suggest."
* "uforanderlige, hos Gud" is textually compressed and elliptical; the sense is that such friendship has its ground in what is eternal and unchangeable with God. The translation must preserve the God-grounded absoluteness.
* "Forsoningslære" means doctrine of atonement/reconciliation; not merely "teaching about forgiveness."
* "Tro-skyldighed" means childlike sincerity, simplicity, trustfulness; not gullibility.
* "Gjenstridighed" means obstinacy, rebellious resistance.
* "Vantro" is unbelief, not just doubt.
* "ynkedes inderlig" means was moved with deep compassion; it must retain affective strength.
* "forkomme paa Veien" means perish, collapse, fail on the way; stronger than merely "grow tired."
* "heftig bevæget i Aanden" and "rystet" are forceful expressions; they must not be softened into mild sadness.
* "undse dig" means feel embarrassed, hesitate shyly, hold back.
* "tager sig af" in Hebrews 2 is not generic concern only; it reflects taking hold of/helping/coming to the aid of.
* "fristes" may mean tempted or tried; context in Hebrews allows both resonance and aid under trial.
* "sætter sit Liv til" means lay down his life.
* "raa dført sig" means conferred, taken counsel.
* "Opvækkelse" is raising, awakening from death; "resuscitation" would be too clinical.
* "Forbitrelsens og Forargelsens Bæger" is a potent metaphor: the cup of bitterness and offense/scandal.
* "iskjænket" literally poured out/served into the cup; image must remain vivid.
* "Udaad" means evil deed, outrage, crime.
* "gudfrygtigheds Skin" means semblance/show of godliness or piety.
* "befatte sig med saa smaa Ting" means concern himself with such small matters.
* "henrive" here means to be carried away, deeply moved.
* "Hjerteløshed" is heartlessness.
* "lindre og læge" is a paired formula: relieve and heal.
* "turde bede om eller forstaa" means dare to ask or understand; doctrinally important, echoing divine superabundance.
* "Rettesnor" is rule, guiding norm, standard.
* "Menneskens Søn" should likely be rendered with biblical force as "the Son of Man," though literally archaic plural-genitive morphology appears in the source.
* "levende Grundvold" is living foundation, not abstract basis only.
* "holder fast ved ham" has covenantal-existential force: hold fast to him.
* "fuldtro Ven" is a fully faithful, utterly dependable friend.
* "Efold" is singleness/simplicity of heart.
* "Trosind" is believing mind/disposition, trustful spirit.
* "uforvisnelig" means unfading, imperishable.
* "Salighed" is blessedness, salvation-blessedness; not mere happiness.
* "sviger" in the hymn means fails, proves false, betrays.
* "stedt i Sorg og Nød" means placed/brought into sorrow and distress.
* "Adams Kjøn" means Adam’s race, humankind; "gender" would be wrong.

Syntactic Hazards

* The sermon proceeds in long accumulative sentences with doctrinal movement embedded in clause chains; these should not be chopped into short modern English sentences.
* Enumerations such as the four marks of a true friend carry rhetorical architecture; numbering and sequence must remain clear.
* Semicolon logic is central: propositions are often balanced or intensified rather than simply linked.
* Biblical citation is woven into the sermonic prose; transitions into and out of quoted Scripture must remain natural and weighty.
* Rhetorical questions are not casual but press the conscience and advance doctrine.
* Repeated formulae such as "En saadan Ven er han" and "En saadan Ven er Jesus" structure the meditation; repetition must be preserved.
* Contrastive turns introduced by "Men," "Thi," "Derfor," "Saaledes" are doctrinal pivots and should remain explicit.
* Embedded explanatory clauses often interpret earlier claims; removing them would weaken the argument.
* Apostrophic address to the hearer near the close must retain directness and oral force.
* The final question "Maria, Marta, tror du dette?" intentionally transfers Christ’s question to the hearer; it must remain sharp and immediate.

Theological Hazards

* The balance of Christ’s true humanity and true divinity is central: "if he is the Son of God, then he is also Mary’s Son." This must remain doctrinally exact.
* Christ must remain personal, not dissolved into abstraction: the contrast between dogmatic orthodoxy and cold Christianity without the personal friend Jesus is essential.
* Divine compassion is not sentimental but incarnational and redemptive.
* Sin, unbelief, obstinacy, judgment, sorrow, death, and resurrection must retain full theological seriousness.
* Christ’s love is shown supremely in self-giving unto death; the Bethany miracle is read in light of the Cross.
* The sermon ties Christ’s power to obedience to the Father; omnipotence without pride and humility alongside power are part of Christology here.
* The language of faith and unbelief must not be psychologized into mere feeling states.
* "Guds Herlighed" is the glory of God in revelatory and saving manifestation.
* The resurrection of Lazarus is both historical event and pastoral pledge of the resurrection of believers.
* Final consolation culminates in "the resurrection of the dead" and "eternal and unfading joy and blessedness"; eschatological clarity must remain.

Voice Hazards

* The tone is solemn, pastoral, exhortational, and doctrinally warm without sentimentality.
* Over-modernization would erase the sermonic rhythm and moral seriousness.
* The preacher repeatedly presses the hearer toward trust, bringing conscience and affliction directly before Christ.
* Emotional intensity comes from inevitability, truth, and consequence, not melodrama.
* Repetition is part of the persuasive force and should not be reduced for stylistic variety.
* Direct appeals beginning "Søger du..." and "Har du fundet ham..." must sound like living proclamation, not literary paraphrase.
* The closing devotional poem must remain simple, lyrical, and pious, but not childish.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* "i Nød og Død" means in the whole range from earthly distress to dying itself. It names extremity and total vulnerability.
* "sætte min hele Hjertens Tillid" means to place the whole trust of one’s heart in someone. It is more existential and covenantal than ordinary trust.
* "i alt mit Hjertes inderste Trang og Tørv" expresses the innermost need and want of the heart. The doubled phrase intensifies human inward poverty and necessity.
* "flyr til ham" means flees to him. This has strong biblical and devotional force: Christ as refuge. Literal retention is preferable because "turns to him" is too weak.
* "barnlig Troskyldighed" is childlike trustful simplicity, not naivete. It belongs to pietistic spirituality and echoes Christ’s teaching on becoming like little children.
* "græde med de sørgende" naturally recalls Romans 12:15, "weep with those who weep." That biblical cadence should be preserved in English.
* Hebrews 2:16-18 is explicitly cited. "Han jo ikke tager sig af Engle, men af Abrahams Afkom" resonates with the scriptural idea that Christ takes hold of/help brings aid to Abraham’s offspring. A translation should preserve the soteriological force rather than reduce it to general concern.
* "sætte sit Liv til for sine Venner" clearly echoes John 15:13. "Lay down one’s life for one’s friends" is the natural biblical English equivalent.
* "den sidste Draabe i ... Bæger" is an idiom of final provocation: the last drop in the cup. Because the cup metaphor is alive in the sentence, it should remain a cup, not become an abstract "final straw."
* "smykke deres Udaad med hyklet Gudfrygtigheds Skin" means to adorn their evil deed with a hypocritical appearance of piety. The image matters because it exposes false religion.
* "lade sig henrive af sine personlige Følelser" suggests being carried away by personal emotion; in context it represents the unbelieving judgment of onlookers.
* "Vantroens og Hjerteløshedens Tale" means the speech or language of unbelief and heartlessness. It personifies a spiritual condition.
* "han baade kan og vil hjælpe langt, langt over, hvad du endog turde bede om eller forstaa" strongly recalls Ephesians 3:20. A natural equivalent is "far beyond all that you might even dare to ask or understand," preserving both daring and transcendence.
* "ved dit Hjertes Dør; han banker paa" echoes Revelation 3:20. The phrase should remain literal because the biblical image is theologically active.
* "gaar som Vandflod over din Sjæl" evokes overwhelming flood imagery from the Psalms and prophets. This must remain figurative, not be reduced to "overwhelms you."
* "læge din Sjæl" means heal your soul, with pastoral and salvific depth.
* "i de dødes Opstandelse" is explicitly eschatological and should not be generalized into "in eternal life."
* "uforvisnelig Glæde og Salighed" has a strong biblical, almost Petrine resonance of what does not fade away. "Unfading joy and blessedness" preserves that force.
* The final poem’s "I Stormen ved hans Bryst jeg hviler" is intimate devotional imagery of resting at Christ’s breast in the storm. It should remain vivid and personal.
* "den deiligste af Adams Kjøn" means the fairest/noblest of Adam’s race. It combines christological beauty language with human solidarity.

King James or classic biblical equivalents useful here

* "weep with those who weep"
* "lay down his life for his friends"
* "the glory of God"
* "the resurrection of the dead"
* "Behold, how he loved him" may be too archaic because the instructions forbid archaizing pronouns and caution against inherited KJV tone, but the elevated biblical register can still inform cadence.

Expressions where literalism would obscure force

* "Tro kun, Maria; frygt ikke, Marta!" literally "Only believe, Mary; fear not, Martha!" This is understandable and powerful in English, so near-literal rendering is best.
* "tager sig af Engle" should not become merely "cares for angels" without note to the stronger sense of redemptive aid; in the final translation a balanced form such as "he does not take hold of angels, but of the offspring of Abraham" may be too alien for prose flow. A rendering like "he does not come to the aid of angels, but of the offspring of Abraham" preserves meaning better.
* "Menneskens Søn" should not be literally translated as "the Son of men." The biblical title "the Son of Man" is required.
* "sviger" in the hymn should not be rendered literally as "fails" in every instance if rhythm requires slight variation, but consistency is preferable because the poem turns on steadfastness.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Opening thesis: Four things are required for a friend to be a true and perfect friend in whom one may securely place the whole trust of the heart.
* First supporting movement: The four qualities are set out in order.
* * He must fully sympathize and understand the heart’s deepest need.
* * He must love so greatly that he is ready to give all, even his life.
* * He must have actual power to do what love requires.
* * His friendship must rest in the eternal and unchangeable with God.
* Christological identification: Such a friend is nowhere found among men in fullness; Jesus is such a friend, and the text reveals him as such.
* Polemical turn: Many have let Jesus recede into abstract dogma, so that he remains only a doctrine of atonement; this yields cold, joyless, forsaken Christianity.
* Corrective: Jesus is a personal friend to the one who flees to him in childlike faith.
* First major proof: Christ’s fellow-feeling is demonstrated from the Gospels broadly and then from Bethany specifically; his compassion extends from world-historical judgment to individual bodily need.
* Exegetical focus: "Jesus wept" reveals his true, deep, personal compassion.
* Scriptural reinforcement: Hebrews 2 confirms that because he shared our condition and suffered temptation/trial, he can help the tempted/tried.
* Second major proof: If he understands us so fully, he is also ready to give all for us. Bethany is read prospectively toward the Cross.
* Doctrinal deepening: In raising Lazarus, Jesus knowingly moves toward his own death; his love accepts that cost.
* Contrast with unbelief: Onlookers misread both his tears and his power because unbelief cannot understand love that does not preserve itself.
* Christological paradox: He has power, yet does not use it for self-advantage; the Son of God becomes man and dies for sinners.
* Pastoral application from miracle: Jesus does more than the sisters dare ask or understand; his love and power exceed both faith and fear.
* Third and fourth qualities integrated: Christ acts not independently but in obedient unity with the Father; his love is divine, rooted in God, and guided by God.
* Concluding exhortation: The hearer is directly summoned to seek, receive, and hold fast this friend in life and death.
* Final promise: In every sorrow and need Christ can relieve sorrow, heal the soul, and in the resurrection of the dead give eternal, unfading joy and blessedness.
* Final rhetorical transfer: "Mary, Martha, do you believe this?" becomes the question addressed to the present hearer.
* Hymnic seal: The poem gathers the sermon into personal confession and trust.

STEP 4: BIBLICAL ECHO IDENTIFICATION

* Main text: John 11:32-45, the raising of Lazarus.
* "He wept over Jerusalem" echoes Luke 19:41-44.
* Judgment on Jerusalem and the chosen people draws on Gospel prophecies of Jerusalem’s destruction, especially Matthew 23-24, Mark 13, Luke 21.
* Compassion for the hungry crowd who had been with him several days and had no food echoes the feeding narratives, especially Matthew 15:32 and Mark 8:2-3.
* The widow of Nain is Luke 7:11-15.
* "Jesus wept" is John 11:35.
* Hebrews 2:16-18 is cited directly.
* "No one has greater love than this, that one lay down his life for his friends" is John 15:13.
* "While we were enemies" is Romans 5:8-10, especially verse 8 and the surrounding reconciliation logic.
* Caiaphas and the counsel leading to Jesus’ death echo John 11:47-53, especially verse 51.
* "Others he helped; himself he cannot help" echoes the mockery at the Cross in Matthew 27:42 and Mark 15:31.
* "If you could believe, you would see the glory of God" from the sermon’s paraphrase reflects John 11:40.
* "Far beyond all that you dare ask or understand" echoes Ephesians 3:20, though phrased through the preacher’s diction.
* "He stands at the door of your heart and knocks" echoes Revelation 3:20.
* "The resurrection of the dead" is broad biblical language, strongly linked in this context to John 11:25 and the Christian resurrection hope generally.
* Flood imagery for overwhelming distress evokes Psalms such as Psalm 69 and other biblical flood metaphors.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* Warnings against cold dogmatic Christianity must remain sharp and sorrowful: "alas, what a cold, joyless, forsaken Christianity!"
* Christ’s compassion must remain intense: not mild empathy, but deep inward agitation, tears, and self-giving resolve.
* Unbelief must be named directly as unbelief, and heartlessness as heartlessness.
* The scandal of the incarnation and atoning death must remain offensive to unbelief, just as the source says.
* Pastoral imperatives must remain imperative: "hold fast," "fear nothing," "complain to him of all your distress," "count nothing too small."
* The final appeal must keep its probing directness.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* "kan" and "vil" in repeated pairings must remain distinct: Jesus both can and will help.
* "skulde" often expresses consequence or intended result and should not be weakened when it carries necessity within the argument.
* "burde blive sine Brødre lig" in the Hebrews citation should preserve obligation/fitting necessity: "it behooved him" or "he had to be made like his brothers." In the final prose, "he had to be made like his brothers in all things" preserves force without archaic excess.
* "maa faa komme ind" should remain "may come in," retaining the image of Christ’s offered entrance rather than presumptive intrusion.
* "skal lindre... og ... give dig" must remain promissory, not merely possible.
* "kunde ikke han..." in unbelieving speech must keep rhetorical challenge.

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

* Ven = friend
* Kjærlighed = love
* Troskyldighed = childlike trustful simplicity / childlike sincerity of faith (final wording should remain stable)
* Vantro = unbelief
* Guds Herlighed = the glory of God
* Fader = Father
* Guds Søn = the Son of God
* Marias Søn = Mary’s Son

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* The cup of bitterness and offense must remain a cup.
* The heart’s door and Christ knocking must remain literal imagery.
* Sorrows going over the soul like a flood must remain a flood image.
* Heart-wound must remain wound imagery: "their heart’s wound was healed."
* Living foundation for Christ’s love should remain architectural and alive.
* Resting at his breast in the storm in the hymn must remain bodily and intimate.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* The sermon moves from reflective doctrinal opening to warm christological consolation, then to sharp critique of unbelief, then back to promise and intimate exhortation.
* Severity appears in references to judgment, obstinacy, unbelief, heartlessness, and the scandal of the Cross.
* Consolation appears in Jesus’ tears, sympathy, help, and resurrection promise.
* Hope rises most strongly near the end and in the hymn. These tonal shifts must remain distinct.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Sentence length should often remain extended where argument and exhortation accumulate.
* Repetitions such as "Such a friend is he" should be kept for spoken cadence.
* Direct address should sound preachable aloud.
* Commas and semicolons should be used to preserve breath patterns and sermonic pacing.
* The poem should remain singable/recitable in cadence even if not metrically exact.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

A Faithful Friend in Distress and Death.

Sixteenth Sunday after Trinity.

John 11:32-45. When Mary now came where Jesus was, and saw him, she fell down at his feet and said to him, Lord, if you had been here, my brother would not have died. When Jesus now saw her weeping, and the Jews also weeping who had come with her, he was deeply moved in spirit and shaken, and said, Where have you laid him? They said to him, Lord, come and see. Jesus wept. Then said the Jews, See how he loved him! But some of them said, Could not he who opened the eyes of the blind man also have caused this man not to die? Then Jesus, again deeply moved, came to the tomb. Now it was a cave, and a stone lay upon it. Jesus said, Take away the stone. Martha, the sister of the dead man, says to him, Lord, he already stinks, for he has lain there four days. Jesus said to her, Did I not tell you that if you could believe, you would see the glory of God? Then they took away the stone where the dead man had been laid. But Jesus lifted up his eyes and said, Father, I thank you that you have heard me. Yet I knew that you always hear me; but for the sake of the people who stand around I said it, that they might believe that you have sent me. And when he had said this, he cried with a loud voice, Lazarus, come out here! And the dead man came out, bound hand and foot with grave-clothes, and his face was wrapped with a cloth. Jesus said to them, Loose him, and let him go. Then many of the Jews who had come to Mary, and had seen what Jesus did, believed in him.

Four things are required if a friend is to be a true, perfect friend, one in whom I may securely place the whole trust of my heart. And who would not wish to possess such a one? The first is that he can truly feel with me, that he can wholly and fully understand me in all the innermost need and want of my heart, in sorrow and in joy, in distress and in death. The second is that he loves me so highly that he is ready to give up everything, were it even his life, in order to help me and make me glad and blessed. The third is that he truly also has power to do what his love and faithful friendship bid him do, that, in other words, he not only gladly will, but also can. The fourth is that such friendship has its ground in that which is eternal and unchangeable with God.

Where among men is such a friend to be found? Jesus is such a friend, and reveals himself thus in our text.

For in the consciousness of many, Jesus has receded so far away into the depth of God’s power and eternity that for them he stands almost only as a doctrine of atonement. His personality vanishes into what is purely dogmatic. That may be altogether orthodox; but alas, what a cold, joyless, forsaken Christianity! A Christianity without the friend, the personal friend, Jesus!

For such he is to the one who flees to him in childlike trustful simplicity. If he is the Son of God, then he is also Mary’s Son, a human being like you and me. He wept over Jerusalem, and with unspeakable sorrow he both saw and foretold the dreadful judgment that would come upon David’s city and the chosen people, indeed upon the whole world, for the sake of obstinacy and unbelief. But the compassion of his heart was not confined to these great events of world history. He had care for the distress and need of each individual, in small things as in great. He had deep compassion on the people who had been with him for several days without getting food; he took thought that they should not perish on the way, and he fed them. He stopped when he saw the weeping widow in Nain, had deep compassion on her, and gave her back her son. And when he saw Mary weeping, and those who were with her weeping over the death of Lazarus, then it is written of him that he was deeply moved in spirit, indeed shaken, and that he also wept.

Jesus wept! Such a friend is he; you need not be ashamed or afraid to go to him with everything that lies upon your heart; for he both will and can feel with you; he can sing with the glad and weep with the sorrowing. Therefore it is written of him in Hebrews 2:16-18, that he does not come to the aid of angels, but of the offspring of Abraham, and that he had to be made like his brothers in all things, and since he has suffered and has himself been tempted, he can come to the help of those who are tempted.

But if he can so truly and deeply feel with us and understand us in all the movements of our heart, then he is also ready to give up all that is his in order to help us. That was what he did for Martha and Mary. "No one has greater love than this, that one lay down his life for his friends," he himself says; this rule he has followed for us all on the Cross, when we were enemies. How much more readily, then, does he not give all for his friends!

"Where have you laid him?" he asked; by then he had already taken counsel with his Father in Heaven and formed his decision as to what he would do. He knew that the raising of Lazarus would be like the last drop in the cup of bitterness and offense which his words and deeds had poured out for the leaders of the Jews. They would resolve upon his death, and the high priest Caiaphas would even adorn their evil deed with the hypocritical appearance of godliness. But nothing could hold Jesus back from helping his friends in their sorrow and distress. His heart was full; and as he followed them to the tomb, he wept, not only over sin and its painful consequences, death and sorrow, but also out of true and deep compassion for the mourners, whom he therefore also willed to help.

Then, as now, there were many kinds of astonishment at this, which pride and wisdom often call weakness, that Jesus wept. "See how he loved him," said some. It no doubt seemed strange to them that the great prophet, who had aroused attention and in part even stir throughout the whole land, could concern himself with such small things and in such a degree let himself be carried away by his personal feelings.

"Could not he who opened the blind man’s eyes also have caused this man not to die?" asked others.

These were the same who, when they had brought him to the Cross, cried out, "He helped others; himself he cannot help; let him come down from the Cross, and we will believe."

That is the speech of unbelief and heartlessness, which cannot understand that when a man has power, he should not use it for his own advantage. Love is to them a hidden and unknown thing. That Jesus had power to heal diseases and to raise the dead, and yet should not use it to spare himself sorrow and suffering, was to them wholly incomprehensible. That God’s own Son became man and died the death of scorn for sinners is to unbelief, to this very day, incomprehensible and offensive.

But his power, then as now, Jesus used to relieve and heal the sorrow of those who turned to him in trust. He did far more than they dared ask or understand. Such a friend is he. "Lord, if you had been here before, my brother would not have died," and, "he already stinks; he has now lain four days in the tomb," thus the two sisters spoke to their friend. His love and power were greater than both their trust and their fear. Only believe, Mary; do not fear, Martha! and you shall see the glory of God. Thus he went to the tomb and cried, "Lazarus, come out!" and he came out. The sisters received their brother again, and the wound of their hearts was healed. The love of Jesus had done it all. Such a friend is he.

But he did not do it apart from the will of the Father. His love was, like all true love, divine. It has its source and its rule in God. This same Jesus, who says of himself, "All power in Heaven and on Earth is given to me," goes as an obedient Son to the Father, asks him for everything, and does nothing apart from his will. "Father, I thank you that you have heard me," he cried, before he stepped to the tomb and raised the dead. Power and pride most often go together among men. Omnipotence and humility go side by side in the Son of Man and form the living foundation of his love. Such a friend is Jesus.

Are you seeking such a friend? Oh, here he is at the door of your heart; he knocks and asks whether he may come in and be for you what he was for Martha and Mary. Have you found him, oh, then hold fast to him in life and death, and fear nothing.

When sorrow and sickness and distress, all the painful consequences of sin, go like a flood over your soul, you have a fully faithful friend; keep near to him, speak with him childlike and confidently, complain to him of all your distress, count nothing too small to lay before him; he both can and will help far, far beyond what you might even dare to ask or understand; come like Martha with all your thoughts and all your doubts, come like Mary with all the singleness of your heart and your trustful spirit, he shall relieve your sorrow and heal your soul and, in the resurrection of the dead, give you an eternal and unfading joy and blessedness.

Mary, Martha, do you believe this?

"I know a friend who smiles on me
When I am set in sorrow and distress;
He fails me not, in life and death,
In the storm I rest upon his breast.
A friend so mighty and so fair,
The fairest of Adam’s race,
A friend who never fails."

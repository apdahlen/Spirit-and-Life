STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* Hvad der er umuligt for Menneskene er muligt for Gud: not merely “what people cannot do,” but a theological axiom concerning salvation, regeneration, and divine intervention.
* Attende Søndag efter Trefoldighed: liturgical dating; best rendered “Eighteenth Sunday after Trinity.”
* arve et evigt Liv: “inherit eternal life,” not merely “receive” or “get”; preserves biblical and doctrinal resonance.
* Hvi: archaic “why,” preserving scriptural register.
* bedrive Hor: specifically “commit adultery” or “fornication” depending context; here from commandment register, so “commit adultery.”
* slaa ihiel: archaic spelling of “slå ihjel,” “kill” / “murder”; in commandment context, “murder.”
* stiele: archaic “steal.”
* sige falsk Vidnesbyrd: formal commandment diction, “bear false witness.”
* besvige: older term meaning “defraud,” doctrinally and ethically weighty.
* ær din Fader og din Moder: commandment formula, “honor your father and your mother.”
* saae paa ham og elskede ham: spiritually charged; must remain simple and direct, not psychologized.
* En Ting fattes dig: “One thing you lack,” central refrain; must remain consistent.
* ilde tilmode: not merely “sad,” but “deeply troubled,” “distressed,” “sorrowful in spirit.”
* Gods: property, possessions, estate; “great possessions” in biblical idiom.
* vanskelig / saare vanskeligt: “difficult” / “exceedingly difficult,” not to be flattened into “hard.”
* forlade sig paa sin Rigdom: “trust in riches,” not simply “have wealth.”
* blive salig: major theological term; here “be saved,” not “be happy” or “be blessed.”
* Forjættelsen: “the Promise,” often singular and redemptive-historical, not casual “promise.”
* uddøde Moderliv: literally “withered/dead womb”; must preserve physical impossibility and biblical pathos.
* Kvindens Sæd: “the woman’s seed,” preserving Genesis 3:15 echo.
* knuse Slangens Hoved: “bruise/crush the serpent’s head”; “crush” best conveys triumph while retaining biblical force.
* Helligaand: “Holy Spirit.”
* overskygge dig: “overshadow you,” preserving Luke 1 diction.
* under Loven: “under the Law,” doctrinally Pauline.
* Forkrænkelighedens Vilkaar underkastet: subject to the conditions of corruptibility; must not lose incarnation emphasis.
* Kjød og Blod: “flesh and blood,” biblical idiom.
* Djævel: “the devil.”
* Misundheds Forjættelse: almost certainly a textual form for the promise concerning enmity in Genesis 3:15; should be handled cautiously in analysis and rendered by sense, not obscured.
* ny Natur / ny Fødsel: “new nature” / “new birth,” crucial regeneration terminology.
* delagtig i Guds Væsen: “partaker of God’s nature/being”; doctrinally delicate, likely echoing 2 Peter 1:4. “partaker of the divine nature” is the natural biblical equivalent.
* syndesuld Verden: literally sin-stained/sin-guilty world; should retain density of corruption.
* ei heller: “nor.”
* fattes og staar iveien: “is lacking and stands in the way”; spiritual obstruction motif.
* staar iveien for din Salighed: “stands in the way of your salvation.”
* Øverste: “ruler,” as in Luke 18:18.
* tilraads: “to seek counsel” / “for advice,” solemnly.
* Forargelse: in biblical diction, “offence” or “stumbling-block,” not mere annoyance.
* Mismod: dejection, despondency.
* udvortes Retfærdighed: “outward righteousness,” often legal or external morality.
* Haa bet om et udvortes herligt Messiasrige: expectation of an outward glorious Messianic kingdom.
* dunkelt bevidst: dimly conscious.
* overmaade forfærdede: “exceedingly amazed/terrified.”
* Staffels Disciple!: apostrophic address, likely “staggering/halting disciples” if taken metaphorically from “stave/stumble,” but uncertain as printed. Must preserve vocative sharpness while noting textual uncertainty.
* kjødelige jødiske Forestilling: “carnal Jewish conception,” sharp theological phrase that must not be softened.
* hernede / Hisset: “here below” / “there” (beyond, hereafter).
* tro indtil Enden: “faithful unto the end.”
* dygtig: “sufficient,” “fit,” “adequate.”
* hæfte og fange og fordærve: chained verbs of spiritual entanglement and ruin; rhythm must be preserved.
* ydmygen flye til Golgata: “humbly flee to Golgotha”; retain devotional urgency.
* gjøre vor Sjæl retfærdig for Gud: “make our soul righteous before God” or “justify our soul before God”; must preserve divine action and forensic force.
* neppe frelses: “scarcely be saved.”
* ugudelige: “ungodly.”
* komme frem: here not “come forth” literally, but “what will become of / where will [they] appear.” Biblical resonance from 1 Peter 4:18.
* med Gru: “with horror.”
* omstiftes til evig Pine: existence transformed into eternal torment; must preserve dread and eschatological seriousness.
* Faderhjertet: “the Father’s heart,” intimate but reverent.
* tornekronede Pande: “thorn-crowned brow.”
* Blodsstraaberne rinder: “drops/streams of blood run.”
* Ørkenen: “the wilderness,” with shepherding/redemptive overtones.
* hviske dig dette ene Ord ind: “whisper this one word into”; intimate pastoral image, to be preserved.
* himmelfaren: “ascended into heaven.”

Syntactic Hazards

* Extensive sermon-period sentences accumulate doctrine through clauses, dashes, and semicolons; these should not be broken into brisk modern English units unless clarity absolutely requires it.
* Scripture citation is embedded directly into argument rather than separately introduced; translation must keep this seamless movement.
* Refrains such as “En Ting fattes dig” and “hvad der er umuligt for Menneskene...” organize the discourse and must recur recognizably.
* Rhetorical questions drive exhortation and conscience-address; they must remain pointed and unsoftened.
* Antithetical structures—impossible for man / possible for God; cross / crown; here below / there—must stay formally visible.
* Vocative turns (“Ven,” “Broder og Søster!”) intensify oral address and should remain distinct.
* Parenthetical biblical references and asides often carry doctrinal weight and should not be treated as expendable ornament.
* Enumerations of temptations and obstacles build pressure through accumulation; their cadence should be preserved.

Theological Hazards

* The sermon’s theme is not divine omnipotence in abstraction, but omnipotence in service of salvation history and personal salvation.
* Abraham, Sarah, Mary, the incarnation, and regeneration are linked in one redemptive line; this structure must remain explicit.
* “The woman’s seed” is christological and protoevangelical, not merely figurative fertility language.
* Christ’s incarnation includes full participation in human weakness except sin; this must remain doctrinally precise.
* New birth is presented as humanly impossible and divinely wrought; any Pelagian or moralistic weakening would distort the sermon.
* The “one thing” lacking functions as the soul’s decisive idol, whether earthly or spiritual; this must remain existentially sharp.
* Riches are not condemned as matter, but trust in riches is exposed as excluding entrance into God’s kingdom.
* Cross-bearing is necessary discipleship, not optional spirituality.
* Salvation is insecure to presumption; perseverance “unto the end” remains essential in the sermonic logic.
* Final appeal joins terror of judgment with Christ’s pursuing mercy; tone balance is doctrinally central.

Voice Hazards

* The prose is solemn, exhortational, and pastoral, with a grave Lutheran-pietist pressure on the conscience.
* The voice repeatedly implicates the hearer directly; any detached academic tone in the final translation would fail.
* Severity and tenderness alternate without contradiction; both must remain.
* Biblical diction is frequent, but the translation should not slide into artificial Jacobean archaism.
* The author’s heat comes from inevitability, contrast, and consequence, not from flamboyant wording; translation must mirror that discipline.
* The sermon retains oral force; cadence, pauses, and emphatic repetitions are part of meaning.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* “arve et evigt Liv”
  Meaning: to inherit eternal life.
  Scriptural context: Mark 10:17 and parallels.
  Theological implication: eternal life is not earned possession but covenantal inheritance, though the hearer wrongly frames it as something to secure by doing.
  Natural English equivalent: “inherit eternal life.”
  Literal-risk note: rendering as “get eternal life” would flatten biblical and doctrinal nuance.

* “En Ting fattes dig”
  Meaning: one decisive lack remains.
  Scriptural context: Mark 10:21.
  Theological implication: salvation is not blocked by many deficiencies equally, but by the one reigning attachment that resists surrender to Christ.
  Natural English equivalent: “One thing you lack.”
  Literal-risk note: “you are missing one thing” is workable but weaker and less scriptural.

* “forlade sig paa sin Rigdom”
  Meaning: to rely upon, place confidence in, one’s riches.
  Scriptural context: Mark 10:24.
  Theological implication: the issue is not possession but trust; riches become an idol of security.
  Natural English equivalent: “trust in riches.”
  Literal-risk note: “depend on wealth” is acceptable but less biblical in resonance.

* “blive salig”
  Meaning: to be saved.
  Scriptural context: common older Scandinavian religious usage, often corresponding to salvation rather than blessedness alone.
  Theological implication: includes rescue from sin, judgment, and death, not merely spiritual well-being.
  Natural English equivalent: “be saved.”
  Literal-risk note: “be blessed” or “be happy” would be doctrinally false here.

* “Kvindens Sæd”
  Meaning: the woman’s seed.
  Scriptural context: Genesis 3:15.
  Theological implication: messianic promise fulfilled in Christ.
  Natural English equivalent: “the woman’s seed.”
  KJV note: KJV offers the natural equivalent here.
  Literal-risk note: explanatory paraphrase would weaken the sermonic biblical echo.

* “under Loven”
  Meaning: under the Law.
  Scriptural context: Galatians 4:4.
  Theological implication: Christ entered the human condition under the Law’s demands to redeem those under it.
  Natural English equivalent: “under the Law.”

* “af samme Kjød og Blod som du og jeg, og dog en anden Slægt”
  Meaning: of the same flesh and blood as we are, yet of another stock/order.
  Scriptural context: Hebrews 2 echoes for “flesh and blood,” with christological distinction added.
  Theological implication: true humanity without sin.
  Literal-risk note: “another race” would sound biologized and misleading; “of another order” or “yet of another kind” better preserves theological intent.

* “ny Fødsel”
  Meaning: new birth.
  Scriptural context: John 3.
  Theological implication: regeneration as divine act, not moral reform.
  Natural English equivalent: “new birth” or “be born anew.”
  KJV note: KJV/New Testament diction can support the resonance, but pronouns should remain modern.

* “delagtig i Guds Væsen”
  Meaning: sharing in God’s being/nature.
  Scriptural context: likely 2 Peter 1:4 (“partakers of the divine nature”).
  Theological implication: not ontological deification in a speculative sense, but real participation in divine life through regeneration.
  Natural English equivalent: “partaker of the divine nature.”
  Literal-risk note: “share in God’s essence” would sound too metaphysical and potentially misleading.

* “udvortes Retfærdighed”
  Meaning: external righteousness.
  Scriptural context: broad biblical and Lutheran critique of pharisaic/legal righteousness.
  Theological implication: respectable morality without surrender to Christ.
  Literal-risk note: “visible justice” would miss the point.

* “Korset bliver en Forargelse”
  Meaning: the cross becomes an offence/stumbling-block.
  Scriptural context: Pauline and gospel idiom.
  Theological implication: suffering, humiliation, and self-denial attached to Christ repel the natural heart.
  Natural English equivalent: “the cross becomes an offence” or “a stumbling-block.”
  Literal-risk note: “annoyance” would be wholly inadequate.

* “gribe Kronen” over against “forkaste Korset”
  Meaning: seize the crown while rejecting the cross.
  Scriptural context: not a direct quotation, but classic Christian contrast of glory and suffering.
  Theological implication: desire for triumph without discipleship.
  Literal-risk note: must remain metaphorical and sharp.

* “gjennemborer det med Dødens Braad”
  Meaning: pierces it with the sting of death.
  Scriptural context: 1 Corinthians 15:55–56 echo in “death’s sting.”
  Theological implication: small attachments become spiritually deadly.
  Natural English equivalent: “pierces it with death’s sting.”

* “tro indtil Enden”
  Meaning: faithful unto the end.
  Scriptural context: Revelation 2:10 / persevering discipleship motifs.
  Theological implication: perseverance is necessary; the sermon does not permit complacency.
  Natural English equivalent: “faithful unto the end.”

* “ydmygen flye til Golgata”
  Meaning: humbly flee to Golgotha.
  Scriptural context: devotional synthesis rather than direct quotation.
  Theological implication: return in repentance to Christ crucified as sole refuge.
  Literal-risk note: should not be weakened to “go back to” or “remember.”

* “den retfærdige neppe frelses, hvor vil Synderen og den ugudelige komme frem?”
  Meaning: if the righteous scarcely is saved, where shall the sinner and the ungodly appear?
  Scriptural context: 1 Peter 4:18.
  Theological implication: heightens fear of judgment and strips away complacency.
  KJV note: KJV gives a very natural English equivalent, but modern pronouns should be retained if adapted.
  Literal-risk note: “how will they manage?” is too weak.

* “han staar ved dit Hjertes Dør, han banker paa”
  Meaning: he stands at the door of your heart and knocks.
  Scriptural context: Revelation 3:20, adapted through devotional usage.
  Theological implication: Christ’s seeking mercy addresses even the despairing sinner.
  Natural English equivalent: “he stands at your heart’s door and knocks.”

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Thesis
  What is impossible for human beings is possible for God, and God does the impossible not merely to display omnipotence but to save human beings and reveal His love.

* First supporting movement
  Salvation history proves this principle:

* Sarah’s barren womb and Abraham’s faith show God accomplishing what human nature cannot.

* The promise to Abraham is linked to the blessing of all nations and to the promise of the woman’s seed.

* Second supporting movement
  The incarnation is an even greater instance of the impossible made actual:

* The virgin birth.

* The Son of God born of a woman, under the Law.

* Full humanity without sin.

* His saving work against sin, death, and the devil.

* Third supporting movement
  The sermon pivots from salvation history to personal regeneration:

* The new birth is, from the human side, more impossible than all the preceding miracles.

* Nicodemus voices the natural objection.

* Only God can cause one to be born of God.

* Fourth supporting movement
  Application through the rich young ruler:

* “One thing you lack” becomes the universal diagnosis.

* One clung-to thing creates an impassable wall between soul and God.

* The hearer is directly challenged to identify that one thing.

* Fifth supporting movement
  Case analysis of the rich ruler:

* He was earnest, morally serious, and sincere.

* Yet he turned away because he could not relinquish the one thing.

* Therefore zeal, morality, and sincerity are insufficient where surrender is absent.

* Sixth supporting movement
  Broader warning:

* Many still turn from Christ because of the offence of the cross.

* Even outward righteousness can become a retreat from true discipleship.

* A seemingly small obstacle can exclude one from God.

* Seventh supporting movement
  Warning to believers:

* God’s children also face the danger of rejecting the cross to grasp the crown.

* Small attachments, earthly or spiritual, can ensnare and ruin the soul.

* Eighth supporting movement
  Interpretation of the disciples’ fear:

* They still cherished hopes of an outward Messianic kingdom.

* Jesus exposes the impossibility of salvation where trust in riches remains.

* Their question, “Who then can be saved?” is expanded into the believer’s continuing question.

* Ninth supporting movement
  Evangelical resolution:

* Christ’s answer remains: impossible for man, possible for God.

* The proper response is humble flight to Golgotha and total surrender into Christ’s hands.

* God alone can make the soul righteous before Him.

* Tenth supporting movement
  Final dual appeal:

* Warning from 1 Peter 4:18 intensifies the peril.

* Direct address to the brother or sister who turned away from Jesus.

* The hearer is told to contemplate the abyss of eternal torment.

* Then comes the tender gospel reversal: Christ seeks, knocks, pursues, and speaks the saving word.

* Final christological conclusion: the incarnate, crucified, risen, ascended Savior surely can save.

STEP 4: BIBLICAL ECHO IDENTIFICATION

* Mark 10:17–27 is the governing sermon text.
* Romans 1:20: God’s power revealed in the created order.
* Genesis 18 / 21: Sarah’s laughter and the birth of Isaac.
* Genesis 12:3 / 22:18: in Abraham’s seed all families/nations of the earth shall be blessed.
* Genesis 3:15: the woman’s seed shall crush the serpent’s head.
* Luke 1:35: “The Holy Spirit shall come upon you...”
* Galatians 4:4: born of a woman, under the Law.
* Hebrews 2:14–17 echo: sharing flesh and blood.
* John 3:4: Nicodemus asks how one can be born when old.
* John 1:13: born not of blood, nor of the will of the flesh, nor of the will of man, but of God.
* 2 Peter 1:4 likely echoed in “partaker of the divine nature.”
* 1 Corinthians 15:55–56 echoed in “death’s sting.”
* Revelation 2:10 echoed in “faithful unto the end.”
* 1 Peter 4:18 quoted in substance: if the righteous scarcely is saved...
* Revelation 3:20 echoed in “he stands at your heart’s door and knocks.”
* Luke 15 may echo in the image of Christ going out to seek the lost.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* Warnings must remain warnings: no softening of spiritual danger, self-deception, or eternal punishment.
* Rebukes must preserve directness, especially where the hearer’s idol is named as the one thing that blocks salvation.
* Consolation must remain strong but not sentimental: Christ’s mercy comes as active, seeking, blood-bought mercy.
* The rhetorical heat should arise from doctrine applied to the conscience, not from inflated language.
* Direct addresses (“Friend,” “Brother and Sister!”) must retain pastoral immediacy.
* The final appeal must preserve both dread and hope in full contrast.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* “skal” in scripture quotations and doctrinal statements should usually remain “shall” where this carries necessary force, especially in biblical promises and commands.
* “kan” / “kunde” in impossibility contexts must often remain “can/could,” since the sermon hinges on incapacity.
* “maa” equivalents of necessity should not be weakened to preference.
* “bør” where present in similar texts would require “ought,” but this passage relies more on stronger forms of necessity and impossibility.
* “kan ei” type force, where represented by impossible constructions, must remain categorical.
* “kun” in “kun en Ting hernede, som er sikker: Korset” must retain exclusivity: “only one thing here below is certain: the cross.”

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

* evigt Liv = eternal life
* Guds Rige = the kingdom of God
* salig / Salighed = saved / salvation
* Forjættelsen = the Promise (when redemptive-historical), promise when context is less formal
* Kvindens Sæd = the woman’s seed
* Helligaand = Holy Spirit
* under Loven = under the Law
* Synd / syndig = sin / sinful
* ny Fødsel = new birth
* Guds Barn / Guds Børn = child of God / children of God
* delagtig i Guds Væsen = partaker of the divine nature
* Korset = the cross
* Kronen = the crown
* retfærdig for Gud = righteous before God
* den ugudelige = the ungodly
* Faderen i Himmelen = the Father in heaven
* Frelseren = the Savior

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* “the woman’s seed” must remain a living biblical image.
* “an impassable wall” between soul and God must remain concrete.
* “a little stone in their way became a mountain” must not be paraphrased away.
* “clings like a thorn to their hearts and pierces it with death’s sting” must stay vivid.
* “reject the cross and seize the crown” must remain concise and antithetical.
* “look down into the abyss” and “the Father’s heart” must remain imagistic.
* “from whose thorn-crowned brow the drops of blood run” is central to the final Christ-image and must remain visually immediate.
* “he stands at your heart’s door and knocks” must retain the embodied pastoral image.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* Historical narration of Abraham and Mary is reverent and declarative.
* The transition to regeneration becomes doctrinally intense and contemplative.
* The address to the rich ruler turns diagnostic and searching.
* The warnings to present hearers become severe and urgent.
* The interpretation of the disciples includes sympathetic exposure of their delusion.
* The turn to Golgotha is penitential and evangelical.
* The final paragraph moves from threat, to abyss, to tender pursuit by Christ, to triumphant confidence in His saving power.
* These shifts must remain distinct; no flattening into one uniform tone.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Preserve long lines of reasoning where clauses accumulate pressure.
* Use semicolons and commas to sustain sermonic breath.
* Retain repeated openings such as “And,” “But,” “Now,” “Therefore,” where they carry oral momentum.
* Keep vocatives and questions in positions of emphasis.
* Preserve the alternation between exposition and direct address.
* Avoid clipped contemporary sentence rhythms that would remove the sense of proclamation.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

What Is Impossible for Men
is Possible for God.

Eighteenth Sunday after Trinity.

—

Mark 10:17–27. And as He had gone forth onto the road, one came running and fell on his knees before Him and asked Him: Good Master! What shall I do, that I may inherit eternal life? But Jesus said to him: Why do you call Me good? None is good save One, that is, God. You know the commandments: You shall not commit adultery, you shall not murder, you shall not steal, you shall not bear false witness, you shall not defraud, honor your father and your mother! But he answered and said to Him: Master! All these have I kept from my youth. But Jesus looked upon him and loved him, and said to him: One thing you lack: go away, sell what you have, and give it to the poor, and you shall have a treasure in heaven; then come, follow Me, and take up the cross! But he was troubled at that saying and went away sorrowful; for he had great possessions. And Jesus looked around and said to His disciples: How difficult it shall be for those who have riches to enter into the kingdom of God! But the disciples were astonished at His words. But Jesus answered again and said to them: Children! How difficult it is for those who trust in their riches to enter into the kingdom of God! It is easier for a camel to go through a needle’s eye than for a rich man to enter into the kingdom of God. But they were exceedingly astonished, and said among themselves: Who then can be saved? But Jesus looked upon them and said: For men it is impossible, but not for God; for all things are possible for God.

When God does the impossible, it is not in order to reveal His omnipotence, — for that He does also through the regular order of nature (Rom. 1:20), — but in order to save men, and to reveal His love.

Sarah laughed when God promised Abraham offspring by her; Abraham himself, on the other hand, looked neither to his old age nor to Sarah’s dead womb, but in childlike faith held fast to the Promise, that in his seed all the families of the earth should be blessed; and as Abraham believed, so it came to pass; what was impossible for men, that God did; He gave Sarah a son, and thus initiated the fulfillment of the great Promise: “The woman’s seed shall crush the serpent’s head.”

For the sake of this same cause God did what, so to speak, is yet more impossible for men: He caused a Virgin to bear a Child without knowing man. “The Holy Spirit shall come upon you, and the power of the Most High shall overshadow you; therefore also that Holy One who is born of you shall be called the Son of God.” That was the angel’s message to Mary, and according to this word God’s own Son was born of a woman, under the Law, subjected to all the conditions of corruptibility, of the same flesh and blood as you and I, and yet of another order, a Man without sin, who could die for us, overcome death, and deliver us from sin and death and the devil, in order that all God’s promise of enmity should be fulfilled in the woman’s seed, the Son of God and of Mary.

God’s love does not stop here; from this point of departure it so to speak only then begins truly to reveal its power, which, as the Word says, is stronger than death. There is one thing which, if one may so express it, is a thousandfold more impossible than anything else: that a sinful, corruptible human child should be able to receive a new nature, undergo a new birth, and thereby become a child of God, a partaker of the divine nature, holy and incorruptible in the midst of a world stained by sin and in a corruptible body. With good reason Nicodemus could ask: “How can a man be born when he is old? Can he enter a second time into his mother’s womb and be born?” And would that very many might ask the question with the same seriousness as Nicodemus, and not give it up until they had received an answer from God, until they had been born “not of blood, nor of the will of the flesh, nor of the will of man, but of God,” and had truly learned by experience that “what is impossible for men is possible for God.”

For “one thing you still lack,” the Lord says to the rich young man, one thing you lack; it is this one thing that makes it impossible for a man to inherit eternal life. One thing you trust in, be it never so slight, never so corruptible, — that sets an impassable wall between you and your God, between your soul and eternal life.

Friend, have you found in yourself this one thing which is lacking and stands in the way of your salvation?

“Good Master, what shall I do, that I may inherit eternal life?” cries out the rich young man.

He had striven so honestly and earnestly; the commandments he had kept from his youth, — how many can say the same? — and yet he was no nearer. Now he came, surely after much inward struggle, — for he was a ruler (Luke 18:18), — to the despised Nazarene, who was even then on the way to His suffering, and asked His counsel. That he also asked in all sincerity seems to follow from what is said of Jesus: “He looked upon him and loved him.” And yet, when he received this answer: “One thing you still lack; sell what you have, and give it to the poor; then come and follow Me and take up the cross!” then he went away sorrowful, and it is added: “For he had great possessions.”

This man lacked neither earnest striving nor a sincere desire to be saved; he wanted so gladly to be saved, but he could not; there was one thing he could not give up; therefore he could not follow Jesus and take up the cross either.

Alas, how many are there not still to this very day for whom the cross thus becomes an offence, and who in despondency turn away from Jesus, if not to the world’s frivolity and vices, then at least to its outward righteousness! A little stone in their path became a mountain and shut them out from God.

And God’s children are daily exposed to the same danger: to forget and cast away the cross in order at once to seize the crown. A seemingly little thing comes into their way, clings like a thorn to their hearts, and pierces them with death’s sting.

Therefore Jesus looked around upon His disciples when the young man went away. He knew what thoughts were stirring in their hearts. The hope of an outward glorious Messianic kingdom still had power over their souls. Is it not possible, then, to be rich in the kingdom of God? Is not the kingdom of God a kingdom of riches and power? Thus they thought, more or less dimly consciously, and were therefore also astonished when Jesus told them that it is exceedingly difficult for a rich man to enter into the kingdom of God. And when He both explained and sharpened this word to mean that rich in this sense is the one who trusts in his riches, and that it is just as impossible for such a one to enter into the kingdom of God as for a camel to go through a needle’s eye, — then, it is written, they became even more exceedingly astonished, and asked one another: “Who then can be saved?”

Staggering disciples! As Peter said, they had left all to follow Jesus (v. 28), in order thereby to attain the glory of the Messianic kingdom according to the carnal Jewish conception they had of it.

Now it was as though everything had been torn from their hands. Jesus was to suffer the reproach of the cross. The disciples would indeed also have earthly things in the world, but all under persecution, and besides this “many who were first shall be last” (vv. 30, 31). Even for the Lord’s disciples, then, only one thing here below is certain: the cross. There, it is eternal life, that is true, but only for the one who remains faithful unto the end. And who is sufficient for that? When the one thing, sometimes of a purely earthly sort, such as possessions, honor, influence, family, fleshly desires and thoughts, — just as often of a spiritual sort, such as anger, envy, hatred, sloth, esteem as a good Christian, yes, even inward vanity over one’s Christian strength, — can hinder and bind and capture and destroy the soul of a child of God, oh, how must we not, like the disciples, be filled with fear and ask: “who then can be saved?” and anew humbly flee to Golgotha and surrender ourselves wholly and altogether into His hands, — blessed be His name forever! — for whom that is possible which is impossible for us poor men: to make our soul righteous before God!

But if the righteous is scarcely saved, where shall the sinner and the ungodly appear?

Brother and Sister! Have you, with the rich young man, turned away from Jesus because His cross frightened you, and because He required of you the one thing which it was impossible for you to give up? Where then are you going? Think seriously and simply about this question: Where does it lead? And when you are finished, when with horror you have looked down into the abyss where your existence will be transformed into eternal torment, and in anguish begin to look back toward the Father in heaven and see an impassable wall between yourself and the Father’s heart, oh, then look to Him from whose thorn-crowned brow the drops of blood are running; see, He goes out into the wilderness precisely to seek you, He does not wait until you come to Him, He seeks you, He stands at your heart’s door, He knocks, He will come in and whisper this one word into your despairing soul:

“What is impossible for men is possible for God.”

Look to Him, the Savior, born of Abraham’s seed, of a Virgin, dead, risen, ascended into heaven! Can He not save?

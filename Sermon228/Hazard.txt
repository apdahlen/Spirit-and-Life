STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* Arven: not merely “inheritance” in a casual legal sense, but the covenantal inheritance promised by God; bound up with sonship, promise, and eschatological possession.
* Frugten / bære Frugt: not generic productivity, but spiritual fruit owed to God; carries strong biblical and judgmental force.
* Stenen / Hovedhjørnesteen / Anstødssten: doctrinally loaded biblical stone imagery; must preserve intertextual resonance from Psalms, Isaiah, and the New Testament.
* kjødelig Hovmod: “fleshly pride” or “carnal pride,” not merely arrogance; implies fallen human self-regard opposed to faith.
* Forsængelighed: older form suggesting worldly vanity, self-delusion, or corruptibility; contextually linked to spiritual blindness and pride.
* Forjettelse / Forjettelserne: “promise” in the biblical-covenantal sense, not mere prediction or assurance.
* Udkaarelse: election, chosenness, filial adoption; here specifically “the filial election” or “adoption as sons” in the Son.
* Barnekaar: condition or status of being a child before God; must retain theological force of filial standing.
* Egenretfærdighed: self-righteousness in the doctrinal sense, especially over against grace and faith.
* Bod: repentance in the older penitential sense, not merely “amendment” or “penance” in a narrow sacramental sense.
* sønderknust Hjerte: “broken” or “contrite” heart; strong biblical diction is needed.
* Samvittighed: conscience, but with moral and spiritual accusatory depth.
* Helligdom: sanctuary; may also imply the holy presence of God in typological relation to Christ.
* forgribe sig paa Sønnen: not merely “lay hands on,” but violently transgress against the Son.
* undse sig for min Søn: to feel shame before, to have reverence enough to refrain before; must not be flattened.
* ilde ødelægge de onde: emphatic judicial destruction; preserve severity.
* sønderstødes / sønderknuse: distinct verbs of shattering and crushing; the escalation should remain visible.
* mærket og født ved Daabens hellige Vand: baptismal marking and new birth; doctrinally charged sacramental language.
* afhugges: cut off; a strong Pauline warning that must retain covenantal and eschatological seriousness.

Syntactic Hazards

* Long sermonic periods accumulate doctrinal reasoning clause by clause; they should not be broken into short modern sentences where the cumulative force matters.
* Repeated semicolon pivots organize the argument morally and logically; they must be preserved where possible.
* Scriptural quotations are embedded into the author’s prose and argument; they should remain integrated rather than isolated as modern block quotations.
* The pattern “not this, but that” is central to the theological argument and should remain sharply balanced.
* Enumerative structure governs the sermon: three neglected things, then first, second, and third. This logical scaffolding must remain explicit.
* Rhetorical questions are accusatory and pastoral, not informational. They address the conscience directly and must remain pointed.
* Shifts from exposition of Israel to direct address to present hearers are sudden and deliberate; this homiletic turn must remain forceful.

Theological Hazards

* The contrast between outward descent from Abraham and true spiritual descent by faith is central and must remain doctrinally precise.
* Grace and promise are set over against law, claim, descent, and outward works; weakening this contrast would distort the sermon.
* Inheritance belongs to children and therefore presupposes new birth, faith, and life from God.
* Fruitlessness is not presented as a minor defect but as proof of spiritual death and ground of judgment.
* Christ as Son, Heir, cornerstone, sanctuary, and stone of offense must remain typologically and christologically clear.
* The sermon moves from Israel’s judgment to a warning to the baptized church; this covenantal transfer and warning must not be softened.
* Baptism is treated sacramentally and covenantally, yet with a warning against fruitless presumption; both aspects must be retained.
* Repentance, brokenness of heart, grace, and contrition are presented as the only acceptable response before God.

Voice Hazards

* The tone is solemn, judicial, exhortational, and pastorally severe. Any smoothing into neutral academic prose would destroy the voice.
* The preacher’s moral pressure comes from consequence and inevitability, not emotional flourish; English must preserve that pressure.
* Direct address such as “Brødre og Søster!” and “Ven” must retain intimacy and seriousness without sounding sentimental.
* The sermon’s cadence belongs to proclamation; flattening repetitions or tightening the prose too much would erase its oral force.
* Warning passages must remain uncompromising; the author does not mitigate the danger for nominal believers.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* “Arven” carries covenantal and filial meaning. In this sermon it refers not only to land but to the whole promise of God fulfilled in Christ. “Inheritance” is the natural rendering, but it must always be heard in relation to sonship and promise.
* “Arvingen selv” refers to Christ as the Heir Himself. This must be preserved because the sermon turns on the irony that those without true sonship try to seize the inheritance by murdering the Son.
* “Gives alene til Børnene” reflects Pauline logic: inheritance is given, not seized; it belongs to children. This is a doctrinal statement, not a sentimental one.
* “Gud kan opvække den Slags Abrahams Afkom af Stene” echoes the preaching of John the Baptist and Christ that God can raise up children to Abraham from stones. The force is anti-presumption: biological descent gives no claim before God.
* “Et Kjødhjerte istedetfor Stenhjertet” echoes Ezekiel’s language of the heart of flesh replacing the heart of stone. Literal rendering is preferable because the metaphor is scriptural and doctrinally weighty.
* “Bære Frugt for Gud” is biblical covenant language. It means producing the life and obedience God seeks. It should not be paraphrased as “live well” or “do good things.”
* “Herrens kjæreste Plantning” carries Isaiah’s vineyard song into the sermon. “Dearest planting” is possible, though slightly formal; “beloved planting” may read more naturally while preserving scriptural dignity.
* “Maalet fuldt” means the measure was full, that judgment had reached completion. This is a biblical-judicial idiom and should be translated as such rather than colloquially.
* “Anstødssten” and “Forargelsens Klippe” echo biblical language of stumbling and offense. “Stone of stumbling” and “rock of offense” are natural English equivalents and preserve scriptural resonance.
* “Byggede sit Egenretfærdigheds Huus paa Sand” is more than a figure of bad construction; it joins the house-on-sand image to self-righteousness. The metaphor must remain intact.
* “Bød Naade for Bod og et sønderknust Hjerte” means that Christ offered grace in return for repentance and a broken heart, or offered grace only for repentance and contrition. The construction is slightly compressed, but the theological sense is that grace is offered not to self-righteousness but to the repentant. This must be rendered clearly without flattening the tension.
* “Hang ham paa Forbandelsens Træ” echoes apostolic language about Christ being hanged on the tree and bearing the curse. “Tree of the curse” preserves the scriptural force better than a more modern paraphrase.
* “Mærket og født ved Daabens hellige Vand” contains baptismal sign and rebirth imagery. It should remain sacramental and concrete.
* “Sat paa Hovedhjørnesteinen” likely means founded or set upon the cornerstone. It should preserve the architectural metaphor and the stability implied by Christ as foundation.
* “Se derfor Guds Godhed og Strenghed” is Pauline admonition. “Behold” would sound overly archaic; “See therefore the goodness and severity of God” preserves both gravity and scriptural familiarity. The KJV offers “goodness and severity,” which is the natural equivalent here.
* “Vær ikke hovmodig, men frygt!” also echoes Pauline admonition. The plain force should remain: “Do not be high-minded, but fear!” or more idiomatically, “Do not be proud, but fear!” The second is clearer; the first is more biblical in cadence.

King James-adjacent equivalents that are natural in English without using archaic pronouns

* “stone of stumbling”
* “rock of offense”
* “cornerstone” / “chief cornerstone”
* “goodness and severity”
* “broken heart” or “contrite heart”
* “the measure was full”
* “cut off”
* “children of Abraham” / “seed of Abraham” depending on context

Expressions where literal translation could obscure force

* “undse sig for min Søn” should not become a weak modern phrase like “be embarrassed in front of my Son.” The intended sense is reverential restraint: “They will surely have regard for my Son.”
* “ilde ødelægge de onde” needs strong judicial force. A literal “badly destroy the evil ones” is awkward and weak; “He will miserably destroy those wicked men” or “He will bring those wicked men to a wretched end” preserves the force.
* “forgribe sig paa Sønnen” must not be softened to “mistreat the Son.” It implies violent outrage against him.
* “bød Naade for Bod” cannot be rendered too literally if that obscures the doctrinal point. It must communicate that grace is offered only along the way of repentance and brokenness, not self-righteous claim.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Thesis: Christ’s parable speaks severe words of judgment upon Israel because there were three things they failed to heed in fleshly pride and vanity.
* First movement: The inheritance.
* * The promises to Israel centered on inheritance.
* * The land of Canaan was only the outward and earthly part.
* * The deeper inheritance included the whole world, divine glory, worship, the Word, and above all filial election in the Son, the Messiah.
* * Paul clarifies that this inheritance is not by the law but by the righteousness of faith and therefore by grace.
* * Israel forgot this and claimed the inheritance by descent and outward works rather than by faith and new birth.
* * Therefore they had no true inheritance-right, because without the new heart there is no life, and without life no fruit for God.
* Second movement: The fruit.
* * Israel also forgot the necessity of bearing fruit for God.
* * Isaiah’s vineyard song proves that despite God’s care the vineyard yielded wild grapes.
* * The fig tree imagery intensifies the accusation: year after year no fruit was found.
* * Even after repeated intercession and cultivation, there was only leaf and no fruit.
* * Therefore judgment had to come: the kingdom would be taken away and given to a people who would bear its fruit.
* Third movement: The stone.
* * Israel also failed to heed the stone, which was both cornerstone and stone of stumbling.
* * Because they lacked life, fruit, and sonship, they tried to seize the inheritance and assault the Son.
* * They rejected the prophetic word about the foundation stone in Zion.
* * Entrusted with God’s house for edification, they rejected the cornerstone and built their self-righteous house on sand.
* * They stumbled at Christ because his lowliness judged their conscience and rejected their righteousness, offering grace only for repentance and a broken heart.
* * Thus the prophecies of Isaiah were fulfilled in them.
* Climax: In killing Christ they sealed their judgment. When the risen Christ sent out the gospel to other peoples, the stone fell on temple and people; judgment was completed.
* Homiletic turn to the hearers: Those who are in Christ are Abraham’s seed and heirs according to the promise; the baptized are addressed as present heirs, called to bear fruit and founded upon the cornerstone.
* Final warning: If the baptized do not bear fruit, the inheritance will be taken from them and the despised stone will crush them.
* Intensification: Their judgment will be worse than Israel’s if they presume upon grace, because Paul warns that God did not spare the natural branches.
* Conclusion: The hearers are summoned to fear, repentance, and self-examination. The sermon ends in direct, searching questions: Where is the fruit of the Kingdom among us? Do you have fruit?

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* The sermon must sound like a preached judgment-word, not an explanatory essay.
* Accusations against Israel must retain their severity, because they prepare the warning addressed to the baptized church.
* The transition to “Brothers and sisters” and then “Friend” must preserve pastoral nearness without reducing the seriousness.
* The closing questions must remain stark, brief, and searching.
* The English should let the force arise from doctrinal inevitability: no life, therefore no fruit; no fruit, therefore no inheritance; rejection of the Son, therefore judgment.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* “skal” in judgment contexts should usually remain “shall,” especially where consequence and certainty are foregrounded.
* “maa” often carries necessity, not mere possibility. In “Dommen maatte komme,” “judgment had to come” is required.
* “kan ei” or equivalent negatives must remain strong impossibility where applicable.
* “behøves” should be rendered as “is required” or “is needed,” not weakened to “helps” or “is useful.”
* “vilde bære dets Frugt” in prophetic/judgment context should be “would bear its fruit,” retaining the certainty within reported speech.

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* Vineyard, fig tree, fruit, branches, stone, cornerstone, house, heart of stone, heart of flesh, tree of the curse, and temple must all remain as living metaphors.
* These images carry the sermon’s theology. They should not be converted into abstract explanation.
* The movement from inheritance to fruit to stone is itself metaphorically structured and should remain visible.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* Expository covenant history at the beginning gives way to severe indictment.
* The section on fruitlessness heightens lament and judgment.
* The stone section intensifies into christological rejection and final destruction.
* The address to the baptized begins with privilege and promise, then sharply turns into warning.
* The closing combines severity with urgent pastoral appeal to repentance and self-examination.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Preserve sentence length where the preacher gathers force through accumulation.
* Use semicolons and commas to sustain breath and sermonic flow.
* Let scriptural phrases fall with recognizable rhythm.
* Keep the final admonitions and questions short and direct for oral impact.
* Avoid over-polishing transitions; some abruptness is part of the preached effect.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

The Inheritance, the Fruit, and the Stone.

Twentieth Sunday after Trinity.

Matthew 21:33-44. Hear another parable: There was a householder, who planted a vineyard, and made a hedge around it, and dug a winepress in it, and built a tower, and let it out to husbandmen, and went into a far country. But when the time of the fruit drew near, he sent his servants to the husbandmen to receive its fruits. And the husbandmen took his servants; one they beat, another they killed, another they stoned. Again he sent other servants, more than the first, and they did likewise to them. But at last he sent his Son to them and said: They will surely have regard for my Son. But when the husbandmen saw the Son, they said among themselves: This is the Heir; come, let us kill him and seize upon his inheritance! And they took him and cast him out of the vineyard and killed him. When now the lord of the vineyard comes, what shall he do to those husbandmen? They said to him: He shall miserably destroy those wicked men, and let out the vineyard to other husbandmen, who shall give him the fruits in their seasons. Jesus said to them: Have you never read in the Scriptures: The stone which the builders rejected, the same has become the head of the corner; this is the Lord’s doing, and it is marvelous in our eyes? Therefore I say to you, the Kingdom of God shall be taken from you and given to a people that bears its fruits. And whoever falls upon this stone shall be broken to pieces; but the one upon whom it falls, him it shall crush.

These are severe words of judgment that the Lord in this parable speaks to the people of Israel, and that because there were three things to which, in fleshly pride and vanity, they had not given heed.

The first is the inheritance.

All God’s promise to Israel turned upon the inheritance. At the Lord’s command Abraham had gone out from his home and his people into a strange land, to which otherwise he had no right, but concerning which God said to him: “To your seed will I give this land” (Gen. 12:7). Thus the land of Canaan became the inheritance of the people of Israel.

But this was only one part, and indeed only the outward and earthly part, of the inheritance which the Lord gave Abraham and his seed to possess through the covenant and the promises. Through it there was also promised the whole world, the glory and the giving of the Law and the worship of God and the Word, and above all things the filial election in the Son himself, the Messiah, the Savior of the world, the goal and end of all the promises, the Heir himself, to whom the Father had given the Gentiles for an inheritance (Ps. 2), and whose day Abraham saw and rejoiced.

Therefore the Lord had also said to Abraham: “In you shall all the families of the earth be blessed,” and Paul expresses it thus: that Abraham and his seed received the promise that they should inherit the world (Rom. 4:13). But he also adds: “Not through the Law, but through the righteousness of faith,” and still further he says (Rom. 4:16), “that the inheritance is by faith, in order that it may be by grace, and that the promise may stand fast.”

This was precisely what the people of Israel had forgotten. They thought that because in an outward and fleshly sense they were descended from Abraham, therefore they had the right of inheritance, although, as John and Jesus say, God can raise up children of Abraham of that sort from stones. They passed over faith, without which no one can become Abraham’s true spiritual seed, and grace, which abolishes all claims and without which the promises do not stand fast. They claimed the inheritance by virtue of descent and the outward works of the Law; but an inheritance cannot be taken, it must be given, and given only to the children, the true heirs. To be Abraham’s seed, Abraham’s faith is required; and to be God’s children, it is required that one be born of God through faith. A new heart is required, a heart of flesh in place of the heart of stone, and where this change has not taken place, there is no right of inheritance, because there is no life, and therefore no fruit for God.

That was the second thing the people of Israel forgot: to bear fruit for God.

In the fifth chapter of the prophet Isaiah this fruitlessness of the chosen people is portrayed in vivid strokes. What was it that the Lord had not done for his vineyard? And when he came to harvest good grapes, he found wild ones. What then shall he do with such a vineyard? “I will take away,” says the Lord, “its hedge, and it shall be devoured; I will break down its wall, and it shall be trodden down. And I will lay it waste; it shall not be pruned nor hoed, so that briers and thorns come up; and I will command the clouds that they rain no rain upon it; for the vineyard of the Lord of hosts is the house of Israel, and the men of Judah his beloved planting; and he looked for justice, and behold, bloodshed; for righteousness, and behold, a cry” (Isa. 5:5-7).

Year after year the Lord came to his fig tree to find fruit and found none (Luke 13:7). Year after year God’s servants cried to him and said: “Spare the tree yet this one year!” In vain they dug about it and manured it; the Lord’s beloved planting, the men of Judah, brought him no fruit; and when the Son himself came to bring the fruit home, the measure was full; on the fig tree there were only leaves and no fruit, and judgment had to come, as the Savior pronounces it in our parable, the judgment of scattering and destruction, the judgment of the curse: the Kingdom had to be taken from them and given to a people that would bear its fruit.

The people of Israel also gave no heed to the stone. It was a chief cornerstone and became to them a stone of stumbling.

Since they had no life, no fruit, and therefore no right of sonship, they wished to rob the inheritance and lay violent hands upon the Son, in whom alone are life and the status of children. They cast from them the blessed Word: “Behold, I have laid in Zion a stone, a tried stone, a precious, sure-founded cornerstone; whoever believes shall not hasten” (Isa. 28:16). And although God’s house had been entrusted to them for edification, they rejected this chief cornerstone and built the house of their self-righteousness upon sand; when then the torrents came, its fall was great. They were offended at this chief cornerstone, because he was lowly in appearance and yet judged their conscience and rejected their righteousness and offered grace only for repentance and a broken heart, that sacrifice and that fruit which alone please God. The prophet’s word was fulfilled upon them, so that he who is the true sanctuary “became a stone of stumbling and a rock of offense for both the houses of Israel, a snare and a trap for the inhabitants of Jerusalem” (Isa. 8:14).

And so it came to pass that when in blind rage they rushed forward against this living stone and hanged him upon the tree of the curse, their judgment was sealed, their foundation torn away from beneath them; and when he rose victorious from the dead and from the Father’s right hand sent forth his messengers with the Gospel to hand over the Kingdom to other peoples who would bear its fruit, then the stone fell upon the temple and crushed it, and upon the people and scattered it; the judgment was accomplished.

Brothers and sisters! If anyone is in Christ, then he is Abraham’s seed and, according to the promise, an heir; “as many as have been baptized into Christ have put on Christ.” You who have been marked and born by the holy water of baptism, you are now the heir; you are the one who shall bear the fruit of the Kingdom of God, you are set upon the chief cornerstone.

But if you do not bear fruit, then the inheritance shall be taken from you, and the stone which you have despised shall fall upon you and crush you. It shall go worse with you than with Israel; for if God did not spare the natural branches, neither shall he spare you. See therefore the goodness and severity of God: severity toward those who have fallen, but goodness toward you, if you continue in his goodness; otherwise you also shall be cut off (Rom. 11:21-22). Do not be proud, but fear!

“I say to you, unless you repent, you shall all likewise perish.”

Friend, where is the fruit of the Kingdom of God among us? Do you have fruit?

STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* "blive træt" does not merely mean physical tiredness; in this sermonic context it means to lose heart, grow weary, slacken, or give up in prayer.
* "skaf mig Ret" is not just "help me" but "procure justice for me," "vind my case," or "grant me my right"; it has forensic force and must retain juridical sharpness.
* "Modstander" is both legal adversary and spiritual accuser. In the exposition it clearly widens into the Devil as accuser.
* "Bønhørelse" means not merely "answer to prayer" in a casual sense, but the hearing and granting of prayer.
* "Vedholden i Bøn" and "Udholdenhed" carry the full force of perseverance, steadfast continuance, and endurance.
* "Vrangkvillighed" denotes perverse unwillingness, obstinate ill-will, not mere reluctance.
* "stunder og længes" expresses deep yearning and homesick longing, not simple desire.
* "anfægter" is a key theological word. It means assails, contests, accuses, troubles, puts under spiritual trial. It must not be flattened into merely "bothers."
* "retfærdiggjør" is doctrinally exact: justifies.
* "Udfaarelses Aand" is an older form equivalent to the Spirit of sonship/adoption crying out. It must be rendered with doctrinal care, not paraphrased vaguely.
* "Barnekaaret" means the state, right, or standing of being God’s child.
* "Aandens Fattigdom" echoes poverty of spirit and spiritual neediness; it is not psychological insecurity.
* "Forjettelser" means promises, especially divine promises.
* "Beskuelse" suggests contemplation or beholding in a blessed, eschatological sense.
* "udvortelig Besiddelse" means outward/full manifested possession, not merely inward ownership.
* "bestjæmmet" means put to shame, confounded, disgraced.
* "det lider mod Aftenen, og Dagen helder" is both temporal and symbolic, suggesting decline toward the end.
* "Verdsligheden" is worldliness in the theological sense.
* "Egenvisdom" means self-wisdom, self-conceit in understanding, not just intelligence.
* "slumme, kolde Aftentaaage" is vivid metaphor: sluggish, dull, cold evening fog; it must remain imagistic.
* "bestikker jeg Vægtere" in older usage means appoint/set watchmen, not bribe.
* "paaminder Herren" means remind the Lord, call upon Him by putting His promises before Him.
* "eders" must become modern "your," but the solemn imperative force must remain.

Syntactic Hazards

* The prose uses long cumulative sentences with semicolon pivots and layered dependent clauses. These carry sermonic pressure and should not be broken into brief modern sentences without necessity.
* Repetition is deliberate: "bad og tiggede og tiggede og bad," "bed, bed altid, bed uafladelig," "Naade, Naade, Naade." Such repetition must be preserved because it builds urgency and oral cadence.
* The argument often advances by rhetorical question followed by doctrinal resolution. This movement must remain intact.
* Biblical quotation is embedded into authorial prose without modern distancing. The translation should preserve that integration.
* The alternation between exposition of the parable and direct address to the hearer is rapid and intentional; this should not be softened.
* The sentences often accumulate through anaphora: "indtil... indtil... indtil..." This progression must be preserved.

Theological Hazards

* The widow signifies both the individual believing soul and the believing Church on earth. This double referent must remain visible.
* The adversary becomes explicitly the Devil as accuser. The legal frame is theological, bound to justification.
* The contrast between unjust judge and loving Father is central; the sermon does not liken God morally to the judge, but argues from lesser to greater.
* Grace, Christ’s righteousness, justification, sonship, spiritual longing, and perseverance in prayer are interwoven. None of these may be generalized into vague devotional language.
* The sermon treats prayer as constitutive of faith, not merely an activity of faith: "Troen er en uafladelig Bøn." That exact theological force must remain.
* The cessation of prayer when faith and hope cease in eschatological fulfillment must be kept precise; it refers to consummation, not loss.
* "the last enemy, death" and inheritance imagery belong to Pauline and Matthean eschatology; this must remain doctrinally clear.
* The warning about the last days, scoffers, and encroaching worldliness has eschatological seriousness and must not be toned down.

Voice Hazards

* The tone is solemn, admonitory, earnest, pastoral, and uncompromising. Any smoothing into gentle devotional prose would damage the voice.
* The preacher directly implicates the reader: "Beder du altid...?" This interrogative pressure must be preserved.
* The sermon’s warmth lies in inevitability and doctrinal consequence, not sentimental flourish.
* Severe warning and tender consolation alternate. The contrast must remain sharp.
* Elevated diction is appropriate, but archaizing English such as "thee" or "thou" would introduce the wrong register.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* "Altid bede og ikke blive træt"
  Meaning: always pray and not grow weary, lose heart, or give up.
  Scriptural context: Luke 18:1.
  Theological implication: prayer is constant perseverance under delay.
  Natural English equivalent: "always to pray and not lose heart" is a strong biblical equivalent, though "not grow weary" also preserves sermonic gravity.

* "Skaf mig Ret over min Modstander"
  Meaning: vindicate me against my adversary; grant me justice against my opponent.
  Context: legal language in Luke 18.
  Theological implication: in the sermon it becomes forensic vindication before God against Satan’s accusation.
  Literal translation danger: "help me with my opponent" would lose the judicial dimension.

* "sagt Verden Farvel"
  Meaning: has bid the world farewell.
  Theological implication: renunciation of worldly belonging.
  Literal rendering works well and preserves devotional gravity.

* "død for Verden som Verden er død for den"
  Meaning: dead to the world as the world is dead to it.
  Scriptural resonance: Galatians 6:14 and Pauline death-to-the-world language.
  Literal rendering should be kept because the paradox is doctrinally meaningful.

* "paa Veien til det himmelske Hjem"
  Meaning: on the way to the heavenly home.
  Pietistic implication: earthly life as pilgrimage.
  Literal rendering is appropriate.

* "stunder og længes"
  Meaning: yearns and longs, with deep ache.
  Literal translation as "yearns and longs" preserves intensity.

* "raabe til ham Dag og Nat"
  Meaning: cry to Him day and night.
  Biblical context: Luke 18:7.
  This should remain "cry" rather than merely "pray," because the cry conveys distress.

* "Giv mig Naade! Skaf mig Ret?"
  Meaning: Give me grace! Vindicate me!/Grant me my right!
  Theological implication: the soul pleads both for mercy and forensic justification.
  Literal translation should preserve both petitions distinctly.

* "Kristi Ret maa være din Ret"
  Meaning: Christ’s right/righteous claim/cause must be your right. In context this is Christ’s righteousness and vindication counted as yours.
  Literal translation danger: bare "right" in English can be obscure. It may need a rendering that preserves the juridical and justificatory force, such as "that Christ’s righteousness may be your righteousness," unless the immediate legal motif requires "cause/right." Here theological clarity favors "righteousness."

* "Abba, Fader"
  Meaning: Abba, Father.
  Scriptural context: Romans 8:15; Galatians 4:6.
  This should follow standard biblical English.

* "de uudsigelige Sukke"
  Meaning: unutterable groanings.
  Scriptural context: Romans 8:26.
  A natural biblical equivalent is "groanings which cannot be uttered" or "groanings too deep for words." Since the sermon’s diction is older and more literal, "unutterable groanings" fits.

* "Bønnen forstummer, og Sukkene stilner"
  Meaning: prayer falls silent, and the sighs grow still.
  Theological implication: loss of living faith.
  Literal rendering is effective and vivid.

* "Troen er en uafladelig Bøn"
  Meaning: faith is unceasing prayer.
  Theological implication: prayer is intrinsic to faith.
  This must remain direct and aphoristic.

* "Døden er opslugt for evigt"
  Likely echo: 1 Corinthians 15:54, though the exact biblical phrase is "swallowed up in victory."
  The sermon slightly reshapes the image toward eternal finality. The translation should preserve the author’s wording, perhaps "swallowed up for ever," not correct it to the canonical phrasing.

* "Gak hen og arv det Rige"
  Likely from Matthew 25:34.
  Natural equivalent: "Come, inherit the kingdom..." That is the standard English biblical rendering.

* "det lider mod Aftenen, og Dagen helder"
  Meaning: it draws toward evening, and the day declines.
  Implication: the age moves toward its close.
  Literal rendering preserves symbolic force.

* "de hæse Raab"
  Meaning: the hoarse cries.
  Implication: harsh, insistent unbelief.
  Literal translation should remain vivid.

* "slumme, kolde Aftentaaage"
  Meaning: sluggish/drowsy cold evening fog.
  Implication: spiritual torpor spreading over Christendom.
  Literal translation should retain metaphor rather than explain it abstractly.

* "giver eder ingen Ro" / "giver ei ham nogen Ro"
  Meaning: give yourselves no rest / give Him no rest.
  Scriptural context: Isaiah 62:6-7.
  The KJV gives a natural equivalent here: "keep not silence" / "give him no rest." The latter phrase should be preserved because the sermon relies on it.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Thesis
  The Lord’s parable teaches that one must always pray and not grow weary.

* Initial exposition of the parable
  The widow did not prevail because her cause was just, but because she persevered without ceasing.

* Lesser-to-greater doctrinal inference
  If persistent prayer can overcome the perverse unwillingness of an unjust judge, how much more will persistent prayer prevail with a loving Father.

* Typological application
  The widow represents the believing soul and the believing Church on earth: lonely, homeless, longing for the heavenly home, opposed by an evil adversary.

* Identification of the adversary
  The adversary is the Devil, who contests the soul’s right and seeks to rob it of life and hope.

* Necessity of divine vindication
  Only God’s righteous judgment can silence the Devil’s accusation.

* Practical exhortation
  Therefore the soul must imitate the widow and cry day and night until help comes.

* Two doctrinal points on prayer
  First, prayer must be continual.
  Second, it must continue until it is fulfilled.

* Direct address to conscience
  The preacher asks whether the hearer truly prays continually, given the constant accusation and constant need for grace.

* Christological-grounding section
  The hearer’s only answer to accusation is Christ’s righteousness, supported by scriptural promise from Romans 8.

* Pneumatological/filial grounding
  These promises become ours through the Spirit of adoption by whom we cry, "Abba, Father."

* Diagnostic warning
  If prayer ceases, sighing ceases, and longing for Heaven vanishes, then the state of sonship is lost and the blessedness of the promise is gone.

* Definition aphorisms
  Faith is unceasing prayer; poverty of spirit is an unceasing cry; sonship is the blessed right to say, "Abba, Father."

* Temporal extent of prayer
  Prayer lasts until full vindication, final victory, the defeat of death, coronation, and the coming of the Son of Man.

* Eschatological consummation
  Then prayer ceases, because faith and hope cease in sight, and eternal love’s contemplation remains.

* Restatement
  As long as faith, struggle, and earthly life remain, prayer must remain.

* Final-vindication image
  The inheritance is not fully possessed until the Lord pronounces the final verdict.

* Exhortation to endurance
  Therefore endure to the end and do not grow weary.

* Final warning from Christ’s question
  Will the Son of Man find faith on earth? This is turned into an admonition to the individual and the Church.

* Last-days diagnosis
  Worldliness and self-wisdom increase; prayer becomes rarer; scoffing unbelief grows louder.

* Scriptural confirmation
  The preacher cites the apostolic warning about scoffers in the last days.

* Counter-call to the praying remnant
  Against the cold evening fog over Christendom, the Prophet appoints watchmen who give the Lord no rest.

* Concluding appeal
  Praying souls are those watchmen; they must remain faithful, keep the lamps burning, and refuse silence until the Lord comes.

STEP 4: BIBLICAL ECHO IDENTIFICATION

* Luke 18:1-8 is the governing text throughout.
* "dead to the world" echoes Pauline cruciform/world-renouncing language, especially Galatians 6:14.
* "Er Gud for os, hvo kan da være mod os?..." cites Romans 8:31-34.
* "Abba, Fader" echoes Romans 8:15 and Galatians 4:6.
* "de uudsigelige Sukke" echoes Romans 8:26.
* "Aandens Fattigdom" likely resonates with Matthew 5:3, "poor in spirit."
* "den sidste Fiende" is 1 Corinthians 15:26.
* "Døden er opslugt for evigt" echoes 1 Corinthians 15:54 and possibly Isaiah 25:8.
* "Gak hen og arv det Rige, som dig var beredt før Verdens Grundvold blev lagt" closely echoes Matthew 25:34, with "from the foundation of the world."
* "kun saaledes faaes Livsens Krone og Retfærdighedens Krans" echoes James 1:12 / Revelation 2:10 (crown of life) and 2 Timothy 4:8 (crown of righteousness).
* "i de sidste Dage skulle komme Spottere..." cites 2 Peter 3:3-4.
* "Paa dine Mure, Jerusalem, bestikker jeg Vægtere..." cites Isaiah 62:6-7.
* "eders Lamper være tændte og eders Lys brændende" echoes Luke 12:35.
* "naar Menneskens Søn kommer" returns to Luke 18:8 with eschatological focus.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* The translation must retain direct appeals and interrogations: "Do you pray always...?"
* Repetition must remain because it carries urgency and oral insistence.
* Warnings about blindness, loss of sonship, and spiritual decline must remain as warnings, not become neutral observations.
* Consolation must also remain strong: divine promises are "great and glorious," but they belong to praying children.
* The final appeal to praying souls as watchmen should sound mobilizing and solemn, not decorative.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* "bør bede" should retain obligation: "ought always to pray" or "should always pray." In the sermon title and governing theme, "ought always to pray" preserves force.
* "skal" often carries certainty or divine promise and should often be rendered as "shall."
* "maa" carries necessity and must usually be rendered as "must."
* "nytter ikke" should remain strong: "It avails nothing" or "It is of no use," not merely "it is not ideal."
* "gjælder om" means "the matter is" or "what matters is," not just mild preference.
* "kan ei" and negative capacities should remain impossible, not merely difficult.

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

* "Naade" = grace
* "Retfærdighed" = righteousness
* "Retfærdiggjør" = justifies
* "Forjettelse" = promise
* "Barnekaaret" = sonship / the state of sonship
* "Aanden" when theological = the Spirit
* "Sjælen" = the soul
* "Troen" = faith
* "Bønnen" = prayer
* "Bønhørelse" = the hearing/granting of prayer; in running English often "answer to prayer"
* "Modstander" = adversary
* "Anklager" = accuser
* "Fiende" = enemy
* "Arv" = inheritance
* "Herlighed" = glory
* "udvalgte" = elect/chosen ones. In this sermon, "His elect" is appropriate.

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* Widowhood must remain central as the image of earthly spiritual desolation.
* Pilgrimage to the heavenly home must remain concrete.
* The Devil’s opposition is cast as courtroom accusation; that legal metaphor must remain intact.
* Prayer as cry, sigh, and ceaseless pleading must remain embodied language.
* Death swallowed up, crown set on the head, lamps burning, evening fog over Christendom, and watchmen on Jerusalem’s walls all must remain vivid images.
* The final shift from prayer to contemplation in eternity must remain figurative and elevated.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* Severity: warning against blindness, silence in prayer, loss of sonship, and the chill of unbelief.
* Consolation: God is not an unjust judge but a loving Father; His promises are great and glorious.
* Hope: final victory, inheritance, crown, the coming of the Son of Man.
* Exhortation: endure to the end, do not grow weary, keep the lamps burning.
* These tonal movements must remain distinct rather than being leveled into a single devotional register.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Preserve sentence flow where clauses accumulate argument and pressure.
* Keep repeated imperatives and repeated nouns: "pray, pray always, pray without ceasing," "Grace, grace, grace."
* Use punctuation that allows proclamation: semicolons, commas, and periodic buildup where needed.
* Avoid clipped modern prose. The sermon was shaped for the ear.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

Always to pray and not grow weary.

Twenty-First Sunday after Trinity.

Luke 18:1-8. And He spoke to them also a parable to this end, that one ought always to pray and not grow weary. And He said: There was a judge in a certain city, who did not fear God and had no regard for any man. And there was a widow in that same city, and she came to him and said: Vindicate me against my adversary. And for a long time he would not; but afterward he said within himself: Though I do not fear God nor regard any man, yet because this widow causes me much trouble, I will vindicate her, lest by her continual coming she wear me out. And the Lord said: Hear what the unjust judge says! And shall not God vindicate His elect, who cry to Him day and night, though He delays long over them? I tell you: He shall speedily vindicate them. Nevertheless, when the Son of Man comes, will He find faith on the earth?

A widow suffering wrong is the image that the Lord chooses in order to set forth the believing soul and the believing Church on earth. And this widow prayed and begged and begged and prayed the ungodly and unjust judge to vindicate her, until she obtained what she desired. It was not her good cause that helped her; it was her tireless persistence in prayer. The judge cared nothing for her good cause; but her stubborn endurance became to him vexation and trouble, and so he helped her.

It is not the Savior’s meaning hereby to show how God is toward us; for God is precisely not like an unjust judge. Rather, the meaning is to show us how we are to be steadfast in prayer. For if persistent prayer can overcome the perverse unwillingness of an unjust judge, how much more shall persistent prayer overcome the heart of a loving Father and obtain the hearing of prayer.

A widow with an evil and unjust adversary is a moving image of the believing soul in the world. It is often truly lonely and forsaken; for it has bidden the world farewell, and it is dead to the world as the world is dead to it. It is homeless in the world, and as yet only on the way to the heavenly home. It has not yet reached home; it yearns and longs for it. And while it is thus in widowhood and forsaken, it has an evil adversary, who always tries to place himself between it and its heavenly inheritance, an accuser who contests its right and its cause, an enemy who would gladly rob it of its life and its hope. And this adversary, the Devil, yields before none but the righteous Judge; only God’s righteous judgment can strike down the Devil’s accusation and opposition and make it powerless. And often it seems as though God delays, as though it lasts so long, so dreadfully long, before He helps the poor soul in its distress.

Therefore the soul has such deep need to learn from the widow in our parable. It must do as she did; she plagued the unjust judge with her petitions so long until he helped her. Thus shall the Lord’s elect cry to Him day and night until He helps.

There are two things that we learn here about prayer. First, that it must be constant and unceasing. Second, that it must continue, even until it is fulfilled. It avails nothing to pray now and then, but what matters is to pray day and night. Neither does it avail to pray day and night for a time, for some weeks or months or years; but what matters is to pray without ceasing, until the answer comes, until the prayer is fulfilled.

Do you pray always, do you pray day and night? You do indeed have an adversary who watches constantly, an accuser who assails your right without ceasing. Do you pray just as unceasingly: Give me grace! Vindicate me? You do indeed know that you always need grace; you do indeed know that you always need the Lord’s righteousness. Or are you still so blinded that you think you can help yourself? But if you always need grace, and if it is only Christ’s righteousness that can strike down the Devil’s accusation, then pray, pray always, pray without ceasing, that Christ’s righteousness may be your righteousness. It stands written: "If God is for us, who can be against us? Who shall accuse God’s elect? God is He who justifies. Who is he that condemns? Christ is He who died, yea rather, who is also risen, who is also at God’s right hand, who also intercedes for us." Such promises are great and glorious, but how do they become yours, how can you appropriate them to yourself? They are appropriated to you through your filial Spirit of adoption, by whom you cry: Abba, Father! These promises are for the children, but the children are the praying souls in whom the Spirit works the unutterable groanings. If, then, prayer falls silent, and the sighs grow still, and the longing for Heaven vanishes, then the state of sonship is lost, and then the blessedness of the promise is gone. Faith is unceasing prayer, and poverty of spirit is an unceasing cry, and sonship is the blessed right to say: Abba, Father! Therefore pray without ceasing, pray day and night. Grace, grace, grace is the constant petition of believing prayer to God.

How long, you say, how long? Until you are heard, until you have received all that you need, until you have obtained vindication over your adversary; until the victory is won for ever, until the last enemy is overcome, until death is swallowed up for ever, until the crown is set upon your head, until the Son of Man comes. Then prayer ceases, when faith and hope are no more, but only the contemplation of eternal love.

So long as faith endures, prayer must endure. So long as the conflict continues, prayer must continue. So long as earthly life endures, it must be a life in prayer. For the victory is not won before the last enemy, death, has been struck down; and the inheritance, which your adversary would wrest from you, is not yours in full and outward possession before you have received the final answer from the Lord’s mouth: "Come, inherit the kingdom prepared for you before the foundation of the world was laid." Then you have received final judgment in your case; then your adversary is put to shame for ever and brought to silence.

Therefore endure unto the end and do not grow weary; only thus are obtained the crown of life and the wreath of righteousness.

But why then does the Savior say: "Nevertheless, when the Son of Man comes, will He find faith on the earth?" This is the serious word of warning to me and to you and to God’s Church. The times become more difficult for the elect, as it draws toward evening and the day declines. Prayer grows rarer because worldliness grows greater and men’s self-wisdom blinder. Already we hear the hoarse cries more and more loudly: "There is no God, there is no hearing of prayer, there is no salvation, there is no deliverance; all things happen according to a blind fate, according to an unchangeable predetermination, according to unchangeable laws of nature." It has come to pass according to the Lord’s word: "In the last days scoffers shall come, walking after their own lusts, and saying: Where is the promise of His coming? For since the fathers fell asleep, all things continue as they were from the beginning of the creation." It is this sluggish, cold evening fog that is spreading out over great portions of Christendom and dampening the fire of love and the warmth of prayer.

But then the Prophet says: "Upon your walls, O Jerusalem, I appoint watchmen; all the day and all the night, they shall never keep silence. You who remind the Lord, give yourselves no rest! And give Him no rest, until He establish, and until He make Jerusalem a praise in the earth."

O praying souls! It is you whom the Lord has appointed as watchmen; be faithful at your watch and be not silent day or night, never, until the Lord comes and His glory is revealed. Let not prayer grow still and faith die, but let your lamps be lit and your lights burning, lest all be darkness when the Son of Man comes!

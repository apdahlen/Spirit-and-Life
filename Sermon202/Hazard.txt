STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* "Broderkjærlighed" carries more than ordinary affection; it is theologically charged, denoting love among the brethren within Christ’s body.
* "Disciple" / "Disciple" must remain "disciples," not be weakened into "followers," because the Johannine context is explicit and covenantal.
* "gjælder dem, og dem alene" has restrictive force: "applies to them, and to them alone."
* "Rod og Grund" combines image and foundation; both organic and structural senses matter. "Root and ground" is possible, but "root and ground" in English is less idiomatic than "root and ground/foundation." Care is needed not to lose the doubleness.
* "prøvende Spørgsmaal" means testing or probing question, not merely reflective or interesting. It examines conscience.
* "levende Erfaring" is not bare subjective feeling; it is living, experiential knowledge with spiritual reality.
* "Lovtrældom" means bondage/slavery under law, not simply legalism in a modern abstract sense.
* "Tvang" means compulsion or constraint; it must retain spiritual harshness.
* "Aag" echoes biblical "yoke" and should remain so.
* "bie" means wait, tarry. Because it appears in reported false teaching, the rendering should preserve passivity.
* "Naadens Tid og Frelsens Dag" is scriptural language; it should retain biblical cadence: "the time of grace and the day of salvation."
* "stængt" is more forceful than merely "closed"; it suggests shut fast or barred.
* "synders Bekjendelse" should remain "confession of sins," not vague "admitting wrongs."
* "Misgjerning" is iniquity/evil deed, with biblical-moral weight.
* "saliggjørende Naade" denotes saving/blessedness-bestowing grace. "Saving grace" is the most natural equivalent.
* "hjemfalden til Død og Dom" means fallen under or forfeited to death and judgment; it carries juridical and eschatological force.
* "frimodig" in absolution contexts means "be of good cheer" or "take heart," not merely "be confident."
* "Aand af sin Aand" requires capitalized "Spirit" where divine, preserving theological clarity.
* "overgaar Kundskaben" echoes Ephesians 3:19 and should retain the paradox of knowledge surpassed.
* "selvopofrende" means self-sacrificing/self-renouncing in active love.
* "fortæret min Kraft" is stronger than "used my strength"; it suggests expended, consumed.
* "Menighed" may mean congregation/church/community, but here often denotes the Church or the congregation of God in a collective redemptive sense. Consistency matters.
* "Udlæg" here means outlay/expenditure, not exposition.
* "Guds Riges Arbeide" should remain "the work of God’s kingdom," not be reduced to church work in a merely institutional sense.
* "skyldige" in "what we were skyldige til at gjøre" means duty/obligation, not guilt simpliciter.
* "gjøre Alvor af denne Sag" means make earnest of this matter; one must not dilute the urgency.
* "svære" means burdensome/hard, echoing 1 John 5:3.
* "gavnligt" in context means gracious/beneficial/easy to bear; must align with the biblical yoke saying without sounding colloquial.
* "saart" here means painful/grievous rather than wounded.
* "i Løndom" means in secret, with biblical resonance.
* "Hjertets skjulte Menneske" echoes 1 Peter 3:4; the phrase should preserve its scriptural texture.
* "ter sig kraftigen" is older usage: shows itself mightily/works powerfully.
* "Virkemaade" means mode of working/way of operation.
* "Vantroens Magt" is the power of unbelief, not mere doubt.
* "Flid" means diligence, earnest application.

Syntactic Hazards

* The prose uses long doctrinally cumulative sentences; breaking them into short modern English units would flatten argument and cadence.
* Repeated interrogatives function as appeals to conscience and should retain their sermonic pressure.
* Semicolons often mark theological pivot or amplification rather than simple punctuation; these should often remain.
* Parallel constructions ("har været og er og vedbliver at være") must preserve rhythmic accumulation.
* The address "Sjæl" and "Broder" interrupts the flow for exhortation; these vocatives should be preserved.
* Scriptural citations are embedded into argument rather than isolated; the translation should integrate them similarly.
* Conditional reasoning structures ("Dersom..., hvormeget er det da...") must be preserved in their full force.
* The closing paraenesis culminates by accumulation rather than by concise summary; modern tightening would weaken it.

Theological Hazards

* The distinction between Christ’s love as source of brotherly love and Christ’s love as the measure of it is central and must remain explicit.
* The necessity of personal appropriation of Christ’s love through confession and forgiveness must not be rendered as general religious sentiment.
* The text insists that without confession of sins there is no forgiveness of sins; this doctrinal sequence must remain unsoftened.
* Christ’s death is presented as identical manifestation of his love; this compressed theology must remain intact.
* Absolution is portrayed as Christ entering the impure heart and speaking forgiveness; the sacramental/pastoral force must remain vivid.
* Love for the brethren is a fruit of sharing Christ’s life and Spirit; this is ontological and spiritual, not merely ethical.
* Self-sacrificial service to the Church is treated as necessary evidence of Christian life; avoid reducing it to volunteerism or generosity.
* The contrast between flesh and Spirit ("It is the death of the flesh ... but for the Spirit it is life and joy") must preserve Pauline theological tension.
* Mutual love functions apologetically and missionally as manifestation of God’s love before the world.
* The refusal to seek signs and wonders apart from cruciform love is central; the church’s wonder is love under suffering.

Voice Hazards

* The tone is solemn, admonitory, fraternal, and pastoral; it must not become merely explanatory.
* Direct address to conscience ("Sjæl," "Broder," "Brødre!") must remain vivid.
* Rebuke of stinginess and coldness must keep its moral edge.
* Consolation after confession must remain warm but not sentimental.
* The voice alternates between doctrinal instruction and exhortational summons; preserving this movement is essential.
* The prose has oral force suited to proclamation; smoothing it into academic English would erase its living sermonic quality.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* "Som jeg har elsket eder" is both command-ground and pattern. It does not mean merely "in the way I have loved you" as ethical example, but also from the prior fact of Christ’s redemptive love.
* "Jesus staar for Hjertets Dør og banker" clearly alludes to Revelation 3:20. In devotional usage it describes Christ’s gracious approach to the sinner. The natural English equivalent is "Jesus stands at the door of the heart and knocks," preserving the metaphor.
* "Naadens Tid og Frelsens Dag" echoes 2 Corinthians 6:2. The closest natural English is "the time of grace and the day of salvation," though "acceptable time" may be more biblically literal in some traditions. Because the sermon’s diction is doctrinally immediate, "the day of salvation" should be retained.
* "Hjertets Dør" and "Hjertedøren" are devotional idioms for inward consent, repentance, and surrender before Christ. Literal translation works, because the image is alive in English Christian prose.
* "Synden maa bort" means sin must go; it implies not mere concealment but expulsion and renunciation.
* "Fald ned som Peter for Kristi Fødder" evokes Luke 5:8. Literal translation is best because the biblical scene remains intelligible and powerful.
* "Slaa dig for dit Bryst med Toldneren" evokes Luke 18:13. Literal translation should remain because of its biblical familiarity.
* "Gud vær mig Synder naadig" corresponds to the publican’s prayer. A natural English equivalent with biblical force is "God, be merciful to me, a sinner."
* "Søn, Datter, vær frimodig" echoes Gospel absolution formulas such as Matthew 9:2 and related passages. "Take heart" is idiomatic, but "be of good cheer" carries older ecclesial resonance. Because the translation should avoid archaizing merely for tone, "take heart" is acceptable unless the surrounding cadence benefits from the fuller older phrase.
* "renser dig fra al Uretfærdighed" echoes 1 John 1:9. "cleanses you from all unrighteousness" preserves scriptural resonance.
* "Kristi Kjærlighed, som overgaar Kundskaben" echoes Ephesians 3:19. The natural equivalent is "the love of Christ, which surpasses knowledge."
* "satte Livet til for sine Venner" echoes John 15:13. "laid down his life for his friends" is the natural English biblical equivalent.
* "kjøbt den med sit Blod" echoes Acts 20:28 and related redemptive language. "bought it with his blood" should be preserved despite its starkness.
* "gjøre Alvor af denne Sag" is not an idiom to paraphrase weakly. Best rendered as "you must make earnest of this matter" or "you must take this matter in earnest." The latter is more idiomatic English while preserving force.
* "Jesu Bud er ikke svære; hans Aag er gavnligt og hans Byrde er let" combines 1 John 5:3 with Matthew 11:30. This interwoven biblical cadence should be retained. "His yoke is good" is more literal to some Scandinavian renderings, but English biblical familiarity favors "his yoke is easy" or possibly "kind." Here, because the sermon says "gavnligt," not directly "easy," care is needed. "His yoke is beneficial" is too stiff. "His yoke is kindly" is unnatural. The best English for sense and resonance is likely "his yoke is good, and his burden is light" or "his yoke is gracious, and his burden is light."
* "Omgang med Gud og Faderen" means intercourse/communion/fellowship with God the Father. Modern English cannot use "intercourse" here. "fellowship" or "communion" is best.
* "i Løndom" is biblically charged from Matthew 6 language. "in secret" is the natural equivalent.
* "Hjertets skjulte Menneske" is from 1 Peter 3:4; the KJV-like equivalent "the hidden man of the heart" is natural and precise, though it sounds marked. Because the sermon itself quotes Scripture-like diction, this is an appropriate place for such phrasing.
* "Rødderne skjules i Jordens Skjød" is a vivid organic image. "the roots are hidden in the bosom of the earth" preserves rhetorical force, though "bosom" is elevated. A flatter rendering would lose intensity.
* "Se, hvor de elsker hinanden" recalls the early church witness often paraphrased from patristic tradition. Literal rendering preserves function: "See how they love one another."
* "Stig ned af Korset, saa skal vi tro" echoes the mockery at the crucifixion. "Come down from the cross, and we will believe" is the natural equivalent.
* "Bliv tro i Kjærligheden" means remain faithful in love, steadfast in love. It is not merely emotional constancy.
* "Læg al din Flid paa Kjærligheden" means devote all diligence to love; literal wording should preserve exhortational urgency.
* "ikke med Ord, ei heller med Tungen alene, men i Gjerning og Sandhed" echoes 1 John 3:18. "not in word, neither with the tongue alone, but in deed and truth" preserves the cadence. "deed" is preferable to "action" for biblical resonance.

Places where literal translation would obscure force

* "bie" should be translated "wait" or "sit waiting," not left in a wooden cognate.
* "gavnligt" should not be rendered mechanically as "useful"; it must fit the yoke-saying’s theological comfort.
* "Omgang" should not be translated literally as "dealings" if the sentence becomes awkward; "communion" better preserves spiritual sense.
* "ter sig kraftigen" should not be translated literally as "behaves powerfully"; "shows itself mightily" or "works mightily" is required.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Opening thesis: Christ’s new commandment is given to Christ’s disciples alone, because it is rooted in his prior love for them.
* First doctrinal claim: Christ’s love is both source and measure of brotherly love.
* First probing question to conscience: Have we truly experienced and known Christ’s love toward us?
* Negative clarification: Without living experience of Christ’s love, attempts at loving the brethren become legal bondage and compulsion.
* Pastoral objection addressed: How can one gain living experience of Christ’s love?
* Refutation of false passivity: Reject voices that say one can only wait until Christ’s love seizes him.
* Positive instruction: Christ is already seeking, knocking, loving during the day of grace.
* Diagnosis of resistance: The sinner does not experience Christ’s love because the heart is kept shut.
* Means of opening the heart: confession of sins.
* Three biblical exempla of confession: David, Peter, the publican.
* Doctrinal explanation: Without confession there is no forgiveness; in forgiveness there is the living experience of Christ’s love.
* Christological center: Christ died for sinners; his death is his love.
* Pastoral absolution: Christ enters the unclean house of the heart and speaks forgiveness.
* Spiritual consequence: forgiven sinners learn Christ’s love and thereby learn love for the brethren.
* Second probing question: Do I love the brethren as Christ loved me?
* Concrete measure: living and dying for the brethren, spending strength for God’s church.
* Indictment of present Christianity: complaint and stinginess reveal failure of brotherly love.
* Fraternal rebuke and exhortation: hearts and hands should be open to God’s church because Christ bought it with his blood.
* Practical application: offerings, labor, self-denial, and gratitude toward those gathering support for the church’s work.
* Urgent warning: unless this matter is taken in earnest, Christian life will wither and die.
* Theological consolation: Christ’s command is not burdensome; love is death to the flesh but life and joy to the Spirit.
* Enlarged purpose: mutual love is not only for the church’s own sake, but as public witness that Christians are Christ’s disciples.
* Image of hidden roots and visible growth: hidden communion with God produces visible brotherly love.
* Missional consequence: if Christians loved more, the world’s coldness and darkness would yield more.
* Polemic against demand for miracles: the true miracle is cruciform love, as in Christ’s not coming down from the cross.
* Ecclesial application: the church conquers by steadfast love amid hatred.
* Final exhortation: seek no other miracle; devote all diligence to love, whose light reaches farther than expected.
* Closing apostolic appeal: love not in word only, but in deed and truth.

STEP 4: BIBLICAL ECHO IDENTIFICATION

* John 13:34–35 is the governing text.
* Revelation 3:20: Christ standing at the door and knocking.
* 2 Corinthians 6:2: the day/time of salvation and grace.
* Psalm 32:5: "I acknowledged my sin... and did not hide my iniquity."
* Luke 5:8: Peter falling at Jesus’ feet, confessing himself a sinful man.
* Luke 18:13: the publican striking his breast and asking for mercy.
* Romans 6:10: "the death that he died, he died unto sin once."
* Hebrews 9:28: Christ offered once to bear/remove the sins of many.
* Matthew 9:2 and parallels: "Take heart ... your sins are forgiven."
* 1 John 1:9: cleansing from all unrighteousness.
* 1 John 4:19: we love because he first loved us.
* Ephesians 3:19: the love of Christ which surpasses knowledge.
* John 15:13: laying down one’s life for one’s friends.
* Acts 20:28: the church purchased with blood.
* 1 John 5:3: his commandments are not burdensome.
* Matthew 11:30: my yoke is easy/kind and my burden is light.
* Romans 8 / Galatians 5 thematic echo: flesh versus Spirit, death versus life.
* 1 Peter 3:4: the hidden man/person of the heart.
* Crucifixion mockery narratives (e.g., Matthew 27:40–42): come down from the cross, and we will believe.
* 1 John 3:18: not in word or tongue, but in deed and truth.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* The warnings against false passivity, closed-heartedness, stinginess, and lovelessness must remain severe and searching.
* The exhortations to confession, sacrifice, and visible brotherly love must sound imperative, not advisory.
* Consolation must arise after repentance and absolution, not replace them.
* Direct addresses such as "Soul," "Brother," and "Brethren!" must remain, because they generate the sermonic pressure.
* The indictment of nominal Christianity must remain uncomfortable and concrete.
* The final summons must retain apostolic urgency and not read like a general reflection on kindness.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* "skulle elske hverandre" must retain command force: "are to love one another" or "shall love one another," depending on sentence cadence.
* "skulle prøve" and similar forms should preserve obligation and norm, not mere suggestion.
* "kan ikke andet gjøre end bie" must remain strong: "can do nothing but wait."
* "maa bort" must remain necessity: "must go."
* "ingen anden Maade" must remain exclusivity: "no other way."
* "skulde vi skynde os" and related exhortative forms should preserve moral obligation: "we ought to hasten" or "we should hasten," depending on strength in context.
* "du maa gjøre Alvor af denne Sag" must remain: "you must take this matter in earnest."
* "saa maa og et Guds Barns Livsrødder være skjulte i Gud" must retain necessity: "so must the life-roots of a child of God also be hidden in God."
* "Ønsk ikke" and "tænk ikke" are direct prohibitions and should remain so.

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

* "Kjærlighed" = "love" throughout, unless a possessive or contextual modifier requires explicitness.
* "Broderkjærlighed" = "brotherly love."
* "Brødrene" = "the brethren," to preserve ecclesial and scriptural resonance.
* "Menighed" = "the church" where the corporate body is central; in some places "congregation" is possible, but stability favors "church" in this sermon.
* "Aand" when referring to divine life = "Spirit"; when clearly human spirit, context may determine otherwise. Here most key occurrences are theological and should be capitalized as "Spirit."
* "Synd" = "sin"; "Syndernes Bekjendelse" = "confession of sins"; "Syndernes Forladelse" = "forgiveness of sins."
* "Naade" = "grace."
* "Frelseren" = "the Savior."
* "Kristi Kjærlighed" = "the love of Christ."
* "Guds Rige" = "God’s kingdom."
* "Kjød" = "flesh."
* "Disciple" = "disciples."
* "Guds Barn" = "child of God."
* "Vantro" = "unbelief."

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* Christ at the heart’s door must remain a living image, not an abstract invitation.
* The heart as an unclean house into which Christ enters must remain concrete.
* Christ’s death as the very form of his love must remain compressed and vivid.
* The measure of love as height, depth, inwardness, and self-sacrifice should retain its spatial and affective force.
* Hearts and hands being opened to the church must remain embodied.
* Christian life withering and dying if love fails must remain organic.
* Love as death to the flesh but life and joy to the Spirit must remain paradoxical and experiential.
* Hidden roots and visible trunk/crown must remain as the controlling image for secret fellowship and public love.
* Love’s light shining farther than expected must remain luminous rather than being paraphrased into influence.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* The sermon moves from doctrinal exposition to searching self-examination, from warning to absolution, from rebuke to encouragement, and from inward piety to public witness.
* The consoling section on forgiveness must not cancel the sharpness of the preceding call to confession.
* The rebuke of Christian stinginess must not overshadow the later blessing on hearts enlarged by Christ’s love.
* The anti-miracle polemic is severe, but it ends in hopeful confidence in love’s power.
* The closing apostolic exhortation should sound both tender and commanding.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Sentences should often remain long where thought accumulates by clause and semicolon.
* Repetition such as "has been and is and will continue to be" should be retained for spoken force.
* Vocatives should remain placed where they strike the hearer directly.
* Biblical quotations should be integrated with sermonic rhythm, not detached as formal citations.
* The final paragraph should gather force through imperative sequence: remain faithful, wish not, think not, lay all diligence upon love.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

Brotherly Love.

Thirtieth Sunday after Trinity.

John 13:34–35. A new commandment I give to you, that you love one another; that, as I have loved you, you also love one another. By this shall all know that you are my disciples, if you have love among yourselves.

Jesus Christ’s new commandment is given to Jesus Christ’s disciples and applies to them, and to them alone. For the new commandment has its root and ground in these words of our text: "As I have loved you." Jesus’ love toward us is the fountain of our love toward the brethren. It is also the measure by which we are to measure, and according to which we are to test, our brotherly love.

Therefore this is the first probing question which our text awakens in our souls: Have we in truth experienced and known Jesus Christ’s love toward us? For without a living experience of Jesus’ love, it is of no use to try to love the brethren. It would only become a new bondage under the law and a new compulsion; but compelled love is no true and genuine love. It would only become a yoke which no one could bear.

How, then, can anyone come to the living experience of Jesus’ love? You have surely heard that often, and you ought to know it quite well. Even if misleading voices arise and say, Jesus loves whom he will, and you can do nothing but wait until his love seizes you; yet you know better, Soul, than to listen to such seductive words. You surely know that Jesus stands at the door of the heart and knocks and wills that you should open to him. You surely know that Jesus’ love has been and is and will continue to be toward you, so long as it is the time of grace and the day of salvation. It is not because Jesus does not love you, does not seek you, that you do not experience his love, but because you keep the door shut fast when he knocks, keep the heart closed when he seeks you.

How, then, is the door of the heart opened to the Savior? That too you surely know, if only you were willing to do what you know so well. You keep the heart closed and the door shut fast because you know that your heart is unclean, because you understand that sin must go when Jesus comes in. Then there is no other way by which you can open the heart’s door to the seeking, knocking Savior than by the confession of your sins. Do as David says: "I acknowledged my transgressions and did not hide the iniquity of my sin," and the door is opened. Fall down as Peter did at Christ’s feet and say: "Lord, I am a sinful man," and the heart is open. Strike your breast with the publican and pray: "God, be merciful to me, a sinner," and there is room in the heart for Jesus’ saving grace.

That is the way to come to the living experience of Jesus’ love. Without confession of sins there is no forgiveness of sins; but in the forgiveness of sins there is the living experience of Jesus’ love. He died for you because you were a sinner, fallen under death and judgment; as Paul says: "The death that he died, he died once for sin;" and as we read in the Epistle to the Hebrews: "Christ was once offered to bear away the sins of many." His death is his love, and his love is his death; but his death is for your sins, Soul, and therefore you have no whole and full experience of his love until you confess your sin to him. Then he enters into the unclean house of the heart and speaks his mighty word: "Son, Daughter, take heart; your sins are forgiven you."

Then you experience that he loves you, loves you so highly that he forgives you your sin and cleanses you from all unrighteousness; loves you so highly that he gives you life from his life and Spirit from his Spirit. Then you learn to love him who first loved you.

If this is your experience, then you know the love of Christ, which surpasses knowledge. Then the Spirit also teaches you to love the brethren. Or how should anyone be full of Christ’s love and Christ’s life and not love those who belong to Christ?

But the second probing question which our text awakens in a Christian heart is this: Do I love the brethren just as Christ has loved me? As highly, as deeply, as inwardly, and as self-sacrificingly? How does it stand, Brother, when your love is to be measured by this measure? Do you live for the brethren, just as he lived for them? Are you willing to die for the brethren, as he laid down his life for his friends? Do you live in such a way for the brethren that, when death comes, you can say in truth: I have spent my strength in the labor for God’s church; I have loved it unto death.

If that which is called Christianity among us is to be measured by this measure, how much of it is there then that remains? We grumble and complain when God’s church requires a very little sacrifice, a very small outlay. We are ready to be unkind toward those who go about gathering the money that is needed for the work of God’s kingdom. How does this accord with brotherly love, with love for Jesus and for his church?

Brethren! this ought not so to be. Our hearts and our hands ought to be opened for God’s church. Jesus has bought it with his blood; how should we not love it in heart and deed? When God’s church has need of our money, our labor, our self-denial, our sacrifice, we ought to hasten to bring what we are able. And when the messengers of God’s church come to us and say: Give me what is needed for the church’s work, then we ought to thank them with glad hearts because they do what we were duty-bound to do.

Let this become better hereafter. Think on Christ’s love, how heavy and narrow and bloody was the way that he went, in order that God’s church might be built among us, and love the church and the brotherhood as he loved. You must take this matter in earnest, if your Christian life is not to wither and die. What is the church, if it is not full of love? What is brotherhood, if there is no brotherly love?

And Jesus’ commandments are not burdensome; his yoke is good, and his burden is light. If a human heart is enlarged by Jesus’ love, then it is not heavy and painful to love God’s church and Jesus Christ’s brethren. It is the death of the flesh, that is true; but for the Spirit it is life and joy. Blessed is the heart that loves, and the life that is offered up in love.

But it is not only for our own sake, for the brotherhood’s sake, that we are to love the brethren. Jesus wills that we should love one another, so that all may know that we are his disciples. It belongs to the calling of Christians in the world to bear witness and confess and reveal God’s love to the world. And this takes place through mutual love. "See how they love one another;" that is a revelation of God’s love which we owe to all. Our fellowship with God the Father is to be in secret, that the hidden man of the heart may thrive. The roots are hidden in the bosom of the earth, but trunk and crown grow up gloriously in the clear air. So too must the life-roots of a child of God be hidden in God. But brotherly love must shoot forth in rich growth before the eyes of all, that they may see and wonder at that power of God which shows itself mightily in his children. If God’s children loved one another more, the coldness and darkness of the world would have to give way more before the power of love. The world says to Christians: Do miracles, and we will believe. That is the same thing the Jews said to Jesus: Come down from the cross, and we will believe. But this is Jesus Christ’s miracle, that he did not come down from the cross, but suffered death in love toward the world. Thereby he became victor over the world and death and the devil. That is also the mode of operation of God’s church. Remain faithful in love, and your whole life is a miracle in the midst of the world’s hatred and wickedness and self-love. Desire not yourself to do any other miracle than this. And do not think that the power of unbelief is overcome by any other miracle than this. Lay all your diligence upon love, and the light of love shall shine much farther than you can expect or understand.

Brethren! let us love, not in word, neither with the tongue alone, but in deed and truth!

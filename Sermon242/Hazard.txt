STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* "Saa er jo Børnene fri" — "jo" adds insistence or self-evidence, not merely "so" in a flat logical sense. The force is: "Then surely the children are free" or "So the children are indeed free."
* "alvorfulde Lærdom" — not merely "serious lesson," but weighty, solemn, morally pressing instruction.
* "Forklarelse" — the Transfiguration, not a general "glorification" or "explanation."
* "den elskelige" — older devotional rendering meaning "the beloved," not modern "lovely" in a sentimental sense.
* "ophøiet" — exalted, lifted high in rank and glory; doctrinally charged.
* "indskrænket Embedstolthet" — narrow official pride; the word carries critique of office-bound self-importance.
* "Anordninger" — ordinances, established regulations, appointed arrangements; should not be weakened into merely "customs."
* "Egenraadigheden" — self-will, wilfulness, headstrong self-direction.
* "Menneskefrygten" — fear of man, with clear biblical and spiritual resonance.
* "Øvrigheden" — authorities, ruling powers, magistracy; not simply "government" in a modern bureaucratic sense.
* "tilrest" — in arrears, behind in payment; archaic economic nuance.
* "forekom Jesus ham" — Jesus anticipated him, spoke before he could speak.
* "frihed" / "fri" — freedom in a theological sense: release from accusation, coercion, legal bondage, servility, and anxious self-protection.
* "trættet" — disputed, wrangled, wearied themselves in argument.
* "frigjort" — set free, liberated.
* "Herlighed" — glory, in Johannine theological sense.
* "Medarvinger" — fellow heirs, co-heirs.
* "Lovens Tvang" — the compulsion or coercive force of the Law.
* "udøst i deres Hjerter" — poured out in their hearts, with Pauline resonance; should not be paraphrased away.
* "inderlig Skam" — inward, deep, heartfelt shame.
* "drages med" — dragged along with, burdened by, carried along under the weight of.
* "forlade dig helt paa ham" — entrust yourself wholly to him, rely entirely on him.
* "gaa saa fri og freidig" — go so freely and boldly; "freidig" here is spiritually fearless, not jaunty.
* "agte Bud og Forbud" — heed commandments and prohibitions; in context, count ceremonial restrictions as decisive.
* "Barmhjertigheden roser sig i Dommen" — mercy glories over judgment / rejoices against judgment; a biblical allusion requiring care.
* "nøies med det, jeg har" — be content with what I have.
* "fornedret" — brought low, humbled, abased.
* "vel erfaret" — practiced, instructed by experience, well schooled.
* "Fribrev" — charter of freedom, letter of release, formal document of emancipation. A vivid metaphor that should not be flattened.
* "drives de frivilligt" — they are moved willingly, led voluntarily; paradox of Spirit-led freedom.
* "forarge" — cause offense, scandalize, become a stumbling block.
* "uskikkede" — unfit, incapable, not in a condition to understand.
* "Opofrelsen" — the sacrifice, self-sacrifice, surrender of one’s own right.
* "skal aldrig staa fast for Midler" — should never be at a loss for means, should never come to a halt for lack of resources.
* "Stand" — estate, condition, rank, state of life.
* "forundt" — granted, allowed, vouchsafed.

Syntactic Hazards

* Long cumulative sentences build doctrinal force through accretion; they should not be broken up excessively.
* Semicolon logic is central: assertion, support, consequence, exhortation. The relation between clauses must remain explicit.
* Embedded quotations from Scripture appear as integral supports in the argument, not decorative citations.
* Rhetorical questions drive conscience and doctrinal inference; they must remain sharp and sequential.
* The sermon moves repeatedly from exposition to direct address ("du," "vi") and back again; these transitions must remain intact.
* Parallel patterns such as "hvor er da din Synd og Skyld, hvor er Guds Vrede og Dom over dig?" require preservation of repetition.
* Contrastive structures are frequent: Jesus is free, yet pays; children are free, yet serve in love; love hides freedom without losing it.

Theological Hazards

* Sonship: distinction and relation between Christ as the only-begotten Son and believers as God’s children must remain exact.
* Freedom: not autonomous self-assertion, but evangelical freedom from guilt, condemnation, legal compulsion, and human ordinances where love governs.
* Law and Gospel structure is central throughout: guilt, wrath, judgment, death, atonement, forgiveness, Spirit, and love.
* "Synden og Loven og Vreden og Døden" is a compact theological sequence and must retain full doctrinal force.
* Christ’s substitution is explicit: he bore all "for dig." This must not be softened into general moral influence.
* Forgiveness of sins and Christ’s atonement are treated objectively, not as mere subjective comfort.
* The Holy Spirit is the source of inwardly willing love; freedom is not antinomianism.
* "Ikke at forarge" concerns love’s restraint for the neighbor’s sake, not cowardly compromise on truth.

Voice Hazards

* The tone is solemn, exhortational, pastoral, and doctrinally weighty; modernization would blunt it.
* The preacher addresses conscience directly with urgent, searching questions.
* Moral seriousness arises from consequence and truth, not from emotional overstatement.
* Jesus is contemplated devotionally and doctrinally at once; the translation must preserve both warmth and precision.
* The prose alternates between instruction, rebuke, invitation, consolation, and prayer; these tonal movements must stay distinct.
* Older sermonic dignity should be preserved without using archaic English pronouns.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* "I er jo kaldte til Frihed, Brødre! Bruger kun ikke Friheden til en Anledning for Kjødet" — Pauline idiom from Galatians 5:13. "Kjødet" is "the flesh," not merely the body or sensuality alone. It refers to fallen human nature in its self-serving bent.
* "Du er Kristus, den levende Guds Søn" — confessional formula from Matthew 16:16. "Kristus" should remain "the Christ" in scriptural register when the confessional force is prominent.
* "Denne er min Søn, den elskelige, i hvem jeg har Velbehag; hører ham" — echo of the Transfiguration account. Natural English equivalent: "This is my beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased; hear him."
* "vidnet paa Jorden og vidnet fra Himlen" — not a fixed idiom, but a rhetorical pairing: earthly witness and heavenly witness.
* "Herre over alle Ting" — biblical dominion language; "Lord over all things" is preferable to something flatter.
* "det kom ikke op i deres Hoved eller Sind" — idiomatic doubling: it never entered their mind.
* "med nogen Bebreidelse" — with a certain reproach / note of reproach.
* "lide meget af de Ældste og Ypperstepræsterne og de Skriftlærde" — allusion to Christ’s passion predictions; scriptural cadence should be retained.
* "gjøre Sagen klar med et eneste Ord" — make the matter clear with a single word; idiomatic but straightforward.
* "den, som Sønnen faar frigjort, er virkelig fri" — John 8:36. Natural equivalent: "If the Son sets you free, you will be free indeed." Because the author’s phrasing is slightly adapted, the translation should preserve his wording while letting the biblical echo remain recognizable.
* "Guds Kjærlighed er udøst i deres Hjerter ved den Helligaand" — Romans 5:5. Strong Pauline phrasing; "poured out in their hearts" is the right biblical equivalent.
* "Kom hen til Jesu Kors" — devotional exhortation meaning not physical movement but faith’s approach to Christ crucified.
* "Guds Lam" — "the Lamb of God," unmistakable biblical title.
* "sat sin Sag i Jesu Haand" — entrusted one’s case, cause, whole matter into Jesus’ hand; should not be reduced to mere emotional dependence.
* "gaar saa fri og freidig i Kjærlighedens Tjeneste" — moves freely and boldly in the service of love.
* "Barmhjertigheden roser sig i Dommen" — James 2:13. Natural English equivalent in many translations is "Mercy triumphs over judgment." But because the author’s image is "boasts/glories," something like "mercy glories over judgment" preserves the metaphor better.
* "jeg formaar alt i Kristus, som gjør mig stærk" — Philippians 4:13. Natural equivalent: "I can do all things in Christ who strengthens me" or "through him who strengthens me." Since the author says "i Kristus," preserve that.
* "Kristi Forsoning er deres Fribrev fra Lovens Anklage" — not a set biblical quote, but a juridical-redemptive image: Christ’s atonement is their writ of freedom against the Law’s accusation.
* "for ikke at forarge dem" — important New Testament idiom: to avoid giving offense or becoming a stumbling block.
* "Kjærligheden skal aldrig staa fast for Midler" — love will never lack means; because God supplies what love needs for its burden-bearing work.
* "Fra dig er det altsammen, og af din Haand har vi givet dig" — 1 Chronicles 29:14. Natural equivalent should retain the paradox that the gift returned to God already came from God.
* "Ikke at forarge" — here almost a distilled ethical minimum: even where one cannot spread light and joy, one may at least refrain from becoming an obstacle.

Places where literal translation would obscure force

* "jo" often should be rendered by emphasis rather than a word-for-word equivalent.
* "forarge" cannot be reduced to modern "offend" in the sense of hurt feelings alone; it has moral and spiritual significance.
* "fri" must not become merely politically or psychologically free; it is evangelical and filial.
* "Kjødet" must remain "the flesh," not "desire" or "sinful urges" only, because the doctrinal scope is broader.
* "Anordninger" should not become "traditions" everywhere; at times "ordinances" or "regulations" is needed.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Opening thesis: The text contains a solemn lesson, summarized by Galatians 5:13 — Christians are called to freedom, but that freedom must serve love rather than the flesh.
* Christological grounding: Jesus has just been confessed as the Christ, the Son of God, and confirmed from heaven at the Transfiguration. Therefore, if anyone is free, it is the Son.
* Conflict setup: The tax collectors, ignorant of Christ’s true dignity, insist on the Temple tax out of official pride and zeal for old ordinances.
* Peter’s failure: Peter answers too quickly out of self-will and fear of man, trying to manage the matter prudently and avoid conflict.
* Jesus’ correction: By the kings-and-children analogy, Jesus establishes the principle: the children are free.
* Doctrinal expansion: This freedom belongs not to Christ alone, but also to believers, because what the Son has received he shares with his own; they are heirs with Christ.
* Evangelical application: If hearers feel unfree under sin, guilt, wrath, rules, and bondage, they are directed to the cross, where Christ has borne sin, law, wrath, and death.
* Ethical application from Christ’s example: Jesus moves freely in love beyond ceremonial restriction; believers are called into that same freedom of love and mercy.
* Existential application: Freedom includes contentment in want and abundance, grounded in the Father, Christ’s forgiveness, and the Holy Spirit’s inward working.
* Return to narrative tension: Though Jesus is free from the Temple tax, he pays it so as not to give offense.
* Clarification of love’s restraint: Jesus does not surrender truth under Pharisaic pressure when neighbors suffer, but he does surrender his own right when love counsels restraint.
* Miracle as instruction: The coin in the fish’s mouth teaches Peter that God himself provides the means when love bears burdens for others.
* Concluding synthesis: God’s children walk through the world in freedom and love; if they cannot always spread light and joy, at least they may refrain from causing offense.
* Final prayer: A petition that the congregation may rightly understand Galatians 5:13.

STEP 4: BIBLICAL ECHO IDENTIFICATION

* Matthew 17:24–27 — the sermon text itself: the Temple tax and the saying "then the children are free."
* Galatians 5:13 — "You were called to freedom, brothers; only do not use your freedom as an opportunity for the flesh, but serve one another through love."
* Matthew 16:16 — Peter’s confession: "You are the Christ, the Son of the living God."
* Matthew 17:5 — the voice at the Transfiguration: "This is my beloved Son ... hear him."
* Matthew 16:21 — "suffer many things from the elders and chief priests and scribes."
* Matthew 15:1–2 — reference to prior conflict over the traditions/ordinances of the elders.
* John 8:36 — "if the Son sets you free, you will be free indeed."
* John 17:22 — "The glory that you have given me I have given to them."
* Romans 8:17 — believers as heirs of God and fellow heirs with Christ.
* Romans 5:5 — "the love of God has been poured out in our hearts through the Holy Spirit."
* Johannine / Pauline atonement language condensed in "sin, law, wrath, and death" borne by Christ.
* John 1:29 — "the Lamb of God."
* Gospel scenes alluded to:
* cleansing the leper — Matthew 8:3 and parallels.
* raising Jairus’s daughter — Matthew 9:25 and parallels.
* healing on the Sabbath — multiple Gospel scenes.
* "Take up your bed and walk" — John 5:8.
* James 2:13 — "mercy glories over / triumphs over judgment."
* Philippians 4:11–13 — contentment in want and abundance, and strength in Christ.
* 1 Chronicles 29:14 — "Of your own have we given you."

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* Warnings and rebukes must remain direct, especially where Peter’s self-will and fear of man are exposed.
* The sermon’s appeals to the hearer’s conscience must remain searching: "Do you truly believe ... ?" and "How long will you torment yourself ... ?"
* Consolation must not become sentimental. It is grounded in Christ’s objective work: sin, wrath, judgment, and death borne by the Son.
* Assertions such as "Surely the children are free" and "in all things the children are free" must retain their declarative force.
* The final ethical exhortation must preserve the sober pastoral realism: even where one cannot spread light and joy, one may at least refrain from giving offense.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* "Skulde" often carries rhetorical necessity or hypothetical force; in context it should often be rendered "if there were ever to be any talk..." or "were there ever to be..."
* "maatte" can mark strong logical necessity or earnest wish depending on context; both must be distinguished.
* "vil" in exhortative contexts can be volitional and should not be weakened.
* "skal" is often decisive future or necessity: "shall," "is to," "must," depending on context.
* "kan ei" / impossible formulations must remain strong where present conceptually, even if not in those exact words.
* "burde" in context of reproach should remain "ought," not merely "should perhaps."

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

* "Børnene" / "Guds Børn" — the children / God’s children.
* "frihed" / "fri" — freedom / free.
* "Kjærlighed" — love.
* "Loven" — the Law when doctrinally pointed; otherwise "law" only where context clearly indicates a looser sense.
* "Kjødet" — the flesh.
* "Synd" / "Skyld" — sin / guilt.
* "Vreden" — wrath.
* "Dom" — judgment.
* "Synders Forladelse" — forgiveness of sins.
* "Forsoning" — atonement.
* "den Helligaand" — the Holy Spirit.
* "Anklage" — accusation.
* "forarge" — give offense / become a stumbling block, according to context.
* "Herlighed" — glory.
* "Arvinger" / "Medarvinger" — heirs / fellow heirs.
* "Broder" — brother, with Christological family sense intact.

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* "Børnene er fri" is not only legal logic but filial metaphor: sonship grounds freedom.
* "Kom hen til Jesu Kors" must remain an image of faith’s approach to the crucified Christ.
* "Guds Lam" must remain intact.
* "Kristi Forsoning er deres Fribrev fra Lovens Anklage" must preserve the juridical image of a letter or writ of release.
* "Kjærlighedens Veie" should remain "the ways of love," not a flattened abstraction.
* "skjule sin Frihed" is powerful and paradoxical; love veils freedom without abolishing it.
* The fish with the coin is not merely an anecdote but an enacted sign of divine provision.
* "sprede Lys og Glæde" should remain vivid.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* Severity: critique of tax collectors’ narrowness and Peter’s rashness.
* Elevation: Christ’s divine sonship and lordship.
* Consolation: release from sin, guilt, wrath, and death through Christ.
* Exhortation: come to the cross, learn freedom, do not be bound by human rules.
* Ethical sobriety: freedom serves love and avoids giving offense.
* Final warmth and prayer: "The Lord help us..."

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Keep cumulative sermonic periods where thought gathers force.
* Preserve semicolon pivots and paired clauses.
* Maintain direct address as spoken exhortation.
* Let repeated assertions recur with oral emphasis: "The children are free."
* Retain the cadence of scriptural quotation within prose.

Then surely the children are free.

Twenty-Third Sunday after Trinity.

Matthew 17:24–27. And when they had come to Capernaum, those who collected the Temple tax came to Peter and said, Does your Master not pay the Temple tax? He said, Yes. And when he had come into the house, Jesus anticipated him and said, What do you think, Simon? From whom do the kings of the earth take toll or tax, from their own children or from strangers? Peter says to him, From strangers. Jesus said to him, Then surely the children are free. But so that we may not give offense to them, go to the sea, cast a hook, and take the first fish that comes up; and when you open its mouth, you will find a stater; take that and give it to them for me and you.

The solemn lesson which is contained in our text today Paul has expressed thus in the Epistle to the Galatians: "You were called to freedom, brothers! Only do not use freedom as an occasion for the flesh, but serve one another in love."

Jesus had just come home to Capernaum from the journey during which Simon Peter had confessed concerning him: "You are the Christ, the Son of the living God;" and immediately after this confession three of the disciples had been witnesses of his Transfiguration and had heard the voice from the cloud: "This is my beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased; hear him." It had therefore been witnessed on earth and witnessed from heaven that Jesus was the Son, the only-begotten of the Father, exalted above all and all, so that he was in truth Lord over all things, and no one was lord or ruler over him. If there were ever to be any talk of someone being free, then surely it must be Jesus, the King’s Son, whom all ought to honor, the Teacher of truth, whom all ought to hear.

But those who collected the Temple tax knew nothing of Jesus’ infinite majesty and did not understand that he is Lord of all. In narrow official pride and Jewish zeal for the old ordinances they pressed on with their tax collecting. It never entered their mind that there could be any exception to the rule, or that there could be any talk of any change in the "divine" ordinance, as they would call it. Perhaps these tax collectors were doubly eager to get the tax from Jesus because they had something like a feeling that he held quite another view of the old ordinances than they did, and that they might perhaps get occasion to accuse him of breaking the Law if he should refuse to pay the Temple tax. They met Peter and asked him, with some reproach, it seems: "Does your Master not pay the Temple tax?" They thought it ought to have been paid already long ago, and that it suited such a man poorly to be in arrears with so holy an obligation.

And Peter hastened to answer: Yes. All that he had seen and heard from Jesus and about Jesus in these last weeks had by no means driven self-will and the fear of man out of Peter. He still thought that he understood how matters ought to be arranged, and how Jesus ought to avoid "suffering many things from the elders and chief priests and scribes." He did not want any misunderstanding with the authorities and to expose Jesus to any accusation of rebellion or lawbreaking. He too had understood that Jesus thought otherwise than others about the old ordinances, and he wished to avoid all unpleasantness by quickly settling the matter on his own. Should the tax collectors meet Jesus himself, then in Peter’s judgment there might easily be another clash, as there had just lately been precisely over the ordinances of the elders (Matt. 15:1 and 2).

But Peter had acted too hastily when he took the decision in this matter into his own hand. And yet we can scarcely reproach him for it when we read of the wise and loving way in which Jesus set him right. What should we not have lost from the Gospel if the disciples’ missteps had not been the occasion for Jesus to reveal his heart! Peter was given no time to explain the matter in his own way; Jesus anticipated him, for he would surely not have Peter become more deeply entangled in his thoughtlessness. And Jesus makes the matter clear with a single word: "From whom do the kings of the earth take toll or tax, from their own children or from strangers?" Peter answers: "From strangers." And Jesus’ conclusion is self-evident: Then surely the children are free.

Yes, this is the main thing: the children are free. If we truly and indeed are God’s children, then it is altogether certain that freedom is ours. The learned have disputed much as to whether the children here in this place are only Jesus, or whether it is Jesus and his disciples. But if they had understood what is written, that the one whom the Son sets free is truly free, then they would not have disputed about this. For however great the difference is between the Father’s only-begotten Son and God’s children, yet this is true, that all that the Son has received from the Father, that he has given to his believers, according to what he himself says: "And I have given them the glory that you have given me." God’s children are God’s heirs and Jesus Christ’s fellow heirs, so that the same freedom that he has, his brothers and sisters also have.

The children are free. And there are surely few words that contain so much and that are so difficult to grasp. From the compulsion of the Law and from rules and ordinances the children are free, because the love of God is poured out in their hearts through the Holy Spirit, who is given to them.

But if we feel with inward shame today that we are not free, that we are dragged along with so much sin, we are dragged along with so much guilt, we are plagued with so many rules and forms and bonds, then it is still time to win the children’s freedom. Come to Jesus’ cross and see what sin and the Law and wrath and death have done with the Son. Do you truly believe that he suffered it all for you? Do you truly believe it? Very well then, where is your sin and guilt, where is God’s wrath and judgment over you? Are you not free when the Lamb of God has taken it all upon himself? How long will you torment yourself with a yoke that Jesus will take altogether away, if only you will entrust yourself wholly to him? The children are indeed free. There is no guilt of sin and no sentence of death over those who have placed their cause into Jesus’ hand.

Come to Jesus and see how he goes so freely and boldly in the service of love without heeding commandments and prohibitions. Only look at him, how little he thinks of becoming unclean when he touches a leper, or when he takes the dead girl by the hand and raises her up! Look at him, how free he is when he heals on the Sabbath and commands the healed man: "Take up your bed and walk!" Learn from him that love is free, and mercy glories over judgment, and do not let yourself be bound by the many thousands of human considerations and rules.

The children are free. And if you have not yet learned this, then come to him who had nowhere to lay his head, and learn from him how free the one is who can say with Paul: "I have learned to be content with what I have; I know how to be brought low, and I know also how to abound; in everything and in all things I am instructed, both to be full and to hunger, both to abound and to suffer need; I can do all things in Christ, who strengthens me." Yes, truly in all things the children are free. God is their Father, and he loves them; therefore all his ways are blessed ways of freedom for them. Jesus is their brother, and in him they believe; therefore they are altogether certain of the forgiveness of their sins, and Christ’s atonement is their charter of freedom from the Law’s accusation. The Holy Spirit is poured out in their hearts; therefore they are moved willingly in the ways of love. Truly the children are free.

Therefore Jesus did not need to pay the Temple tax either. Yet he adds: "But so that we may not give offense to them, go to the sea, cast a hook, and take the first fish that comes up! And when you open its mouth, you will find a stater; take that and give it to them for me and you."

See again the wisdom and the love! He who has all God’s power and all God’s freedom does not use it for his own exaltation and his opponents’ humiliation. For him it matters not to give offense to them. That they tried to give offense to him, that was their fault and their misfortune; he will not give offense to them by doing what he had a right to do, but what they were unfit to understand. Jesus did not yield to the Pharisees when it was a matter of healing on the Sabbath and sparing the sick person a day of suffering; but he did not use his right and freedom when it was he himself who had to suffer by the sacrifice. He let the tax collectors have the tax they demanded, and did not reveal his power and his dominion and his freedom to them.

But Peter was to try and see that the children are free even then, when love compels them to hide their freedom. Therefore the Savior sent him out to fish and gave him this time also a wondrous catch; for in the fish’s mouth Peter found a coin large enough to pay the Temple tax for both Jesus and Peter. This was to teach Peter that love should never be at a loss for means when it takes burdens and difficulties upon itself "so as not to give offense to them."

Thus the children are again free, for God is himself the one who gives them all that they use in the service of love. The fish and the stater in its mouth, all is God’s gift, and we do not give ours, but his. It is as David says when he and his people brought rich gifts for the building of the Temple: "Who am I, and what is my people, that we should have strength to give a freewill offering such as this? From you is it all, and from your hand we have given to you" (1 Chron. 29:14).

Thus there is no estate so glorious as that of God’s children. In freedom and love they walk through the world, and if they cannot spread light and joy upon their fellow men’s path, this at least is granted them: not to give offense.

May the Lord help us rightly to understand it: "You were called to freedom, brothers! Only do not use freedom as an occasion for the flesh, but serve one another in love" (Gal. 5:13).

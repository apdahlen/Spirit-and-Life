STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* Menneskens Søns Tilkommelse: literally “the coming of the Son of Man.” Tilkommelse is an older, elevated theological term; “advent” is possible, but “coming” better preserves the biblical plainness of the Gospel citation.
* forgaa: not merely “disappear,” but “pass away,” with biblical and eschatological force.
* ingenlunde: emphatic negation, stronger than simple “not”; best rendered “by no means” or “shall in no wise.” In authorial prose, “shall by no means pass away” preserves force without archaism of pronouns.
* agtede det ikke: not simply “did not notice.” It means they paid no heed, took no warning, gave it no regard. This is morally charged negligence.
* Letsindighed: not mere “lightheartedness.” It means moral frivolity, heedlessness, careless worldliness.
* gruelige: “terrible,” “dreadful,” “ghastly”; not merely “awful” in a weakened modern sense.
* besvarede Herrens alvorlige Advarsel: “answered the Lord’s solemn warning” means responded to it by their conduct, not necessarily verbally replied.
* tog tilægte og gav tilægte: biblical marriage idiom, “marrying and giving in marriage.” Must retain scriptural cadence.
* syndfloden: “the Flood,” specifically the deluge of Noah.
* kristelige Tro: older confessional usage, “the Christian faith,” not vague “religion.”
* Hovedsagen: “the main thing,” “the chief matter.” The sermonic argumentative force depends on this contrast.
* Opvækkelse: spiritual awakening, not merely mental arousal.
* med Vælde: “with might” / “in might”; forceful royal coming.
* hans Betaling for hans Ansigt: biblical idiom of recompense before him. “His recompense before him” preserves scriptural resonance.
* Staden: “the city,” elevated form, here Jerusalem.
* landsflygtigt og slavebundet: exiled and enslaved / driven into exile and bondage.
* særdeles Lastefuldhed: not “special sinfulness,” but conspicuous or exceptional viciousness.
* Tryghed: here false security, complacent safety.
* sanselige Nydelser: sensual pleasures, not necessarily only sexual but fleshly, sense-bound enjoyment.
* Domstanker: thoughts of judgment; not abstract “judgmental ideas,” but consciousness of divine judgment.
* Arme, forblindede Menneskebarn: singular direct address, “poor, blinded child of man” / “poor, blinded human creature.” Must keep lament and rebuke together.
* Afgjørelse: decisive determination, verdict, final settlement. Repetition is important.
* ufravigelige Regel: inflexible / unbending rule.
* Forargelser: stumbling blocks / causes of offense / all that offends. In biblical context, likely “all causes of sin” or “all stumbling blocks.”
* den ganske Jordens Kreds: the whole circuit of the earth, idiomatically “the whole earth.” Yet some elevation should remain.
* Vaagn op du, som sover: biblical awakening formula; imperative force must remain sharp.
* bestandigt: continually, perpetually.
* indretter dig: order your life, settle yourself, arrange your life as though this world were permanent.
* kræves din Sjæl af dig: biblical idiom; “your soul is required of you” is the natural equivalent.
* Naadens Tid / velbehagelige Tid / Frelsens Dag: technical salvific expressions from Scripture; must remain stable and recognizable.
* kom Jesus Kristus ihu: “remember Jesus Christ.” Ihu is an older form with solemn biblical resonance.
* forkastet Kjærligheden: “rejected Love” may be too abstract unless capitalized intentionally for Christ’s saving love. Contextually it is divine love manifested in Christ; “rejected love” may work, but “rejected that love” may better preserve sense without over-allegorizing.
* forstøde dig: reject, cast you off.
* Aarvaagenhed: wakefulness, vigilance, watchfulness. In this sermon, “watchfulness” is the strongest and most theological.
* Vægterne slumrer paa Murene: watchmen slumber on the walls. Important prophetic/biblical register.
* raaber af Struben og ikke sparer: from Isaiah; “cry aloud and spare not” is the natural English equivalent.
* Herrens fulde Rustning: the whole armor of the Lord / full armor. Echo of Ephesians, usually “whole armor of God.”
* Menighed: congregation, church, people of God; here likely “congregation” or “church.”
* Lysestage: lampstand, with Revelation resonance; not merely candlestick in a decorative sense.
* Sjælens evige Brudgom: the soul’s eternal Bridegroom. Must preserve bridal mystical imagery.
* Himmellængselen: longing for heaven, homesickness for heaven. The compound is spiritually dense and should not be flattened.

Syntactic Hazards

* The sermon alternates between Scripture citation, doctrinal exposition, historical exemplum, direct address, and exhortation. This structure depends on long, accumulating sentences and emphatic pivots.
* Semicolon logic is crucial. Clauses are often laid beside one another to intensify consequence rather than merely to coordinate information.
* Repetition of “De agtede det ikke” and “Vaager derfor!” functions as sermonic refrain. This must remain visible and forceful.
* Rhetorical questions drive self-examination. They must not be softened into statements.
* The contrastive structure “Det er ikke ... men ...” is central to the argument and must be preserved precisely.
* Enumerative biblical cadence appears in strings such as “Skrig og Jammer og Frygt og Flugt” and “Dommen, Adskillelsen, Afgjørelsen, den evige Afgjørelse.” These must not be compressed.
* Appositional expansions, such as defining who is truly prepared, must remain cumulative.
* Direct apostrophe to the singular hearer (“dig,” “du”) intensifies conscience-address and should not be generalized into “people” or “we.”

Theological Hazards

* The sermon distinguishes physical death from the Gospel’s proclamation of life. That distinction is doctrinally central and must not blur.
* “Mine Ord skulle ingenlunde forgaa” is the anchor of permanence over against cosmic dissolution.
* “Menneskens Søns Tilkommelse” is not merely an end-times event but the decisive Christological center of faith.
* The coming judgment includes separation, election, recognition by the Lord, condemnation of the wicked, and eternal consequences.
* “Herren kjender sine” carries covenantal and soteriological weight; it is not simple acquaintance.
* “Naadens Tid” means the present season of grace before judgment; this must retain urgency.
* Christ as the one raised from the dead, who comes again and will hold judgment, is the hinge of exhortation.
* “Forkastet Kjærligheden” and “Kjærligheden forstøde dig” present rejected grace turning into judgment; this must remain severe.
* The admonition to God’s children emphasizes sanctification, vigilance, cooling love, and relapse from first love.
* Bridal imagery for the soul and Bridegroom must remain spiritual and ecclesial, not sentimental.

Voice Hazards

* The voice is solemn, urgent, exhortational, and pastorally severe. Over-modernization would reduce moral pressure.
* The author speaks with biblical saturation. The English must preserve Scriptural resonance without introducing gratuitous archaic pronouns.
* Lament and rebuke often coexist in one address: “Arme, forblindede Menneskebarn!” This double movement must be retained.
* The sermon’s heat arises from inevitability, consequence, and direct address, not from emotional overstatement. Translation must preserve that balance.
* The alternation between warning to the world and admonition to believers is deliberate; each requires a different tonal inflection.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* “tog tilægte og gav tilægte”
  Meaning: “marrying and giving in marriage.”
  Scriptural context: Matthew 24:38.
  Theological implication: the point is not that marriage is sinful, but that ordinary earthly continuance can become the form of total heedlessness before judgment.
  Natural equivalent: the familiar biblical phrasing “marrying and giving in marriage” should be retained.

* “de agtede det ikke”
  Meaning: they took no heed, paid no attention, gave no regard to the warning.
  Scriptural context: Matthew 24:39.
  Theological implication: culpable indifference to divine warning.
  Literal “they noticed it not” would be too weak; “they took no heed” best preserves the moral force.

* “den Tid, den Sorg”
  Meaning: roughly, “that time, that sorrow” or “there will be time enough to grieve then.”
  Function: a proverbial expression of postponement or dismissive fatalism.
  Theological implication: if death were all, one might defer concern; but because the Gospel proclaims more than death, such postponement is false.
  Best rendering: an idiomatic English paraphrase is needed, such as “That sorrow can wait until its time,” though care is needed not to become colloquial.

* “Se, den Herre, Herre kommer med Vælde”
  Meaning: “Behold, the Lord God comes with might.”
  Scriptural context: Isaiah 40:10 in older Scandinavian biblical diction.
  Theological implication: royal, saving, and judging advent of the Lord.
  Natural equivalent: “Behold, the Lord God comes with might” is strong and recognizable.

* “hans Løn er med ham, og hans Betaling for hans Ansigt”
  Meaning: his reward is with him, his recompense before him.
  Scriptural context: Isaiah 40:10; also eschatological resonance with Revelation 22:12.
  Theological implication: the Lord comes with judgment and reward already determined.
  Literal translation is workable because English biblical idiom supports it.

* “Verdens fire Hjørner”
  Meaning: the four corners of the world.
  Scriptural context: apocalyptic gathering of the elect.
  Theological implication: universal scope of Christ’s gathering power.
  Best retained nearly literally.

* “Ilden, som ikke udslukkes, og Ormen, som ikke dør”
  Meaning: the unquenchable fire and the worm that does not die.
  Scriptural context: Mark 9:44, Isaiah 66:24.
  Theological implication: irreversible judgment.
  Natural equivalent: “the fire that is not quenched, and the worm that does not die” or smoother “the unquenchable fire and the worm that does not die.”

* “Verdens Ven og Guds Fiende”
  Meaning: friend of the world and enemy of God.
  Scriptural context: James 4:4.
  Theological implication: worldly allegiance is spiritual enmity.
  Should remain direct and binary.

* “Verden forgaar med dens Lyst”
  Meaning: the world passes away with its lust/desire.
  Scriptural context: 1 John 2:17.
  Theological implication: worldly desire is transient and doomed.
  “Lust” may sound too narrow sexually in modern English; “desire” or “its lusts” depends on desired biblical resonance. “Its desire” may weaken plurality; “its lust” sounds closer to old biblical diction. Context suggests “its desire” or “its lust” with explanation implicit.

* “Herrens Dag kommer som en Tyv om Natten”
  Meaning: the day of the Lord comes as a thief in the night.
  Scriptural context: 1 Thessalonians 5:2; 2 Peter 3:10.
  Theological implication: suddenness, unpreparedness, judgment.
  Natural equivalent: the traditional phrase should be retained.

* “Vaagn op du, som sover”
  Meaning: awake, you who sleep.
  Scriptural context: Ephesians 5:14.
  Theological implication: conversion, repentance, spiritual awakening.
  Natural equivalent: “Awake, you who sleep!”

* “Du Daare, i denne Nat kræves din Sjæl af dig”
  Meaning: “Fool, this night your soul will be required of you.”
  Scriptural context: Luke 12:20.
  Theological implication: sudden death exposes false security.
  The KJV-like “required of you” is the most natural and theologically exact rendering.

* “Naadens Tid” / “den velbehagelige Tid” / “Frelsens Dag”
  Meaning: the time of grace / the accepted time / the day of salvation.
  Scriptural context: 2 Corinthians 6:2.
  Theological implication: the present is the divinely appointed time for repentance.
  “Accepted time” is the classic biblical equivalent, though “favorable time” is more modern. Given the sermonic register, “accepted time” fits well.

* “Vægterne slumrer paa Murene”
  Meaning: the watchmen slumber on the walls.
  Scriptural context: prophetic watchman imagery, especially Isaiah and Ezekiel.
  Theological implication: spiritual leaders fail in vigilance and warning.
  Literal rendering preserves force.

* “raaber af Struben og ikke sparer”
  Meaning: cry from the throat with full force and do not hold back.
  Scriptural context: Isaiah 58:1.
  Theological implication: fearless proclamation of sin.
  The natural biblical English equivalent is “cry aloud and spare not.”

* “Herrens fulde Rustning”
  Meaning: the Lord’s full armor, echoing “the whole armor of God.”
  Scriptural context: Ephesians 6:11.
  Theological implication: active spiritual warfare against sin.
  Best rendering: “the whole armor of God” if the echo is foregrounded; but since the text says Herrens, “the Lord’s full armor” could preserve literalness. Because the allusion is obvious, “the whole armor of God” is probably the strongest equivalent.

* “du har forladt din første Kjærlighed”
  Meaning: you have left your first love.
  Scriptural context: Revelation 2:4.
  Theological implication: decline from original fervor and devotion.
  Natural equivalent: “you have left your first love.”

* “flytte din Lysestage”
  Meaning: remove your lampstand.
  Scriptural context: Revelation 2:5.
  Theological implication: loss of ecclesial standing and divine favor.
  Must remain “lampstand,” not generalized.

* “den Sjælens evige Brudgom”
  Meaning: the soul’s eternal Bridegroom.
  Scriptural context: Christ as Bridegroom in Matthew 25 and Johannine / Pauline bridal imagery.
  Theological implication: watchfulness is bridal expectation, not mere fear.
  Literal rendering is necessary.

* “Himmellængselen”
  Meaning: longing for heaven.
  Theological implication: Christian readiness is marked by homesickness for heaven, not attachment to the world.
  Literal explanatory rendering is needed; there is no single exact English compound.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Thesis opening: all creation is subject to vanity and passing away; heaven and earth will pass away.
* Immediate corrective pivot: the Christian faith does not stop at death and dissolution.
* Central doctrinal assertion: the main thing is not that heaven and earth pass away, but that Christ’s words do not pass away.
* Christological center: the decisive matter is the coming of the Son of Man.
* Awakening proclamation: “The Lord comes.” This is not speculation but announced certainty.
* Historical exemplum 1: Noah’s generation ignored warning and was overtaken by the Flood.
* Historical exemplum 2: Jerusalem ignored John, Jesus, and the apostolic call to repentance, and judgment came.
* Contemporary application: the same heedless worldly condition prevails again.
* Clarification: eating, drinking, marrying are not named as exceptional vice, but as signs of worldly complacency and godlessness.
* Exposure of worldly desire: the world wants peace for sensual enjoyment and rejects the Gospel that disturbs it with death, judgment, and eternity.
* Direct address to the hearer: what will you say when the end comes?
* Eschatological consequence: judgment means separation and eternal determination.
* Soteriological criterion: the Lord knows His own and gathers them; the wicked are consigned to irreversible judgment.
* Searching questions: do you know the Lord, and does the Lord know you?
* Warning against worldly friendship: it will not help when the world passes away.
* Evangelical summons: wake up while grace remains.
* Christological appeal: remember Jesus Christ, raised from the dead, who shall come again to judge.
* Sharp personal test: what have you done with Him who died to save you?
* Severe consequence: rejected love becomes rejecting judgment.
* Renewed Gospel invitation: seek grace now; now is the accepted time.
* Second audience shift: not only the world, but also God’s children are addressed.
* Ecclesial admonition: watchfulness is lacking; watchmen sleep; worldliness grows in the congregation.
* Prophetic complaint: where are the voices that cry aloud and spare not?
* Apocalyptic-ecclesial warning: many have left their first love and risk lampstand removal.
* Final positive definition of readiness: the prepared soul is turned from the world, lifted to the Lord, awaiting the Bridegroom, longing for heaven, and centered wholly in Jesus.
* Concluding refrain: therefore watch, because the Son of Man comes at an hour you do not expect.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* Warnings must remain direct, not moderated.
* Repetition must stay exposed: “They took no heed,” “Watch therefore,” “The Lord comes.”
* Direct conscience-address in the singular must remain.
* Lament over blindness should retain tenderness and severity together.
* Images of catastrophe, judgment, and exclusion must not be euphemized.
* Consolation must arise as true Gospel invitation in the midst of warning, not as emotional softening.
* The transition to God’s children must preserve the preacher’s grave disappointment at ecclesial drowsiness.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* skulle: usually “shall,” especially in scriptural and sermonic assertions of certainty.
* maa / bør / kan ei equivalents in this passage should be rendered strongly where implied; nothing should be weakened into “might” or “should maybe.”
* “vilde” in conditional examples should be rendered with proper consequence: “would.”
* “er kun den” repeated three times should remain emphatic: “prepared is only the one who ...” or “only that one is prepared who ...”
* Imperatives such as “Vaager,” “Vaagn op,” “søg Naade” must remain as commands.

* Creation bears the seed of death within itself.
* Vanity has set its stamp upon the world.
* The heavens are rolled up like a garment.
* The flood comes and sweeps all away.
* The Lord comes with might and recompense.
* The world seeks peace for its sensual enjoyments.
* Belshazzar’s feast ends in terror.
* The day comes as a thief in the night.
* Watchmen sleep on the walls.
* A mist of worldliness envelops all.
* A cradle-song of the world lulls into sweet sleep.
* The soul has an eternal Bridegroom.
* Christ leads the Bride home.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* Cosmic transience at the opening is solemn and doctrinal.
* The pivot to Christ’s words introduces firmness and hope.
* Noah and Jerusalem sections intensify warning through history.
* The middle direct address becomes searching and accusatory.
* The Gospel summons introduces tender urgency.
* The address to God’s children becomes chastening and sorrowful.
* The closing bridal imagery turns toward longing and hope, while still under command to watch.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Preserve long movements of thought where clauses build toward a terminal force.
* Use semicolons where the Norwegian accumulates weight through balance and progression.
* Keep repeated openings visible: “They took no heed,” “Watch therefore,” “Prepared is only the one ...”
* Maintain public-proclamation rhythm, especially in warnings and scriptural citations.
* Do not break up rhetorical crescendos into clipped modern prose.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

The Coming of the Son of Man.

Twenty-Fifth Sunday after Trinity.

Matthew 24:35-44. Heaven and earth shall pass away, but My words shall by no means pass away. But of that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven, but the Father alone. But as the days of Noah were, so shall also the coming of the Son of Man be. For as they were in the days before the Flood, eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, until the day that Noah entered into the ark, and they took no heed, until the Flood came and swept them all away, so shall also the coming of the Son of Man be. Then shall two be in the field; the one shall be taken, and the other left. Two women shall be grinding at the mill; the one shall be taken, and the other left. Watch therefore! For you do not know in what hour your Lord comes. But know this, that if the master of the house had known in what watch of the night the thief would come, he would have watched, and would not have let his house be broken into. Therefore be you also ready! For the Son of Man comes in an hour when you do not think.

“Creation has been subjected to vanity,” says Paul. “All flesh is grass, and all its glory as the flower of the grass; the grass withers, and its flower falls away.” All life in the world comes to an end; it bears the seed of death within itself, and vanity has set its stamp upon it. Like a garment the heavens shall be rolled together and changed, and the earth shall perish.

This is as certain as it is appointed unto men once to die. But here God’s Gospel and the Christian faith do not come to a halt. If there were nothing more to preach, one might well say: that sorrow can wait until its time. But now there is more, infinitely more. For God’s Gospel does not preach death, but life. Therefore this is not the main thing in our text, that heaven and earth shall pass away; but this is the main thing: “My words shall by no means pass away.” It is not the end of the world, but the coming of the Son of Man, that is the main thing for the Christian faith. Therefore this cry shall sound for the awakening of all who sleep: The Lord comes! Behold, the Lord God comes with might, and His arm rules; behold, His reward is with Him, and His recompense before Him. The Son of Man comes; and He comes in the hour when you do not think; He comes to judge the living and the dead.

But it is written of the people in Noah’s days that “they ate and drank, married and gave in marriage, until the day that Noah entered into the ark, and they took no heed, until the Flood came and swept them all away.”

So shall the coming of the Son of Man be. “They took no heed”: that was the dreadful frivolity with which Noah’s contemporaries answered the Lord’s solemn warning through Noah’s mouth. And then the Flood came, and then there was crying and misery and fear and flight—and no deliverance any more. Death-struggle and death-agony for a little while, and then the stillness of death, when all was over.

“They took no heed”: that was the frivolity with which the Jews answered the warning cry of John the Baptist and of Jesus and of Jesus’ disciples: “Repent, for the kingdom of heaven is at hand.” They took no heed; they defied God’s voice, and they despised Jesus’ tears over stubborn Jerusalem. And the judgment came, and the city was taken, and the temple was burned, and there was not left one stone upon another; and the unhappy people was driven into exile and bondage, sold into the hand of its enemies.

“They took no heed.” And alas, it appears that the same way is being taken yet once again. For again it goes as in the days of Noah: “They ate and drank, married and gave in marriage.” These words do not describe any exceptional viciousness; but they denote complete frivolity, false security, worldliness, and godlessness. This condition can indeed be joined with the very highest human civilization, and even with a very respectable morality; but not with the fear of God and faith in Jesus Christ. Therefore the world would so gladly have Christ’s Gospel out of the world. The world wants peace and quiet for its sensual enjoyments; it will not be disturbed by thoughts of death and thoughts of judgment and the earnestness of eternity. To enjoy life, to enjoy sensuality and vanity, that is the matter; away with the Gospel of the coming of the Son of Man! Thus it goes as in the days of Noah: “They ate and drank, married and gave in marriage.”

Poor, blinded child of man! What will you say when the end of these things comes? For the end comes, the Lord comes, and what shall then take place? Then comes the judgment, the separation, the decision, the eternal decision: “The one shall be taken, and the other shall be left.” Then it goes according to the inflexible rule: “The Lord knows those who are His.” His elect He shall gather from the four corners of the world, and not one of them shall be forgotten or passed over. But the wicked and all stumbling blocks shall be given over to the fire that is not quenched, and the worm that does not die.

How will it go with you on that day? Do you know the Lord, and does the Lord know you? Or are you still the world’s friend and God’s enemy? Little will the friendship of the world help you on that day, when the world passes away with its desire. As Belshazzar’s feast came to an end in terror in the night when Babylon fell, so the world’s joy will abruptly come to an end when the day of the Lord comes as a thief in the night upon all those who dwell on the face of the whole earth.

Therefore the loving voice of the blessed Gospel sounds to you: “Awake, you who sleep! Awake, awake!” Do you think that you will live upon the earth forever, or do you think that the earth will stand to all eternity, since you order your life as though this were your abiding place? “Fool, this night your soul is required of you.” What will you do when death and judgment come, you who have used your whole life and all your powers in the service of the world and of sin?

Awake, while it is the time of grace, and remember Jesus Christ, who is raised from the dead. He is the one who comes again; He is the one who shall hold judgment. What have you done with Him who went into death in order to save you out of the world’s corruption and open for you the way into God’s blessedness? If you have rejected love, then love will cast you off. Therefore awake and seek grace in the time of grace; behold, now is the accepted time; behold, now is the day of salvation.

But also to God’s children, and quite especially to them, this word sounds today: Watch therefore! There is nothing more necessary in the present time of this world than watchfulness. And yet it is almost forgotten among us. The watchmen slumber upon the walls, and the inhabitants of the city dream sweet dreams and forget the danger. Or is there not a mist of worldliness that enshrouds us all, and a cradle-song of the world that lulls us into sweet sleep? Where are those watchmen’s voices that cry aloud and spare not, but declare to the Lord’s people their sins? Where are those watchful Christians who stand in the whole armor of God, ready to fight against sin in all its forms? Worldliness grows strong in the midst of God’s congregation, and love grows cold in many because the desire of the world grows strong. There are many to whom the Lord sends this word: “But I have this against you, that you have left your first love. Remember therefore from where you have fallen, and repent, and do the first works; but if not, then I come to you soon and will remove your lampstand from its place, if you do not repent.”

Watch therefore! Let the heart turn away from the world and its vain joys and sorrows, and be lifted up toward the Lord, the soul’s eternal Bridegroom, who soon comes to lead the Bride home. Only that one is prepared who waits for the Bridegroom; only that one is prepared who has longing for heaven in the soul and rejoices to depart from the world and its vanity; only that one is prepared for whom Jesus is the first and the last, beginning and end.

Watch therefore! “For the Son of Man comes in the hour when you do not think.”

STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* Dommen can mean judgment in the judicial and eschatological sense, not merely a verdict. In this passage it repeatedly carries the force of final divine judgment.
* Livet is not merely biological life but life in the Johannine and soteriological sense: eternal life, participation in divine life, salvation.
* fornedret means humbled / brought low / abased, with strong Christological resonance tied to humiliation before exaltation.
* Forbandelsens Træ literally means the Tree of the Curse, an allusion to the cross under the category of curse-bearing, not merely “cross” in a neutral sense.
* Opreisning here is deliverance, raising up, restoration, rescue; it should not be flattened into “encouragement” or merely “help.”
* Fordømmelse is condemnation in the judicial and eternal sense.
* byder here means offers / proffers / sets before, not merely “commands.”
* aabenbaret legemligen echoes Colossians 2:9; legemligen must be rendered bodily / in bodily form, not just visibly.
* annamme is an older form meaning receive.
* Menneskens Søn / Menneskesønnen must be handled consistently as Son of Man, preserving both biblical title and judicial authority.
* betænk dig means reconsider / take thought / reflect again, with urgent moral appeal.
* bærer Verdens Synd echoes John 1:29; singular Synd should likely remain singular, “the sin of the world,” to preserve scriptural resonance.
* forsmægtet means languishing, fainting away, wasting with exhaustion; it is stronger than merely “tired.”
* sønderslyngede Skuldre is vivid and concrete; it suggests mangled / lacerated / torn shoulders, not merely wounded shoulders.
* Faderhuset is the Father’s house, a biblically resonant expression that should remain intact.
* forført, forlokket signals seduced, enticed, led astray; both terms are cumulative and should not be collapsed.
* falden blandt Røvere echoes the Good Samaritan. It must retain biblical and narrative force.
* følelsesløs means without feeling, hard, unfeeling; “indifferent” may work contextually but should not lose the moral chill.
* Herberge and Pleie together mean shelter / inn and care / tending; the Good Samaritan echo should remain visible.
* vaandede dig means writhed / agonized, not merely suffered.
* gabede mod dig means yawned / gaped at you, a vivid abyss image.
* besværet og arbeider reflects older biblical idiom corresponding to the weary and heavy laden; laboring here is spiritual toil.
* Strid is warfare / hard service / struggle, echoing Isaiah 40:2; it is not merely “conflict.”
* Misgjerning is iniquity / transgression, stronger than generic wrongdoing.
* forligt means atoned for / pardoned / reconciled in context; the doctrinal sense must be preserved carefully.
* frimodig means be of good cheer / take courage / be of good heart; it has biblical and pastoral force.
* oprigtigt means honestly / sincerely / candidly.
* formedelst ham means through him / by means of him, doctrinally important mediation language.
* det helder mod Aften means it draws toward evening, metaphorically indicating life’s end and the nearness of judgment.
* Mulm is deep gloom / gathering dusk, not just darkness.
* fortærende Ild echoes Hebrews 12:29 and Deuteronomy language; must remain “consuming fire.”
* lader sig ikke spotte echoes Galatians 6:7; “is not mocked” is the natural scriptural rendering.
* Letsind is levity, frivolity, reckless light-mindedness; morally weighty, not mere carelessness.
* uhørt here means without being heard, preserving judicial procedure imagery.
* agtede ikke paa mig means paid no heed to me / did not regard me.
* kjendes ved mig means acknowledge me / own me / confess me.
* tryglede is implored / pleaded.
* forvildet is straying / lost / gone astray.
* studsede et Øieblik means you paused / started / were struck for a moment.
* Letsind og Lyst is the world’s frivolity and pleasure / lust; “pleasure” must retain moral danger.
* gaa irette med dig means enter into judgment with you / contend in judgment with you.
* Aasyn is countenance.
* lokkede dig is drew / beckoned / wooed you, but should not sound sentimental.
* maa in this context signals necessity: you must receive the judgment.
* ugudelige is the ungodly.
* pines means is tormented.
* salige Samfund is blessed fellowship / communion with God.
* en Herre til Gud Faders Ære echoes Philippians 2:11 and should preserve confession and doxological force.
* Salighed is blessedness / salvation in the eternal sense; depending on context, “salvation” best preserves doctrinal clarity.
* Pine is torment.

Syntactic Hazards

* The sermon uses long cumulative sentences with semicolon pivots and a mounting rhythm. Breaking these into short modern units would weaken the exhortational force.
* The repeated interrogatives addressed to “Ven” create direct pressure on conscience; they must remain pointed and personal.
* Parallel hand imagery — “I en Haand ... i den anden ... Med en Haand ... med den anden ...” — should remain tightly parallel.
* Successive rhetorical questions accumulate as moral examination rather than simple inquiry; they must not be softened.
* Biblical citations and paraphrases are woven directly into the prose. Translation must preserve the shifts between authorial narration and scriptural speech without confusion.
* The movement from invitation to indictment is staged through repeated anaphoric structures (“Han ... Han ... Han ...”). This cadence must remain visible.
* Quoted speech by Christ is often embedded within larger sentences. These should remain syntactically integrated where possible.
* The passage makes strong use of antithesis: life / death, salvation / judgment, grace / wrath, offered Christ / judging Christ. This structure should not be blurred.
* The judicial speech near the end depends on cumulative clauses of accusation; sentence division must not lessen the formal gravity of judgment.

Theological Hazards

* The Father giving all judgment and all life to the Son is a Christological claim about divine authority, mediation, and unity with the Father.
* Christ’s humiliation and exaltation are held together: the crucified one is the judge. This paradox must remain sharp.
* “In him the fullness of the Godhead is revealed bodily” is doctrinally dense and should retain incarnation language.
* Honoring the Son as one honors the Father is not devotional excess but a claim about the Son’s divine dignity.
* The offers of grace are concrete invitations to salvation, forgiveness, rest, and eternal life; they must not be generalized into emotional comfort.
* The language of sin, wrath, hell, condemnation, and eternal death is explicit and must not be toned down.
* The Son of Man judges precisely because he became man; the judicial role is tied to incarnation.
* The condemnation scene emphasizes human culpability in rejecting grace, not arbitrary damnation.
* References to faith, forgiveness, atonement, and reconciliation must preserve substitutionary and evangelical force.
* The final acknowledgment that Jesus Christ is Lord to the glory of God the Father is compulsory recognition even by the condemned; this must retain solemn eschatological force.

Voice Hazards

* The prose is solemn, urgent, searching, and pastoral at once. Over-literary English would distort its directness; over-modern English would flatten its gravity.
* The repeated address “Ven” is intimate but not casual. “Friend” must sound earnest and probing, not warm in a light modern sense.
* The author speaks to the conscience with relentless seriousness. This moral pressure must remain.
* The tenderness of Christ’s invitations is immediately set against the terror of rejected grace; preserving this tonal contrast is essential.
* Vivid bodily imagery — bleeding head, thorn-crowned brow, torn shoulders, wounds, hellish anguish — must remain concrete.
* The sermon’s force arises from inevitability and consequence more than ornament. The translation must preserve that pressure without melodrama.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* “Forbandelsens Træ” means the accursed tree, the cross understood under the biblical category of bearing the curse. Likely scriptural background includes Galatians 3:13 and Deuteronomy 21:23. “The Tree of the Curse” preserves the image and theological force better than simply “the cross.”
* “I ham er Guddommens Fylde aabenbaret legemligen” clearly echoes Colossians 2:9. A natural English equivalent is “In him the fullness of the Godhead is revealed bodily” or “dwells bodily.” Because the Danish has aabenbaret, “revealed bodily” is more faithful to the wording, while still letting the Pauline echo sound.
* “Lammet, som er slagtet, er værdigt ...” echoes Revelation 5:12. A natural English equivalent is “The Lamb who was slain is worthy to receive ...” This has a familiar biblical cadence in English.
* “ikke langt fra nogen af os” echoes Acts 17:27. “He is truly not far from any one of us” preserves the biblical resonance.
* “Se det Guds Lam, som bærer Verdens Synd!” echoes John 1:29. The most natural equivalent is “Behold the Lamb of God, who bears the sin of the world!” “Behold” preserves the scriptural force better than “look at.”
* The image of Christ carrying the sinner “paa mine sønderslyngede Skuldre tilbage til Faderhuset” merges the lost sheep motif with cruciform suffering. Biblical context includes Luke 15, but the shoulders are marked by passion imagery. The metaphor should remain vivid, not explained away.
* “Han søgte dig som en Moder søger sit Barn” is not a fixed biblical citation, but it participates in biblical maternal imagery for divine compassion; compare Isaiah 49:15 and Christ’s lament over Jerusalem.
* “falden blandt Røvere” directly invokes Luke 10:30, the Good Samaritan. The following details — wounds, lifting onto the beast, bringing to shelter and care — confirm the allusion. This must remain transparent in translation.
* “Som Havsbølger gik dine Synder over dit Hoved” echoes Psalmic inundation imagery; compare Psalms 38, 42, 69, and Jonah 2. Literal rendering is important because the overwhelming flood image carries spiritual force.
* “Helvedes Afgrund gabede mod dig” is apocalyptic and biblical in tone; the abyss gaping toward the sinner should remain figurative and severe.
* “Kom hid til mig du, som er besværet og arbeider, jeg vil give dig Hvile for din Sjæl” echoes Matthew 11:28–29. The King James offers “Come unto me, all ye that labour and are heavy laden ...” but per instruction, pronouns should be modernized. A suitable form is “Come to me, you who labor and are burdened, and I will give rest to your soul.”
* “løfte dit Øie til Korset, som Israels døende Folk løftede sit Blik til Kobberslangen” echoes Numbers 21 and John 3:14–15. The analogy must remain explicit.
* “tale til dig som til Jerusalems Hjerte” echoes Isaiah 40:2 (“Speak to the heart of Jerusalem”). “Speak to your heart” preserves the prophetic tenderness and authority.
* “din Strid er fuldendt, din Misgjerning er forligt” is very close to Isaiah 40:2. The terms carry atonement and completed warfare / service; the line should not be paraphrased loosely.
* “Vær frimodig, dine Synder er dig forladte” echoes Gospel absolution scenes such as Matthew 9:2. “Take courage; your sins are forgiven you” or “Be of good cheer” are options. “Take courage” avoids archaic tone while preserving force.
* “uden Penge og uden Betaling” echoes Isaiah 55:1. It signifies free grace, not merely free access.
* “det helder mod Aften, og Natten kommer snart” is both literal idiom and eschatological metaphor. “It draws toward evening, and the night soon comes” preserves the solemn temporal drift.
* “Han lader sig ikke spotte, og hvad et Menneske saar, det skal det og høste” echoes Galatians 6:7. The natural English equivalent is “God is not mocked; whatever a man sows, that shall he also reap.” With modernized pronouns elsewhere, this can still stand in a scriptural register.
* “bankede ... paa dit Hjertes Dør: Slip mig ind” echoes Revelation 3:20. “Knocked at your heart’s door: Let me in, that I may save you” keeps the direct appeal.
* “som en Fader kalder et forvildet Barn hjem” evokes the prodigal son theme of Luke 15, though phrased more generally.
* “O, om du vidste endnu idag, hvad der tjener til din Fred” echoes Luke 19:42. It should be preserved very closely because the lament is recognizable.
* “gjemme dig under mine Vinger som en Kylling” echoes Matthew 23:37 / Luke 13:34, where the hen gathers her brood. The unexpected singular “a chick” should remain if possible, since it reflects the Danish phrasing.
* “Se hvilket Menneske!” echoes John 19:5, Ecce Homo. “Behold the Man!” is the natural English equivalent and carries established scriptural resonance.
* “Korsfæst! hans Blod kommer over mig og mine Børn!” combines the cry for crucifixion from the Passion narratives with Matthew 27:25. It must remain shocking and self-condemning.
* “i Skyerne” with angels coming to judge echoes synoptic apocalyptic language and Danielic Son of Man imagery.
* “det lyse, venlige Blik ... er bleven en fortærende Ild” combines pastoral tenderness with judgment imagery; compare Revelation 1:14 and Hebrews 12:29.
* “gaaer bort fra mig, I forbandede, i den evige Ild ...” echoes Matthew 25:41 and should remain close to the Gospel wording.
* “ingen Fred for den ugudelige” echoes Isaiah 48:22 and 57:21.
* “en Orm, som aldrig dør, og en Ild, som aldrig slukkes” echoes Mark 9:44, 46, 48 and Isaiah 66:24. Literal retention is essential.
* “Jesus Kristus ... er en Herre til Gud Faders Ære” echoes Philippians 2:11. The natural equivalent is “Jesus Christ ... is Lord, to the glory of God the Father.”

Expressions where literal translation could obscure force

* “Ven” literally means friend, but in context it is a searching pastoral address. It should remain “Friend,” though the surrounding tone must ensure proper seriousness.
* “bøier sin Ryg for dig” is literally “bends his back for you.” In English, “bows his back for you” would sound odd. “Bends down beneath you” or “bows himself to bear you” may explain too much. The best context-sensitive rendering is “he bends his back for you and says...” so long as the image remains concrete.
* “tog dig varligt paa sit Dyr” literally “took you carefully onto his beast.” In English, “set you gently upon his beast” preserves the Good Samaritan coloring without awkwardness.
* “du studsede et Øieblik” literally “you started for a moment.” Depending on rhythm, “You paused for a moment” or “You were arrested for a moment” may communicate better.
* “holde Dom” should generally remain “hold judgment” or “execute judgment” depending on syntax. “Judge” can be too flat in places.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Thesis opening: The Father has given the Son all judgment and also all life. The same Christ who judges is the one who possesses and offers life.
* Christological paradox: The one once deeply humiliated on the accursed tree is now crowned with all authority. His suffering and exaltation are placed side by side.
* Central antithesis: In one hand he holds life, in the other death; with one hand restoration, with the other condemnation; today salvation is offered, tomorrow judgment is held.
* Doctrinal grounding: In Christ the fullness of the Godhead is bodily revealed; therefore honoring the Son is necessary, not optional.
* Cosmic confirmation: Heaven itself honors the slain Lamb. This universal worship reinforces the command to honor the Son.
* Personal turn: The sermon shifts sharply from doctrine to direct address. “Friend, have you listened to his voice?” The issue becomes existential and individual.
* Sequence of gracious visitations: The preacher rehearses multiple scenes in which Christ sought the sinner — in desert desolation, in moral ruin like the wounded man among robbers, in anguish under sin, at Golgotha, in the word of pardon.
* Refrain of examination: After each gracious approach comes the question, “Friend, what did you answer?” This structures the appeal and intensifies conscience.
* Honest reckoning: The preacher asks for plain speech about the sinner’s real response to Christ’s repeated offers of life and grace.
* Eschatological pivot: Evening approaches; night and judgment are near. The tone darkens from invitation to warning.
* Judicial hearing: Since the sinner has rejected grace, can he endure judgment? The Son of Man will summon all, and none will be judged unheard.
* Prosecutorial speech of Christ: The sermon imagines Christ’s address at judgment, reciting his saving initiatives and the sinner’s repeated refusal.
* Climactic indictment: Even the sights of Pilate’s Christ and the crucified Savior did not produce repentance; the sinner returned to the world and effectively cried for crucifixion.
* Appearance of the Judge: The same rejected Son of Man returns in majesty and with angels, now to enter into judgment.
* Removal of excuse: The sinner has nothing to answer. The fault lies not in Christ’s refusal but in the sinner’s rejection.
* Sentence: The Matthew 25 condemnation is pronounced.
* Final conclusion: The condemned must acknowledge Christ’s lordship and confess that grace and life were offered, but judgment and eternal death were chosen instead.

This progression must remain intact: doctrine -> exalted Christ -> personal appeal -> remembered offers of grace -> warning -> judicial speech -> sentence -> final irreversible recognition.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* Warnings must remain severe. The passage does not merely caution; it presses the sinner under the certainty of consequence.
* Christ’s tenderness must remain deeply personal and concrete, but never sentimental.
* The rejection of grace must appear culpable, repeated, and tragic.
* The judgment scene must remain judicial, solemn, and terrible.
* The translation should keep the direct second-person address wherever present.
* Emotional force should come from the accumulation of facts, invitations, refusals, and consequences — not from inflated English diction.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* skulle in the biblical citation and throughout often indicates certainty, not mere futurity. In many places “shall” best preserves this force.
* maa / maatte must be rendered with necessity: “must,” not “will have to” where that would dilute directness.
* kunde in rhetorical judicial questions should often remain “could,” preserving the implied impossibility of excuse.
* vilde ikke must remain “would not,” not merely “did not,” because the will is morally engaged.
* har faaet Magt / har givet should preserve bestowed authority, not generic ability.
* lader os tale oprigtigt should remain “let us speak honestly,” preserving hortatory force.

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

* Dommen / Dom -> judgment
* Liv / det evige Liv -> life / eternal life
* Døden -> death
* Fordømmelse -> condemnation
* Frelse -> salvation
* Naade -> grace
* Salighed -> salvation or blessedness, depending on whether the context stresses the state or the saving gift; here mostly salvation
* Synd / Synder -> sin / sins
* Misgjerning -> iniquity
* Faderen -> the Father
* Sønnen / Guds Søn -> the Son / the Son of God
* Menneskesønnen / Menneskens Søn -> the Son of Man
* Faderhuset -> the Father’s house
* Fred -> peace
* Pine -> torment
* fortærende Ild -> consuming fire
* Herre -> Lord

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* Christ holding life in one hand and death in the other must remain literal as image, not abstracted into authority over life and death.
* The thorn-crowned, bleeding head and the tree of the curse must remain visible.
* Desert desolation, empty cups of worldly pleasure, the sinner borne on torn shoulders, the wounded traveler, sea-waves of sin, hell’s abyss, evening and night, heart’s door, wings sheltering a chick, consuming fire, worm undying — all these images must remain intact.
* The translation should resist explaining metaphors inside the sentence. Their force lies in their immediacy.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* The passage alternates between majesty and tenderness, invitation and accusation, hope and terror.
* Christ first appears as bearer, healer, seeker, comforter, forgiver, giver of life.
* He then appears as judge, accuser, and condemner.
* The translation must not flatten these movements into a uniform devotional tone.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Preserve long sentence flow where argument accumulates.
* Retain vocative openings such as “Friend.”
* Preserve parallel clauses and repeated openings.
* Keep semicolon turns where they help the sermonic pressure.
* Let quotations sound speakable and weighty.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

The Judgment and the Life.

Twenty-Sixth Sunday after Trinity.

John 5:22–29. For neither does the Father judge anyone, but has given all judgment to the Son, that all should honor the Son, just as they honor the Father. Whoever does not honor the Son does not honor the Father who has sent him. Truly, truly, I say to you: Whoever hears my word and believes him who has sent me has eternal life and does not come into judgment, but has passed from death to life. Truly, truly, I say to you: The hour comes, and now is, when the dead shall hear the voice of the Son of God, and those who hear it shall live. For just as the Father has life in himself, so he has also given to the Son to have life in himself; and he has given him authority also to execute judgment, because he is the Son of Man. Do not marvel at this! For the hour comes when all who are in the graves shall hear his voice, and they shall come forth: those who have done good, to the resurrection of life; but those who have done evil, to the resurrection of judgment.

The Father has given the Son all judgment; but he has also given him all life; he has given him to have life in himself. For the Father loves the Son and has given all things into his hand.

Thus equipped he comes before us, he who was so deeply abased and hung upon the Tree of the Curse. With such power has the Father crowned him whose head bled from the thorns of mockery.

In one hand he holds life, in the other he holds death. With one hand he reaches out raising up, with the other condemnation. Today he offers salvation; tomorrow he holds judgment.

In him the fullness of the Godhead is revealed bodily. In him, a man, the holy God comes before men with his omnipotence and grace and says: Honor the Son! No one can honor the Father without honoring the Son. Around him the hosts of heaven unite and cry with a loud voice: “The Lamb who was slain is worthy to receive power and riches and wisdom and strength and praise and honor and blessing.”

The Son of Man has been given all power in heaven and on earth, and he is truly not far from any one of us.

Friend, have you listened to his voice, or have you had the courage to shut your ear in contempt when he spoke to you and said: “Here is life, there is judgment”?

O, consider yourself once more; here he comes forth before you again, behold him! Behold the Lamb of God, who bears the sin of the world! Far away in the desert’s waste and loneliness he followed after you; your soul was drained and languishing; bitterly had the world deceived you with the empty cup of its pleasures. Here Jesus stands before you, God’s own Son, himself full of wounds and pains; he bends his back for you and says: “My child, come, and I will carry you upon my torn shoulders back to the Father’s house.” Friend, what did you answer?

He sought you as a mother seeks her child and found you seduced, enticed, and fallen among robbers; ah, how many an earthly friend, to whom you had looked up with expectation and trust, did not pass you by without feeling in your misery! He came, whom you had forgotten, and who yet had suffered even hell’s torments for you; he came, he bent his thorn-crowned head over you and spoke lovingly to you; he healed your wounds and set you gently upon his beast in order to bring you to safe shelter and care. Friend, what did you answer?

You writhed in anguish and pain; like sea-waves your sins went over your head, and hell’s abyss gaped toward you. “Is there no rescue for my poor soul?” you cried in despair. Then the Savior stood there again and said: “Come to me, you who are burdened and labor, and I will give rest to your soul.” Did you come? Did he get to draw you to Golgotha? Did he get to lift your eye to the cross, as Israel’s dying people lifted their gaze to the bronze serpent? Did he get to speak to your heart as to Jerusalem and say that your warfare is ended, your iniquity is atoned for, I have paid with my blood? Did he get to whisper to you this blessed word: “Take courage, your sins are forgiven you”? Did he get to press you to his breast in faith and give you eternal life according to his own words: “Whoever believes in the Son has eternal life”?

Friend, let us speak honestly with one another; how does it stand? Thousands of times the Son of God has stretched out his hands full of life and grace and offered you all of it without money and without price. Ah, how have you received him, how have you answered him who has power to save completely all those who come to the Father through him?

It draws toward evening, and the night soon comes. Gloom and darkness and judgment are in the Lord’s right hand; his wrath is a consuming fire. He is not mocked, and whatever a man sows, that shall he also reap.

If you have had the courage or the levity to turn back his gracious offer of life and salvation, have you also strength to hear his judgment? For there comes a day when all shall hear the voice of the Son of God; the dead shall come forth before his throne, and none shall be judged unheard.

“Is there anything that I could have done to save you, and which I neglected?” says the Son of Man. “In the desert, among robbers, in your soul’s anguish I visited you, and you paid no heed to me. With my Spirit and my word I knocked again and again at your heart’s door: ‘Let me in, that I may save you!’ but you would not. I became a man like you, despised for your sake and full of pains, and you would not acknowledge me.

I pointed to your sin and to hell’s open abyss, I showed you the way of life and death, I implored and begged you to return as a father calls a straying child home, I wept and said: ‘O, if only you knew, even today, what serves for your peace; O, if only I might hide you under my wings as a chick,’ — but you would not.

Yet I did not grow weary. I led you to Pilate; a man stands before you, beaten and bleeding: ‘Behold the Man!’ You turned away in indifference.”

At the foot of the cross you were allowed to see the Savior crowned with thorns and nailed fast, in unspeakable suffering and hellish anguish, all of it in order to save you. You paused for a moment; but then you turned again to the world’s levity and pleasure and cried: “Crucify! his blood be upon me and upon my children!”

Now he comes, the same Son of Man whom you have despised and rejected; power and majesty the Lord has clothed him with, and with the angels of heaven he comes in the clouds to enter into judgment with you and hold judgment. He who has life because he willed to become man, and has stretched it out to you, he has also received authority to hold judgment because he is the Son of Man. Have you anything to answer? Have you anything with which to excuse yourself?

Do you see him now? His countenance is changed; the bright, kindly look with which he drew you a thousand times has become a consuming fire. You tremble, and in unspeakable anguish you cast your eyes down. It was not he who rejected you; but you rejected him. Now you must receive the judgment.

“I was hungry and thirsty and naked and sick and a stranger and in prison; you neither gave me food nor drink, you neither clothed nor tended me, you neither came to me nor received me; depart from me, you cursed, into the eternal fire prepared for the devil and his angels!”

O, how dreadful, when you must learn that there is no peace for the ungodly, that he is tormented by a worm that never dies and a fire that is never quenched, and that in the midst of that tormenting exclusion from God’s blessed fellowship you shall yet have to acknowledge that Jesus Christ, the Son of Man, whom you despised and rejected, is Lord, to the glory of God the Father; he offered you grace and salvation, you chose judgment and torment; he offered you eternal life, and you chose an eternal death!

—

Twenty-Seventh Sunday after Trinity.

Sermon for the Sixth Sunday after Epiphany.

STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* Hvo: archaic/danish-norwegian form of "who/he who." It carries biblical and sermonic resonance. A flat modern rendering like "whoever" may be possible in some places, but where the cadence is scriptural, "he who" better preserves force.
* Øren at høre med: biblical idiom, not merely physiological hearing. It implies spiritual receptivity and responsibility before the Word.
* Trefoldighed: literally Trinity; in church-calendar usage, "the Twenty-Second Sunday after Trinity."
* Skjæppen / Bænken / Lysestagen: biblical household terms from the Gospel text. These must be rendered in a scripturally recognizable way: "bushel," "bed" or "bench," "lampstand." Choice affects biblical echo.
* lønligt: hidden, secret, concealed. It has moral and revelatory force, not merely privacy.
* Agter paa: take heed to, pay careful attention to. Stronger than "notice."
* Maal / maaler / maales: measure. This is a biblical judicial metaphor and must remain stable.
* Fratages: be taken away from. Strong passive deprivation with divine-judicial force.
* ubemærkelige: seemingly unremarkable, unnoticed. Must not become trivialized.
* beror paa: depends upon, rests upon. Strong causative dependence.
* gjensidig Forbindelse: mutual connection; reciprocal relation between Word and hearing.
* tveægget Sværd: two-edged sword. Strong biblical phrase with Hebrews-like resonance.
* Hjærternes hemmelige Raad: the secret counsels of hearts. "Counsels" preserves deliberative moral inwardness better than merely "thoughts."
* Dødens Lugt til Død / Livsens Lugt til Liv: biblical idiom from 2 Corinthians 2. "Savor" or "fragrance" must preserve scriptural solemnity.
* lister at høre: has a taste for hearing, likes to hear, takes pleasure in hearing. The element of inclination and chosen delight is important.
* aandløst: here likely "spiritless" or "empty," but in context with "letfærdigt Snak" it suggests shallow, soulless talk.
* letfærdigt: frivolous, wanton, morally light. Needs moral color, not mere casualness.
* Tidsfordriv: pastime, diversion, amusement. Often with critical moral nuance.
* gemytligt Lag: convivial company, cozy gathering. Must preserve the deceptively pleasant social atmosphere.
* ond Omtale / Bagvaskelse: evil speaking / slander, backbiting. Distinctions matter.
* lifligste Drik: sweetest or most delightful drink. Metaphor of eager inward absorption.
* raadden Snak: "corrupt communication" or foul talk, with strong biblical echo from Ephesians 4:29.
* gjækkelig: mocking, scoffing, jeering, buffoonish. Tone matters.
* letfærdig Skjæmt: frivolous or indecent jesting; likely echoing apostolic moral warnings.
* usedelige: unchaste, immoral, indecent.
* Eder: oaths, curses, swearing.
* Bespottelser: blasphemies, revilings. Context suggests speech against holy things.
* gjennemsyres: permeated through and through, saturated.
* Edder og Gift: venom and poison. Must keep the vivid infernal image.
* høie Emner: elevated subjects, high matters. Social and intellectual prestige is in view.
* anseede Selskab: esteemed society, respected circle. Social aspiration is part of the critique.
* Sjælstilstand: state or condition of the soul.
* Lyst i: delight in, takes pleasure in.
* Høremåder: modes of hearing, ways of listening. Not just auditory forms, but morally chosen habits of reception.
* uskaadt: unharmed, unscathed. Moral self-deception is implied.
* bringer Fred: brings peace. This is not mere calm but spiritual peace.
* trænges: is needed, is required.
* Stad paa et Bjerg: city on a hill/mountain. Biblical image must remain vivid.
* Himmelkraft: heavenly power. Strong theological intensity.
* lønligt: concealed; repeated term of hiddenness, tied to revelation.
* Vederstyggelighed: abomination, hideousness. Very strong moral-theological word.
* Domsbasun: trumpet of judgment.
* Tiden, som aldrig slukkes, og Ormen som aldrig dør: the transmitted text likely reflects "fire that is never quenched" and "worm that does not die," but the printed text reads "Tiden." The translation should preserve the probable sense only if editorial emendation is justified; otherwise note the apparent textual irregularity. Because fidelity is primary, the safer course is to preserve the line as transmitted in analysis and render according to intended biblical sense in translation only where context compels it.
* Himmerøst: heavenly voice.
* betegnes: is designated, is signified by.
* Salig: blessed. More than happy.
* gjør derefter: does accordingly, acts upon it, does thereafter/thereupon. In biblical idiom: hears and does.
* vender ikke tomt tilbage: does not return empty/void. Clear echo of Isaiah 55.
* sat mange i Israel til Fald og til Opreisning: appointed/set for the fall and rising of many. Strong biblical judicial wording.
* modsiges: contradicted, spoken against.
* Livsens dyrebare Skat: the precious treasure of life, or life-giving precious treasure. Context suggests the precious treasure pertaining to life.
* idelig: continually, unceasingly.
* bøjede ikke sit Øre til: did not incline their ear.
* forhærdede sin Nakke: hardened their neck, stiffened their neck. Scriptural idiom should remain intact.
* bortkaste: cast away.
* Varsomhed i Omgangen med Guds Ord: carefulness/caution in one’s dealing with the Word of God.
* holde sig nærmere til at høre end en Daare til at gjøre Offer: echo of Ecclesiastes 5:1; must preserve scriptural shape.
* Aandens Velsignelse: the Spirit’s blessing.
* Suk: sigh, longing cry.
* skuddet, funget og overflødig Maade: clearly traditional wording corresponding to "good measure, pressed down, shaken together, and running over," though the orthography/transmission is irregular. Must preserve the biblical abundance formula.
* føres frem: be led forth.
* Stifs eller Skins Skyld: probably for appearance’s sake, out of stiffness/formality or mere show. The phrase is idiomatic and slightly uncertain in transmission; likely means outward propriety, convention, or hypocrisy. It must not be flattened; its performative religiosity is essential.
* feigere og feigere: more and more cowardly/faint-hearted/spiritless. Context suggests inward enervation rather than fear alone.
* uforvarende: unawares, unexpectedly.
* annammer: receives, accepts.
* en Lette: a relief.
* kommer du ihu: do you remember/call to mind.
* Timeglasset snart er udrundet: the hourglass has nearly run out. Strong mortality image.
* forgjæves: in vain.
* ifærd med at blive forhærdet: in the process of becoming hardened.

Syntactic Hazards

* The sermon moves through long accumulative sentences with semicolon logic. Breaking them into short modern English sentences would flatten the exhortational force.
* Biblical quotations are woven into authorial prose without modern signaling. The translation must preserve this blending.
* Repetition is used structurally and rhetorically: "hvad han hører og hvorledes han hører," "daglig, tidlig og idelig," "tommere og tommere, feigere og feigere." These should remain visibly patterned.
* The prose frequently pivots from doctrinal assertion to direct address without transition. This sermonic suddenness must stay.
* Rhetorical catalogues of corrupt hearing and salutary hearing are cumulative moral exposures, not merely descriptive lists.
* Conditional clauses often carry warning and promise in balanced form; the structure must remain intact.
* Scriptural maxims are interlaced with exposition. Their position in the sentence matters for cadence and authority.
* The final section uses direct interrogation of conscience through a long chain of questions. This must not be broken into neutral prose.

Theological Hazards

* "Ordet" is not generic speech but the Word of God, often closely identified with Christ and the proclaimed Gospel.
* The relation between hearing and salvation is doctrinally central: faith comes by hearing, but hearing is effectual through God’s Word.
* Hearing is morally and spiritually active, not passive reception.
* The text maintains that all hearing exerts formative power on the soul; this must not be psychologized or reduced to mere influence.
* Gospel hearing works either life and blessedness or judgment and death. The polarity must remain absolute.
* The Word reveals hidden sin, judges, consoles, and saves. All these functions must remain united.
* Divine judgment in response to careless hearing is real and imminent, not rhetorical exaggeration.
* Spiritual hardening is progressive and judicial.
* Hunger and thirst for the Word are not metaphors for generic interest but marks of grace-conditioned receptivity.
* Blessing from the Word is tied to the Spirit’s agency.

Voice Hazards

* The voice is solemn, earnest, hortatory, and pastoral, yet uncompromising. Any smoothing into "readable modern devotional English" would distort it.
* The tone alternates between exposure of corruption, doctrinal instruction, invitation, warning, and direct appeal to conscience.
* Social satire appears in the critique of gossip, empty talk, cultured elitism, and formal religiosity. It must remain sharp but controlled.
* The author speaks with biblical saturation rather than ornamental quotation. The translation must sound inhabited by Scripture without becoming faux-archaic.
* Direct address terms like "Ven" and "Broder og Søster" carry pastoral immediacy and should not be sentimentalized.
* Final imperative force depends on brevity after long buildup: "He who has ears to hear, let him hear!"

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* "Hvo som har Øren at høre med, han høre"
  Meaning: A scriptural call to spiritual receptivity and obedient hearing, not mere possession of ears.
  Scriptural context: Repeated saying of Christ in the Gospels.
  Theological implication: Responsibility before revelation; those who truly hear must respond.
  Natural English equivalent: "He who has ears to hear, let him hear."

* "Kommer Lyset ind, for at det skal sættes under Skjæppen eller under Bænken?"
  Meaning: Light is not given to be concealed.
  Scriptural context: Mark 4:21 and parallels.
  Theological implication: The revealing purpose of divine truth and revelation.
  Natural English equivalent: "Does the lamp come in to be put under the bushel or under the bed?"
  Note: "bushel" preserves biblical recognizability better than "measure-basket."

* "Med hvad Maal I maaler, skal eder maales"
  Meaning: The measure one uses will be used in return.
  Scriptural context: Gospel teaching on judgment and reception.
  Theological implication: Reciprocity before God; seriousness of how one handles the Word.
  Natural English equivalent: "With the measure you use, it will be measured to you."

* "Troen kommer af at høre"
  Meaning: Faith comes through hearing.
  Scriptural context: Romans 10:17.
  Theological implication: Saving faith ordinarily arises through reception of God’s proclaimed Word.
  Natural English equivalent: standard Pauline rendering, though the full phrasing here should preserve the author’s wording.

* "Sig mig, hvem du omgaaes, og jeg skal sige dig, hvem du er"
  Meaning: One’s company reveals one’s character.
  Cultural context: proverbial saying.
  Theological implication: Moral formation through fellowship and chosen hearing.
  Adapted authorial use: He recasts it as hearing-preference revealing spiritual identity.

* "indsuger saadan Tale som den lifligste Drik"
  Meaning: They imbibe slander with delight.
  Theological implication: Sin is inwardly enjoyed, not merely tolerated.
  Literal translation is possible, but "drink it in as the sweetest draught" better preserves metaphorical appetite.

* "lader raadden Snak udgaa af deres Mund"
  Meaning: Let corrupt/foul speech proceed out of their mouth.
  Scriptural context: Ephesians 4:29 echo.
  Theological implication: Speech as moral pollution.
  KJV-adjacent equivalent exists, but the translation should use modern pronouns while preserving biblical shape.

* "deres Sjæl gjennemsyres af Edder og Gift fra Helvede"
  Meaning: Their soul is saturated with venom and poison from hell.
  Theological implication: Speech corrupts inward spiritual condition.
  Literal translation should be preserved; explanatory paraphrase would weaken it.

* "under Bænk eller Skjeppe" / "som Lys paa en Stage og Stad paa et Bjerg"
  Meaning: The truth is public, shining, manifest.
  Scriptural context: lampstand and city on a hill imagery.
  Theological implication: The Gospel is not esoteric but openly radiant.
  Natural English equivalent: "as a light on a lampstand and a city on a hill."

* "lyder snart som Domsbasun ... snart som en stille, kjærlige Himmerøst"
  Meaning: The Word sounds now as judgment trumpet, now as tender heavenly voice.
  Theological implication: Law and Gospel contrast within the one Word.
  Literal retention is essential.

* "Dine Ord blev fundne af mig, og jeg aad dem"
  Meaning: I received the Word inwardly and hungrily.
  Scriptural context: Jeremiah 15:16.
  Theological implication: Deep appropriation of divine revelation.

* "Ordet vender ikke tomt tilbage"
  Meaning: God’s Word is effectual.
  Scriptural context: Isaiah 55:11.
  Theological implication: The preached Word always accomplishes something, either salvation or judgment here as expounded.

* "bygger sit Hus paa en Klippe" / "sætter sit Hus paa Sand"
  Meaning: Obedient hearing stands; disobedient hearing collapses.
  Scriptural context: Matthew 7.
  Theological implication: Hearing without doing is ruinous.

* "bøjede ikke sit Øre til" / "forhærdede sin Nakke"
  Meaning: Refused obedient receptivity; became stiff-necked.
  Scriptural context: prophetic rebuke formula.
  Theological implication: persistent refusal intensifies guilt and invites judgment.

* "holde sig nærmere til at høre end en Daare til at gjøre Offer"
  Meaning: Be readier to hear than a fool to offer sacrifice.
  Scriptural context: Ecclesiastes 5:1.
  Theological implication: outward religiosity without receptive obedience is folly.
  Literal translation should preserve the contrast.

* "hungrende og tørstigt Hjerte"
  Meaning: genuine spiritual need and receptivity.
  Scriptural context: biblical hunger/thirst imagery for righteousness and grace.
  Theological implication: right hearing requires need awakened before God.

* "en god skuddet, funget og overflødig Maade i dit Skjød"
  Meaning: an abundant, overflowing measure bestowed into one’s lap.
  Scriptural context: Luke 6:38. The printed wording is textually irregular, but the allusion is clear.
  Natural English equivalent: "a good measure, pressed down, shaken together, and running over, into your lap."

* "Ordene springer tilbage fra din Sjæl som en Kugle kastet mod en Væg"
  Meaning: the Word fails to penetrate and rebounds from a hardened soul.
  Theological implication: progressive hardening against grace.
  The simile should remain vivid and severe.

* "Ordene dig sødere end Honning og Honningkage"
  Meaning: the Word sweeter than honey and the honeycomb.
  Scriptural context: Psalms.
  Theological implication: true believers delight in the Word.

* "øser du Vand med Glæde af Livsens Kilder"
  Meaning: do you draw water with joy from the wells of life/salvation.
  Scriptural context: likely Isaiah 12:3, though the phrase here says "life" rather than explicitly "salvation."
  Theological implication: glad appropriation of divine grace.

* "Timeglasset snart er udrundet"
  Meaning: your allotted time is nearly over.
  Cultural and rhetorical force: mortality and urgency.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Opening scriptural frame
  The sermon opens with Christ’s saying and the Gospel text for the day, establishing hearing, revelation, and measure as the governing themes.

* Thesis
  Hearing and speaking appear to be ordinary natural functions, yet eternal life and eternal death depend upon the Word and right hearing of it.

* Doctrinal grounding
  The author grounds the thesis in Scripture: the Word reveals hearts, kills or gives life, and faith comes by hearing through God’s Word.

* Core proposition
  A person’s salvation truly depends on what he hears and how he hears.

* Moral-anthropological development
  Because humans cannot avoid hearing, the decisive issue becomes preference: what one chooses and delights to hear reveals what one is.

* Catalogue of corrupt hearing
  The author moves through examples:

  * frivolous and filthy talk
  * gossip, slander, and backbiting
  * foul speech, obscene jesting, immoral songs, oaths, blasphemies
  * even elevated cultural discourse when pursued as prestige and delight apart from the Word
    This broadens the indictment from crude corruption to refined worldliness.

* Diagnostic conclusion
  All these forms of hearing shape the soul decisively, yet none gives peace. Anyone who thinks he can indulge them without harm deceives himself.

* Positive contrast
  Only one thing truly benefits the soul to hear: the Word of God, publicly shining, penetrating, revealing sin, sounding both judgment and consolation, and identified with Christ Himself.

* Scriptural amplification
  Prophetic and biblical calls to hear the Word are assembled to intensify authority and desirability.

* Direct pastoral appeal
  The sermon turns from description to address: "Friend, you have ears; do you hear God’s Word?"

* Qualification of blessedness
  Blessedness does not lie in bare hearing but in hearing rightly, which means hearing and doing.

* Effectuality of the Word
  The Word never returns void; where the Gospel is heard, it works either life and blessedness or judgment and death.

* Responsibility theme
  Therefore a great and holy responsibility rests upon everyone entrusted with hearing the Word.

* Historical warning from Israel
  Israel’s refusal to hear, despite God’s persistent sending of prophets, becomes the paradigm of judgment.

* Present application
  The preacher asks whether the same Word and judgment do not still sound to the hearers now.

* Exposition of the text’s warning
  "With the measure you use..." becomes a call to carefulness in dealing with the Word and an exhortation to hearing over empty religious performance.

* Two contrasted responses

  * If one comes to the Word prayerfully, hungrily, thirstily, seeking the Spirit’s blessing, God gives abundance and joy.
  * If one comes indifferently, proudly, formally, and without hunger, the heart grows emptier and harder until judgment comes.

* Final examination of conscience
  The preacher presses the hearer personally: after so many hearings of sin and grace, death and life, how do you hear?

* Climactic warning and conclusion
  Remember God has spoken often; time is running out; neglected words incur responsibility; hardening is already underway; judgment follows. The sermon closes by repeating Christ’s summons: "He who has ears to hear, let him hear!"

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* The translation must keep the sermon’s severity where it exposes frivolous talk, slander, corrupt speech, spiritual pride, and formal hearing.
* Warnings of hardening and judgment must remain direct and unsmoothed.
* Consolation must remain tender but not sentimental; the contrast between trumpet of judgment and heavenly voice is central.
* The direct second-person appeals must keep moral pressure on the reader/hearer.
* The movement from doctrine to conscience-examination must remain sharp and unavoidable.
* Terms of address such as "Friend" and "Brother and Sister" should stay plain and earnest.
* The conclusion must strike with brief finality after the long exhortation.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* "skal" and related forms must generally remain "shall," especially in scriptural or judicial contexts, not weakened to "will" where obligation, decree, or certainty is central.
* "mon ikke" must preserve rhetorical force: "is it not rather...?" or "does it not...?"
* "kan" in moral impossibility contexts should remain "cannot," not "may not be able to."
* "maa" where present in implied force should remain necessity, not mere advisability.
* Warnings like "vil blive" may be rendered "shall become" or "will become" according to context, but not softened.
* Imperatives such as "Agter paa" and "Ser til" must remain forceful: "Take heed," "See therefore," not merely "notice" or "be aware."

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

* Ordet -> the Word
* Guds Ord -> the Word of God
* Hørelse / høre -> hearing / hear
* Troen -> faith
* Liv / Livsens -> life / life’s or of life depending on idiom
* Død -> death
* Salighed -> blessedness
* Dom -> judgment
* Sjæl -> soul
* Synd -> sin
* Naade -> grace
* Frelseren -> the Savior
* Aanden -> the Spirit
* Hjertet -> the heart
* forhærdet / forhærdelse -> hardened / hardening
* Lyst -> delight
* Fred -> peace

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* Keep speech and hearing as more than communication; they are spiritually determinative acts.
* Preserve sword, savor, lamp, lampstand, city on a hill, trumpet of judgment, heavenly voice, poison, drink, honey, water, rock, sand, treasure, hourglass.
* Do not explain metaphors inside the translation.
* Keep the violent simile of words rebounding from the soul like a ball or bullet against a wall.
* Retain hiddenness/revelation imagery as an organic thread from the Gospel text into the sermonic application.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* The text moves from doctrinal calm to severe social-moral indictment.
* It then rises into luminous praise of the Word.
* It shifts into tender scriptural consolation.
* It returns to grave warning about judgment and hardening.
* It culminates in intimate pastoral interrogation.
* These tonal shifts must remain distinct; the translation must not level them into one homogeneous pious tone.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Many sentences are shaped for oral delivery and should remain long where the cumulative force matters.
* Use semicolons and commas to preserve spoken momentum.
* Maintain recurring phrasing and paired contrasts to support proclamation.
* Preserve the cadence of direct address and rhetorical questions.
* End with the scriptural imperative as a pronounced concluding stroke.

He who has ears to hear, let him hear.

Twenty-Second Sunday after Trinity.

Mark 4:21-25. And He said to them: Does the light come in that it should be set under the bushel or under the bench? Is it not rather that it should be set upon the lampstand? For nothing is hidden except that it should be made manifest, neither has anything come to pass in order to remain concealed, but that it should come to light. If anyone has ears to hear, let him hear! And He said to them: Take heed what you hear! With the measure you measure, it shall be measured to you, and to you who hear there shall be given yet more. For he who has, to him shall be given; and he who has not, from him shall be taken even that which he has.

To hear as well as to speak are two common and therefore seemingly unremarkable functions of two natural human organs of sense. Nevertheless, both are of such importance that not only life and death in the purely natural sense often depend upon them, but eternal life and eternal death also rest upon the Word and the right hearing of it. For there is a mutual relation between the two.

Just as it is said of the Word that it is a two-edged sword, which reveals the secret counsels of the hearts, and that for some it becomes a savor of death unto death, for others a savor of life unto life, so it is said of hearing: "Faith comes by hearing, but hearing takes place through the Word of God" (Rom. 10:17). Therefore the Lord says in our text: "Take heed what you hear!" And in another place: "See therefore how you hear!" (Luke 8:18).

A person’s salvation truly depends upon what he hears and how he hears.

It is impossible for one who has hearing to avoid hearing what is spoken and uttered all around him. But it is not long before he begins to make a difference and to prefer hearing one thing rather than another.

The ancients said: "Tell me with whom you keep company, and I shall tell you who you are." One could just as well recast it thus: "Tell me what you delight to hear, and I shall tell you who you are."

Some like to go where they hear spiritless and frivolous talk, such as a dog would be ashamed of if it were written down and read aloud again. They do so for pastime, they say, and readily add some sort of amusement or game besides.

Others prefer a convivial circle where they may hear gossip and evil report and slander of their fellow men, and they drink in such speech as the sweetest draught.

Or they gather with those who let foul speech proceed out of their mouth together with mocking and frivolous jesting, immoral songs, oaths, and blasphemies, and their soul is permeated through and through with venom and poison from hell.

Some go much higher. They set value only upon hearing elevated subjects discussed: science, music, art, poetry, and all such things as require so costly an education that only a few can hope to gain entrance into that esteemed society. In the same proportion they find their pleasure and delight in it.

... all these different kinds of hearing have their own peculiar and decisive influence upon the condition of a person’s soul; but none of them brings peace. And however much anyone may comfort himself with being a Christian, though he has his delight in lending his ear to one or more of the above-mentioned ways of hearing, and thinks he can do so unharmed, he deceives himself. No one can withdraw himself from the influence of that to which he listens with delight and gladness; daily experience convinces us of it.

There is only one thing that truly does the soul good to hear; it is that which requires neither art nor learning to grasp, which need not hide itself "under bench or bushel" either; it is that which, as a light upon a lampstand and a city upon a hill, shines both for all the world and for each individual person with such heavenly power that it penetrates into the soul’s innermost depth and reveals all that is concealed: the most secret counsels and thoughts, the whole nature and abomination of sin; it is that which sounds now like a trumpet of judgment concerning the fire that is never quenched and the worm that never dies, now like a still, loving voice from heaven: "Though a mother forget her nursing child, I have not forgotten you!"—it is that by which the thorn-crowned and ascended Savior Himself is designated: the Word, the Word of God.

"Hear the Word of the Lord, you princes of Sodom," cries the Prophet; "hear My voice, and I will be your God, and you shall be My people." "Your words were found by me," says Jeremiah, "and I ate them, and Your words were to me joy and the gladness of my heart."

"Earth, earth, earth, hear the Word of the Lord!"
Friend, you have ears; do you hear the Word of God? Oh, then you are blessed, if you do thereafter, that is to say, if you hear rightly.
For, says the Lord, "See therefore how you hear."
The Word does not return empty. There, where the Gospel is heard, it works either life and blessedness or judgment and death; it is—as it is said of the Son of God—set for the fall and for the rising again of many in Israel, and for a sign that is spoken against. He who hears the Word and does thereafter builds his house upon a rock; but he who does not do thereafter sets his house upon sand, and its fall shall be great.

A great and holy responsibility therefore rests upon every one who hears the Word of God, and to whom this precious treasure of life is thus entrusted.

To Israel the Lord says: "From the day your fathers came out of the land of Egypt unto this day, I sent to you all My servants the prophets, daily, early, and continually. But they did not hear Me, and did not incline their ear; they hardened their neck, they acted worse than their fathers. Therefore I will cast you out from before My face, just as I cast out your brethren, all the seed of Ephraim."

Does not that same Word and that same judgment still sound to us?
"With the measure you measure, it shall be measured to you again, and to you who hear there shall be given yet more—but from him who has not there shall be taken even that which he has."

In this word of our text the Lord exhorts to carefulness in one’s dealing with the Word of God, and to keep nearer to hearing than a fool to offering sacrifice.

If with prayer and crying out for the Spirit’s blessing, with a heart hungering and thirsting, you go to hear or read the Word of God, so that you think you could gladly swallow the whole of it in order to satisfy your soul, then the Lord shall give you according to your sighing and your desire, "a good measure, pressed down, shaken together, and running over, into your lap." "In joy you shall go out and in peace you shall be led forth; the mountains and the hills shall break forth into shouts of joy before your face, and all the trees of the field shall clap their hands" (Isa. 55:12).

But if you go to the Word with indifference, with the proud thought that you yourself can master it, without hunger and thirst, for mere appearance’s sake or outward form, then your heart shall for each time become emptier and emptier, more spiritless and more spiritless, so that the words spring back from your soul like a ball cast against a wall, until you become hard as stone, and judgment comes upon you unawares as upon Israel. Then there is taken from you even that which you had.

And now, Friend, how often have you been in God’s house, how often have you listened to His Word about sin and grace, about death and life, how lovingly has the Lord not called to you, how gently and earnestly has not the Father drawn you again and again, and how do you hear? Is it with delight and longing, are the words to you sweeter than honey and the honeycomb, do you draw water with joy out of the springs of life, or are you sluggish and dull, is it only reluctantly or by habit that you receive the Savior’s message, is it a relief to you when the preaching of the Word is over, do you hasten with double eagerness to your newspaper, your neighbor’s talk, or to your books and speculations?

Brother and Sister! Do you remember that the Lord has spoken to you daily, early, and continually, that the hourglass is soon run out, that you bear responsibility for all the words God has spoken to you in vain—that you are in the process of becoming hardened, and that after that comes the judgment?

He who has ears to hear, let him hear!

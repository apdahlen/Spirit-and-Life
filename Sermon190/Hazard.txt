STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* "gaa hen" / "gaa bort" / "gaa tilbage" / "vandrede ikke mere med ham": these are not merely spatial verbs; they carry spiritual, relational, and discipleship force. "Go away" or "leave" must be handled carefully so as not to flatten the existential and confessional dimension.
* "afgjørende Vendepunkt": more than a neutral "turning point"; it signals a decisive crisis with spiritual consequences.
* "uberegnelig Velsignelse": literally "incalculable blessing" or "immeasurable blessing"; it must preserve abundance and gravity, not become casual emphasis.
* "vantro" / "frafaldne": doctrinally marked terms. "Unbelieving" and "apostates" or "backsliders" are possible, but choice affects tone and theological precision.
* "Lindring for Saar" and "Bod for haarde Hjerter": "relief for wounds" and "remedy/penance/cure for hard hearts." "Bod" here is not merely punitive penance; it carries the sense of healing correction, repentance-remedy.
* "bedragerlige Hjerte": biblically resonant; likely echoing the deceitfulness of the heart. "Deceitful heart" is necessary.
* "Livsbrød": not merely "bread of life" as a generic phrase, but directly tied to John 6 and Christological doctrine.
* "bespist": not just "fed" in a mundane sense; in context it recalls the miraculous feeding.
* "skjærer ligesom Hul paa deres Hjerter": vivid and violent figurative language. It must not be weakened into "hurt their feelings"; it is something like "cuts, as it were, a hole in their hearts."
* "opbrusende Kjærlighed": not simply "enthusiastic love" but swelling, surging affection.
* "taalte de ikke": often stronger than "did not like"; it implies spiritual inability to endure exposure.
* "smagte dem ikke": idiomatic disapproval, but with older sermonic concreteness.
* "knurre": biblically freighted. "Murmur" is preferable to "complain" because of scriptural resonance.
* "haard Tale": in Johannine context, "hard saying" is the natural equivalent.
* "uforstandige": more than merely "foolish" in a casual sense; spiritually undiscerning.
* "fortryllet": in Galatians echo, "bewitched" preserves scriptural force.
* "male for Øine": vivid proclamation imagery; "set forth before your eyes" may better preserve Pauline cadence than a literal "painted."
* "Taarer græder han endnu idag": must retain the startling present force; not sentimentalized.
* "Satans Trældom": "bondage" or "slavery"; theological force must remain strong.
* "Dolkestik i min Samvittighed": powerful conscience-language; "dagger-thrusts in my conscience" preserves intensity.
* "forhærde mig": biblical hardening, not just becoming stubborn.
* "tegnet mig paa dine Hænder": likely an echo of Isaiah 49:16; must preserve the engraving/image inscription resonance.
* "Frimodig": not merely "cheerful" or "bold"; "be of good cheer" or "be of good courage" depending context. Here it echoes absolution language.
* "dit Legeme": contextually sacramental. It must not be generalized into mere fellowship or presence.
* "Siloas stille rindende Kilde": scriptural-poetic image. Must remain image-rich.
* "omspænder mig med Liflighed": difficult devotional idiom. It suggests surrounding/encircling with sweetness/delight.
* "Himmel-saft": vivid metaphor that must not be flattened. "Heavenly nectar" or "heavenly juice"; "nectar" is likely the least awkward while preserving elevation.
* "Dødens Reb": biblical image of cords/bands of death.
* "forvarer og bevarer": near-doublet intensifying divine protection; both verbs should be reflected if possible.
* "fuldkommes": "is made perfect" retains biblical resonance.
* "Synderinden": traditional "the sinful woman," not merely "a woman sinner."
* "tog ... til Naade igjen": classic language of restoration to grace; must preserve doctrinal dimension.
* "lunkent": biblical "lukewarm," not just cool or indifferent.
* "som Vandflod gaaet over min Sjæl": flood imagery must remain vivid.
* "forbarmeude Broder": clearly "compassionate" or "merciful Brother"; likely a typographic/orthographic irregularity for "forbarmende." Must be normalized in sense, not in tone.
* "Livsens Ord": older genitive form; should become "the word of life" or in context "this your word of life."
* "stakkels skjælvende Barn": must retain tenderness and humility without sentimentality.
* "frelst" / "Frelse": doctrinally precise "saved" / "salvation," not generalized well-being.
* "Kjep og Stav": Psalmic "rod and staff," not modernized.
* "freidig Bekjendelse": not just "confession" but bold, assured confession.
* "hovmodig": scriptural "high-minded" or "proud." In Romans echo, "do not be high-minded, but fear" should be considered.

Syntactic Hazards

* Long periodic sentences accumulate spiritual experience, doctrinal reflection, and confession without modern paragraph-style segmentation. Breaking them too much would weaken sermonic movement.
* Repeated refrain structure: "Til hvem skulle vi gaa hen?" recurs as a liturgical and rhetorical anchor. It must remain stable across occurrences.
* Frequent semicolon logic joins parallel spiritual states rather than merely separating clauses. The translation should preserve semicolons where the argument accumulates.
* The text moves between narrative exposition, direct address to the hearer, prayer, confession, and exhortation. These shifts must remain clear without smoothing transitions.
* Rhetorical questions such as "Men, taaler du det?" and "Er det mig?" must remain piercing and immediate.
* Biblical quotations are woven into prose and sometimes adapted. Translation must preserve integration while allowing recognizability.
* Parallel clauses such as "baade vantro og frafaldne, baade svage og stærke kristne" require balance and rhythm.
* The first-person devotional sections rely on cumulative syntax: burden, conviction, revelation, absolution, joy. This arc should unfold without compression.
* The final movement from confidence to warning depends on a sharp pivot: "Men ak! En af de tolv forraadte ham." The contrast must not be softened.

Theological Hazards

* John 6 language concerning the Bread of Life, Christ’s flesh and blood, and resurrection on the last day must remain doctrinally precise.
* The distinction between seeking Christ for earthly benefit and receiving him by faith is central and must not be moralized into mere sincerity language.
* Exposure of the heart’s motives is treated as a crisis before God, not only psychological self-discovery.
* "Arbeide for den Mad... som varer til et evigt Liv" concerns the orientation of the soul; translation must not suggest works-righteousness where the sermon’s later emphasis is faith in Christ.
* "troede paa ham" / "tro og erkjende": faith and recognition/confession must remain distinct yet joined.
* The text treats Christ as both sacrificial Savior and present nourisher in Word and sacramental body.
* Sin, conscience, hardening, despair, grace, forgiveness, restoration, preservation, and final judgment all appear in a strongly Lutheran devotional frame.
* "til Naade" / "forladte" / "fri og frelst": these terms must preserve forensic and saving dimensions.
* "midt i min Skrøbelighed fuldkommes din Kraft": an explicit Pauline theology of divine strength in human weakness.
* "der er ikke Frelse i nogen anden end dig": exclusivity of salvation in Christ must remain emphatic.
* The warning about Judas functions as a warning against presumption within the visible circle of disciples.

Voice Hazards

* The prose is solemn, searching, exhortational, and pastorally severe. Modern smoothing would erase its conscience-addressing power.
* The author frequently confronts the reader directly: "Men, har du... ?" / "Men, taaler du det?" / "Men, er det gaaet dig ligedan?" These must remain pointed.
* Emotional intensity is controlled, not theatrical. The translator must preserve heat without melodrama.
* The devotional first-person singular is not private lyricism alone; it is exemplary confession meant to draw the hearer into self-recognition.
* The repeated cry "Til hvem skulle vi gaa hen?" carries helpless dependence, not merely preference.
* The final warning must strike with sudden dread after extended consolation.
* Older sermonic dignity depends on sentence rhythm, scriptural saturation, and moral inevitability, not archaic pronouns. The English must preserve gravity without faux-Elizabethan diction.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* "Til hvem skulle vi gaa hen?"
  Meaning: Peter’s confession of absolute dependence on Christ. Not merely "Where else can we go?" but "To whom should we go?" The personal object matters.
  Scriptural context: John 6:68.
  Theological implication: salvation is not an abstract path but bound to the person of Christ.
  English equivalent: "Lord, to whom shall we go?" is the natural biblical equivalent.

* "med Magt vilde gjøre ham til Konge"
  Meaning: they wanted to seize him and make him king by force.
  Scriptural context: John 6:15.
  Theological implication: false messianic enthusiasm seeks earthly benefit and political kingship.
  Natural equivalent: "would take him by force to make him king."

* "skjærer ligesom Hul paa deres Hjerter"
  Meaning: Christ’s word pierces and opens the concealed center of the heart.
  Scriptural context: akin to Hebrews 4:12 in effect, though not quotation.
  Theological implication: divine exposure of inward motives.
  Literal translation risks awkwardness, but the violence of the image should be retained: "cuts, as it were, a hole in their hearts."

* "I søger mig, fordi I aad af Brødene og blev mættet"
  Meaning: they seek Jesus for material benefit, not for who he is.
  Scriptural context: John 6:26.
  KJV-style resonance without archaic pronouns is useful: "you seek me... because you ate of the loaves and were filled."

* "arbeide for den Mad"
  Meaning: labor not in a merely economic sense, but direct the soul’s striving.
  Scriptural context: John 6:27.
  Theological implication: distinction between perishing food and food abiding unto eternal life.
  Literal translation works if cadence is preserved: "labor not for the food which perishes..."

* "knurre"
  Meaning: to murmur in resistance and unbelief.
  Scriptural context: Israel murmuring in the wilderness; also John 6.
  Theological implication: the crowd reenacts wilderness unbelief.
  "Murmur" is better than "grumble" because of biblical resonance.

* "Dette er en haard Tale; hvem kan høre den?"
  Meaning: this teaching is intolerable to the natural heart.
  Scriptural context: John 6:60.
  Natural equivalent: "This is a hard saying; who can hear it?"

* "O, I uforstandige Galater, hvo har fortryllet eder"
  Meaning: rebuke for irrational spiritual defection.
  Scriptural context: Galatians 3:1.
  Natural equivalent: "O foolish Galatians, who has bewitched you...?"
  This is one place where established biblical English strongly helps.

* "malet for Øine"
  Meaning: vividly set forth before the eyes.
  Scriptural context: Galatians 3:1.
  Literal "painted" is possible but slightly odd; "publicly portrayed" is modern, but too modern in tone. "Set forth before your eyes" best fits the sermon’s register.

* "som en Høne samler Kyllingerne under Vingerne"
  Meaning: Christ’s maternal-gathering image for Jerusalem.
  Scriptural context: Matthew 23:37 / Luke 13:34.
  Theological implication: rejected divine mercy.
  Literal rendering should be preserved.

* "tegnet mig paa dine Hænder"
  Meaning: inscribed, engraved upon the hands.
  Scriptural context: Isaiah 49:16.
  Theological implication: covenant remembrance and electing care.
  "You had engraved me upon your hands" is stronger and more biblical than merely "marked."

* "førte mig til Korsets Fod"
  Meaning: brought into faith’s posture before Christ crucified.
  Theological implication: conversion through the cross.
  English equivalent: "led me to the foot of the cross."

* "Vær frimodig, dine Synder er dig forladte"
  Meaning: authoritative absolution and consolation.
  Scriptural context: synoptic healing/forgiveness scenes, especially Matthew 9:2.
  "Be of good cheer" is traditional but somewhat old-fashioned; "Take courage" is possible, but the older cadential quality of "Be of good cheer" may serve, provided archaic pronouns are avoided.

* "øser jeg af Siloas stille rindende Kilde"
  Meaning: draws refreshment from the gentle saving stream of God.
  Scriptural context: Isaiah 8:6 allusion, filtered devotionally.
  Theological implication: quiet divine grace in contrast to worldly force.
  Literal translation should preserve image: "I draw from Siloam’s softly flowing spring."

* "grønne Græsgange"
  Meaning: pastoral nourishment and rest.
  Scriptural context: Psalm 23.
  Natural equivalent: "green pastures."

* "Mit Bæger flyder over"
  Meaning: abundant divine goodness.
  Scriptural context: Psalm 23:5.
  Natural equivalent: "My cup runs over" or "overflows." "Overflows" is slightly less archaic, but "runs over" has more biblical echo.

* "Dødens Reb"
  Meaning: cords or bands of death encircling the soul.
  Scriptural context: Psalms 18:4, 116:3.
  "The cords of death" is the natural equivalent.

* "forvarer og bevarer"
  Meaning: guards beforehand / watches over and preserves. The pair intensifies divine keeping.
  A doubled rendering like "you guard and preserve me" retains force.

* "Synderinden"
  Meaning: the notorious sinful woman of the Gospel narrative.
  Scriptural context: Luke 7.
  Natural equivalent: "the sinful woman."

* "tog den faldne Peter til Naade igjen"
  Meaning: restored Peter to grace after his fall.
  Theological implication: restoration after grave sin.
  Natural equivalent: "received the fallen Peter into grace again" or "took... back into grace."

* "som Vandflod gaaet over min Sjæl"
  Meaning: sins overwhelming the soul like floodwaters.
  Scriptural context: flood/overwhelming waters imagery common in Psalms.
  Literal image should remain.

* "ledte mig stakkels skjælvende Barn igjen til din Fader"
  Meaning: Christ leads the trembling believer back to the Father.
  Theological implication: mediation and adoption.
  The phrase must keep tenderness and dependence.

* "min Kjep og Stav"
  Meaning: Christ as support, correction, guidance, and comfort.
  Scriptural context: Psalm 23:4.
  Natural equivalent: "my rod and staff."

* "freidig Bekjendelse"
  Meaning: courageous, assured confession.
  Theological implication: persevering faith speaks openly.
  Possible equivalent: "this bold confession."

* "vær ikke hovmodig, men frygt"
  Meaning: do not presume; fear lest you fall.
  Scriptural context: Romans 11:20 in older phrasing.
  Natural equivalent: "do not be high-minded, but fear" or "do not be proud, but fear." The former carries more scriptural color.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Title and text:
  The sermon is anchored in John 6:66–71, especially Peter’s confession and the warning of Judas.

* Opening thesis:
  John 6 presents a great and decisive turning point in the relation of both the people and the wider circle of disciples to Jesus.

* Universal applicability:
  All kinds of hearers may read this chapter with blessing: unbelievers, apostates, weak Christians, strong Christians. It both exposes the deceitful heart and offers the life-giving bread of the Gospel.

* Narrative exposition of false enthusiasm:
  After the feeding of the five thousand, the crowd is full of zeal and wants to make Jesus king. Their enthusiasm appears deep and enduring.

* First diagnostic interruption:
  The preacher breaks in with a direct question to the hearer: have you ever been in a similar state of mind?

* Exposure of motive:
  Jesus reveals that they seek him because they ate and were filled. Their admiration is unmasked as self-interested.

* Second diagnostic interruption:
  "But can you endure that?" The hearer is placed under the same exposing word.

* Controversy and decline:
  The crowd turns to religious dispute, asks for signs, appeals to Moses and manna, and murmurs when Christ identifies himself as the true bread from heaven.

* Crisis of decision:
  Their former enthusiasm collapses. Faced with Christ’s teaching on his flesh and blood, many disciples turn back and walk no more with him.

* Third diagnostic interruption:
  "Has it gone the same way with you?" The fall of the disciples becomes a mirror for the hearer.

* Pauline and dominical warning:
  Galatians 3 and Christ’s tears over Jerusalem deepen the warning. Spiritual beginnings can end in flesh; rejected grace brings grief.

* Immediate application:
  Christ still weeps over the one who no longer walks with him.

* Christ’s question to the Twelve:
  "Will you also leave?" This marks the pivot from warning to confession.

* Extended confessional answer:
  A series of first-person devotional paragraphs unfolds Peter’s confession existentially:

  * Christ delivered me from sin and Satan’s bondage.
  * Christ nourishes me by Word and body.
  * Christ preserves me amid weakness and danger.
  * Christ restores me after renewed sin and near-despair.
  * Christ sustains me in death and judgment.

* Refrain-driven structure:
  Each section returns to "To whom should we go?" and then expands one reason why Christ alone remains.

* Culminating confession:
  "You have the words of eternal life, and I have believed and known that you are the Christ, the Son of the living God."

* Final reversal and warning:
  Suddenly the sermon returns to Judas: one of the Twelve betrayed him. This prevents complacency even within confessed discipleship.

* Final self-examination:
  The disciples’ horrified question at the Last Supper becomes ours: "Is it I?"

* Concluding imperative:
  Romans 11:20 supplies the final force: "Do not be high-minded, but fear."

This structure must remain intact in translation: exposition, probing address, crisis, confessional refuge, then sobering warning.

* John 6:15: the crowd wanting to make Jesus king by force.

* John 6:31–35, 41–51, 53–58: Moses, manna, the true bread from heaven, eating Christ’s flesh and drinking his blood, resurrection on the last day.
* John 6:60: "This is a hard saying; who can hear it?"
* Galatians 3:1, 3: "O foolish Galatians, who has bewitched you...? Having begun in the Spirit, are you now made perfect by the flesh?"
* Matthew 23:37 / Luke 13:34: Jerusalem gathered as a hen gathers chicks under her wings.
* Luke 19:42: "If you had known... the things that make for your peace... but now they are hidden from your eyes."
* Isaiah 49:16: "I have engraved you on the palms of my hands."

* Psalms 18:4 / 116:3: cords of death.

* Romans 11:20: "Be not high-minded, but fear."

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* The preacher speaks with searching directness. Questions to the hearer must remain abrupt and personal.
* Warnings concerning apostasy, hardening, and despair must not be softened into mild spiritual reflection.
* The devotional sections should retain warmth, but the warmth arises from deliverance from real peril: bondage, accusation, weakness, death, judgment.
* Christ’s tears over Jerusalem and over the present hearer must remain severe mercy, not sentimentality.
* The confessional refrains must carry helpless necessity: Christ is not one option among many, but the only refuge.
* The final Judas warning must remain startling after consolation; the point is that outward nearness does not exclude betrayal.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* "maatte de faa se og høre igjen" should remain strong: "they had to see and hear him again," not merely "they wanted to."
* "vilde gjøre ham til Konge" and "vilde gaa" retain intent and volition; these should not be weakened.
* "det taalte de ikke" and "hvem kan høre den?" must preserve inability as well as refusal.
* "maatte æde Jesu Kjød og drikke hans Blod" must keep necessity: "had to eat... and drink..."
* "skulde opreise" / "skulde forraade" should preserve future certainty: "would raise up" / "was to betray."
* "skal have et evigt Liv" and "jeg skal opreise ham" require full future-promissory force.
* "skal kunne skille mig" in Romans 8 echo likewise demands strong certainty.
* "maa ogsaa vi ofte spørge" should remain necessity: "we too must often ask."
* "vær ikke hovmodig, men frygt" must remain imperative, not advisory suggestion.

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

Preferred renderings for repeated doctrinal terms in this passage:

* "Livsens Brød" / "Livsbrød" -> "the Bread of Life" / "the Gospel’s blessed bread of life" depending context.
* "Ord" in Christological-soteriological context -> "word" or "words" with care to preserve Johannine resonance.
* "det evige Livs Ord" -> "the words of eternal life"
* "tro" -> "believe" / "faith" depending syntax
* "erkjende" -> "know" or "acknowledge/recognize"; in the Johannine confession formula, "have believed and known" is best.
* "Synd" / "Synder" -> "sin" / "sins"
* "Naade" -> "grace"
* "forladte" -> "forgiven"
* "frelst" / "Frelse" -> "saved" / "salvation"
* "Helligaand" -> "Holy Spirit"
* "Skrøbelighed" / "Skrøbeligheder" -> "weakness" / "weaknesses" or "frailty/frailties" depending rhythm. For Pauline echo, "weakness" is preferable.
* "Trængsler" -> "distresses" or "tribulations"; "distresses" may read more naturally while keeping weight.
* "Angester" -> "anguishes" or "anxieties"; "anguishes" preserves gravity.
* "forbarmede dig" / "forbarmende" -> "had compassion" / "compassionate"
* "tog ... til Naade igjen" -> "took ... into grace again" / "received ... into grace again"
* "Bekjendelse" -> "confession"
* "bevares" -> "be preserved"

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* The heart being cut open must remain a wound-image, not a vague emotional discomfort.
* Bread, manna, flesh, blood, water, spring, green pastures, overflowing cup, cords of death, rod and staff, floodwaters, and cross imagery must remain concrete.
* "Himmel-saft" should preserve its lush spiritual sensuousness; it should not become an abstract "comfort."
* "engraved upon your hands" preserves covenant imagery better than a weakened paraphrase.
* "my sins were nailed to the cross" must remain image-rich and forensic.
* The hen and chicks image must stay literal.
* "poor trembling child" must remain an image of filial dependence before God.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* The opening balances blessing and exposure.
* The crowd scene begins with enthusiasm, then shifts into exposure and offense.
* The sermon moves from stern diagnosis to deep consolation.
* The first-person sections alternate between remembered terror and remembered mercy.
* The approach to death and judgment is dark, but it yields to confidence in Christ.
* The ending reverses again into fear and self-examination. This final sobering turn must be abrupt.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Refrains should remain identical or nearly identical across repetitions for oral force.
* Semicolons should be retained where they preserve breath-grouping and sermonic accumulation.
* Direct addresses to Christ in prayer-like passages should keep a measured cadence.
* Biblical citations embedded in prose should sound proclaimable, not merely informational.
* The English should avoid clipped modern sentence rhythms in the confessional sections.
* Repetitions such as "Er det mig? er det mig?" should remain exact for liturgical dread.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

To whom should we go?

Tenth Sunday after Trinity.

John 6:66–71. From that time many of his disciples went back and walked no more with him. Then Jesus said to the twelve: Will you also go away? Then Simon Peter answered him: Lord, to whom should we go? You have the words of eternal life, and we have believed and known that you are the Christ, the Son of the living God. Jesus answered them: Have not I chosen you twelve? And one of you is a devil. But he spoke of Judas Iscariot, Simon’s son; for he it was that was to betray him, and he was one of the twelve.

In the sixth chapter of John there meets us a great and decisive turning-point, both in the relation of the people and in that of the larger circle of disciples to Jesus. With immeasurable blessing all may read this chapter again and again, both unbelievers and apostates, both weak and strong Christians, and find in it both relief for wounds and remedy for hard hearts. Every one may there both see his own deceitful heart portrayed and find the Gospel’s blessed bread of life.

Jesus had fed five thousand. Then they all became so enthusiastic for him that they even "would take him by force to make him king." Him they had to see and hear again, and be fed by him. They were willing to go however far it might be in order to find him again.

But have you ever been in a like state of mind?

Their enthusiasm became, if possible, even greater when they found him again in Capernaum and learned that he must have gone there across the sea. They pressed in upon him and surely thought that him they would love and cling to all their life. This was a king for Israel!

But here they also receive the first rebuff. The Lord, as it were, cuts a hole into their hearts and lays open before the eyes of all what there was in their admiration and surging love for him: "You seek me because you ate of the loaves and were filled!"
But that the heart’s inmost thoughts and counsels should thus be revealed, they could not endure.

But can you endure it?

Jesus’ admonition not to labor for the food which perishes, but for that which endures unto eternal life, did not please them, and they began—as so many have done and will do—they began to discuss religion, Moses and manna, and to demand signs. When Jesus explained to them that Moses gave no bread from heaven, but that the Son of Man himself was the true manna, the Bread of Life, who had come down from heaven and on the last day would raise up those who, instead of every other sign, believed in him, then they got a welcome occasion to murmur against Jesus and themselves reveal the true state of their hearts. "Is he not Joseph’s son, whose father and mother we know? and he has come down from heaven?" they said.

Now it had come to a decision, a sorrowful decision, with them; they forgot both the feeding and the walking on the sea and their enthusiasm. And when Jesus further unfolded to them that Moses and his manna were only something to die from, and that they had to eat Jesus’ flesh and drink his blood in order to come into the same relation to the living Father as he himself and receive eternal life, then they thought they had full right to regard him as a Samaritan and a madman. Many, even of his disciples, murmured and said: "This is a hard saying; who can hear it?"

And thus it came to pass that many of his disciples went back and walked no more with Jesus.

But has it gone the same way with you?

Upon how many does Paul’s heart-rending cry fit, when he says: "O foolish Galatians, who has bewitched you, that you should not obey the truth, you before whose eyes Jesus Christ was set forth as crucified among you! Are you so foolish? Having begun in the Spirit, will you now be made complete in the flesh?"
But do you not see Jesus’ tears, as he weeps over Jerusalem and says: "Jerusalem! how often would I not have gathered you as a hen gathers her chicks under her wings, and you would not. O if only you had known, even on this your day, what served for your peace! But now it is hidden from your eyes."

These tears he still weeps today over you who no longer walk about with him.

Will you also leave him, you twelve?

"To whom should we go?"

Heavily I sighed under the burden of sin and Satan’s bondage, and your word concerning sin, O Lord, was like dagger-thrusts in my conscience, so that my flesh rebelled, and I was near to hardening myself against your truth.

But you did not let me go, you Shepherd of Israel; you had engraved me upon your hands, and when you led me to the foot of the cross, and in faith I was permitted to look up to my Savior’s thorn-crowned head and knew that the burden of sin was taken away by reason of his blood, and I was free and saved—O how glad and blessed was that hour! You said: "Be of good cheer; your sins are forgiven you!" and I, I would never be parted from my Savior, in life and in death.

"To whom then shall I go?"

You have the words of eternal life. By your good Holy Spirit you are always near me in your word and in your body; when I hunger, I go there and am satisfied; when I thirst, I draw from Siloam’s softly flowing spring and am refreshed. You lead me into green pastures, you surround me with sweetness, you have taught me a new song, a blessed song of joy in the fellowship of your Father and of the angels. My cup runs over with the sweet heavenly nectar of your word.

"To whom should we go?"

The cords of death encompass me; a thousand dangers surround me; the enemies lie in wait for my soul; and I am helpless as a child and frail as a brittle branch. But you are the Son of the living God, you are strong, you guard and preserve me, and in the midst of my weakness your power is made perfect; I am well content in weaknesses, in insults, in distresses, in anguishes for your sake, Lord Jesus; for when I am weak, then I am strong.

"To whom then should we go?"

You are he who proclaims the Gospel to the poor, who had compassion on the sinful woman, whom men cast away, and who took the fallen Peter into grace again. O Lord, how often I too have sinned, how frequently my heart has become lukewarm and cold toward you, who gave your life for me, and how my sins have gone over my soul like a flood. Ah, I had to hide myself like Adam from the righteous God and was near to despair. But you came toward me as a compassionate Brother in this your word of life, you took me by the hand and led me again to Golgotha, where my sins were nailed to the cross, and led me, poor trembling child, again to your Father, and he did not cast me out; for your sake he took me into grace again, and in blessed joy I was once more permitted to learn to believe and know that you are the Son of the living God, and that there is salvation in no other than you.

"To whom then should we go?"

In the bitter hour of death, in the dark passage of the valley of death, in the anguish of the last judgment—then you are my rod and staff; who is he that condemns? Christ is he who died, yea rather, is risen, and sits at the Father’s right hand; what shall I fear? "For this is the will of him who sent Jesus, that every one who sees the Son and believes in him should have eternal life, and I will raise him up on the last day," says the Lord. So that I am certain that there is nothing that shall be able to separate me from the love of God in Jesus Christ, if only I am preserved in this bold confession:

"You have the words of eternal life, and I have believed and known that you are the Christ, the Son of the living God."

"To whom then should we go?"

But ah! One of the twelve betrayed him.

Is it I? can it be I? the disciples asked themselves in terror at the last supper.

Is it I? is it I? we too must often ask in fear.

Therefore, "do not be high-minded, but fear!"
